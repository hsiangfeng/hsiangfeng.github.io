Phone Smashed and Photos Gone? Please Turn On Google Photos Backup (iPhone Setup Guide)

#iPhone #iOS #Google Photos #Backup #Cloud Backup #Mobile

Intro

Phone smashed, dropped in water, suddenly won’t boot — when it happens, the photos are usually the hardest thing to recover. This post walks through why I keep recommending Google Photos backup, and shows the full iPhone setup flow: choosing backup quality, how to use the 15GB free quota, whether to enable mobile data backup, and the one big iOS gotcha — swiping the app away in the App Switcher kills the upload mid-transfer.

For desktop backup I’ve already written about macOS Time Machine taking forever? Here’s a one-trick fix (article in Mandarin). This post is the mobile-side companion — get both done and your data is in a much safer place.

How this came up

The reason I’m writing this is that one of my students messaged me a few days ago saying his phone took a fall and now wouldn’t power on at all. The repair shop quoted him about a week of turnaround.

When he told me, his first reaction wasn’t even sadness about the phone — it was “what about all my photos?” Because the phone wouldn’t boot, there was no way to pull the data off, and all he could do was wait for the repair and hope.

I asked him “did you have Google Photos backup turned on?” He thought for a second and said “I don’t think so?” — and at that point I was already mentally going “oh no.”

The thing about phone repairs is they’re really luck-dependent. If you’re lucky it’s just a screen swap and your data is intact. If you’re unlucky — motherboard fried, storage chip damaged — the data is just gone, and even paying a recovery service doesn’t always bring it back.

So I figured I’d write this as a public reminder to anyone reading: seriously, please go turn on Google Photos backup.

Why you absolutely have to back up your photos

Photos are kind of unique. They’re not like apps, contacts, or messages on your phone — apps you can re-download, contacts you can re-add, but photos are that specific moment, and once it’s gone, it’s gone.

To make this concrete: trips with friends and family, childhood photos, your cat sleeping in a weird position, the last meal your grandma cooked — none of those have a price tag. They’re not replaceable.

And phones, as portable devices, can fail in so many ways:

  • Drop on the floor — cracked screen, dead motherboard
  • Drop in water
  • Charging port goes flaky and won’t charge
  • One day it just black-screens and won’t boot
  • Battery swells
  • Stolen or grabbed

Speaking from personal experience — I hit this exact problem years ago. I was on Android at the time, and one day the phone just wouldn’t boot. Some photos of my pet from back then are just gone forever, and I felt awful about it for a long time afterward.

After that I started turning backup on like a good citizen. To this day, no matter how many times I’ve switched phones, all my photos live in Google Photos. New phone, sign in with the same account, photos come right back. Honestly, super convenient.

Why I recommend Google Photos specifically

There are plenty of cloud backup options out there — iCloud, OneDrive, Dropbox, all of them work. So why do I specifically recommend Google Photos? Basically these reasons:

Aspect Why it matters
Cross-platform Works on both iPhone and Android, so switching phones isn’t a problem
Auto-tagging Google automatically organizes photos by people, places, and events — finding stuff is super fast
Search Type “dog” or “bird” in the search bar and it’ll surface the relevant photos
Free quota Every Google account gets 15GB of free storage (shared with Gmail and Drive)
Integration Most people already have a Google account, so no new signup

Looking at the table, Google Photos really fits most general users. The cross-platform thing matters a lot — you don’t know whether your next phone will be iPhone or Android, and Google Photos lets you avoid getting locked into one ecosystem.

How to set up Google Photos backup

The setup is honestly simple. The flow is basically the same on iPhone and Android — only the UI looks slightly different.

Install the Google Photos app

For this walkthrough I’ll use the iPhone screens. Android steps are essentially identical, just minor UI differences.

If you’re on iPhone, first thing is grab “Google Photos” from the App Store.

Sign in with your Google account

Once Google Photos is installed and open, the first step is signing in with your Google account. If it’s your first time, it’ll walk you through the email/password flow.

Turn on backup

After signing in, you’ll usually see “Auto-back up photos and videos from this device” already pre-checked at the bottom.

If you don’t see it or it’s not on, no problem — once you’re in the main view, tap your profile icon in the top right.

A menu pops up showing whether backup is currently on. If it says “Backup is off”, you haven’t turned it on yet.

Tap “Turn on backup” and you’ll get a screen with backup options. There are two things worth tweaking:

  1. Backup quality: I’d go with “Storage saver”. Regular accounts only get 15GB, and “Original quality” eats through that fast. Unless you really care about preserving every detail, “Storage saver” is plenty.
  2. Backup over: turn on “Use cellular data when no Wi-Fi”. That way photos back up immediately when you’re out and about, instead of waiting until you get home — and it’s also insurance against the “phone got dropped before backup finished” scenario.

That’s it. Your phone will start auto-backing up photos to Google Photos. From now on, no matter if you switch phones or your phone breaks, sign into the same Google account on the new device and your photos come right back.

Some details worth flagging

The setup itself is simple, but there are a few gotchas I want to call out so you don’t get caught off guard later.

Storage quota

Like I mentioned, a Google account comes with 15GB of free space, shared across Gmail, Google Drive, and Google Photos.

If your photo collection eventually fills it up, you can free space by deleting unwanted photos or clearing oversized Gmail attachments.

Keep the app alive in the background

This one’s specifically for iPhone users. iOS is way stricter about background processes than Android — when you swipe Google Photos up out of the App Switcher, the app fully stops, and the backup goes with it.

What does that mean in practice? Say you went out and shot a whole day’s worth of photos. You get home, open Google Photos, see it’s uploading, think “great, it’s running” and swipe it away to do something else. The backup actually got terminated right there — those photos haven’t really finished uploading.

So the right move when Google Photos is mid-upload: don’t swipe it out of the App Switcher. Press the Home button (or swipe back to the home screen) and let it run in the background. That way it can actually finish.

If you accidentally swipe it away, no need to panic — open Google Photos again and it’ll resume from where it stopped. The catch is, if your phone breaks while photos are still pending, those ones don’t come back.

Wrap-up

The main reason I’m writing this is so you don’t have to wait until your phone breaks to regret it. Photos really are the “better safe than sorry” kind of thing — five minutes of setup now can save you from a future of crying about photos you’ll never see again.

That student got his phone back fixed eventually, and luckily the photos were still there. But the very first thing he did was turn on Google Photos backup. Lesson learned.

If you haven’t set this up yet, please go do it after reading this. Photos don’t come back — but the setup only takes five minutes.
