What's RSS? Why Still Use It in 2026? Plus the Chrome Extension I Wrote — Raydar

Intro

This post walks through what RSS actually is, how it differs from Newsletter subscriptions, why it's still worth using in 2026, how it compares to algorithm-driven feeds, what Atom is — and then I talk about Raydar, a Chrome RSS reader extension I wrote. It covers RSS auto-detection, one-click subscribe, three reading modes, local-first storage, no account required, OPML import/export, JSON backup, keyboard shortcuts — plus how to install it and why I bothered writing my own.

The story

I've been wrapping up a Chrome extension I've been writing — an RSS reader called Raydar.

After finishing, I mentioned it offhand to a friend, and got back: "What's RSS?" That made me realize something. RSS is well known in dev circles, but for the general public the term hasn't been visible in years.

On top of that, after Google Reader shut down in 2013, plenty of articles wrote that "RSS is dead", so a lot of newer folks just skip past it, assuming it's from a previous era.

But honestly, in an era where algorithms are getting more aggressive, IG / X keep reranking your feed, and news sites are paywalling more aggressively, RSS is one of the few remaining ways to actually decide what you see. So this post is about what RSS is, why it's still worth using, and then a little about the small tool I wrote.

What is RSS, really?

In short: RSS is a website's publicly-published "latest content list", in XML format, containing each article's title, link, publish date, summary, and so on.

What does that look like? An example helps.

If you open my blog's homepage, you see a page laid out for human eyes — navbar, sidebar, ads, interactive bits.

If you instead open https://israynotarray.com/atom.xml, you see something like this:

xml
<?xml version="1.0" encoding="utf-8"?>
<feed xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2005/Atom">
  <title>Ray Not Array</title>
  <entry>
    <title>What's RSS?...</title>
    <link href="https://israynotarray.com/other/.../" />
    <published>2026-05-11T14:00:00.000Z</published>
    <summary>This post covers what RSS is...</summary>
  </entry>
  ...
</feed>

That's RSS. (Strictly this snippet is Atom, but ignore that for now — I'll cover the difference later.)

It's the machine-readable version of the site — no CSS, no JavaScript, no ads, just a clean content listing. RSS readers periodically fetch these XML files and aggregate the latest items from all your subscriptions into one place.

An everyday analogy

If I had to describe RSS in non-tech terms, it's like "newspaper subscriptions, but reversed".

Traditional newspaper subscriptions: the publisher delivers to your home. RSS: you go check the publisher's door for "anything new?", and grab it if there is. The difference is you don't need to give the publisher your address (email) — you pull, they don't know who you are.

That "you pull, they don't know who you are" property is what makes RSS worth revisiting in this era of pervasive tracking.

How does RSS differ from newsletter subscriptions?

Newsletters have been hot for the last few years — Substack, ConvertKit, etc. — so I get asked a lot whether RSS and newsletters are the same.

They're not. The differences are pretty big:

AspectRSSNewsletter
DeliveryYou pullThey push to your inbox
Required infoNoneYour email
PrivacyThey don't know who you areThey have your email and can track opens
UnsubscribeJust remove from your readerHave to find the unsubscribe link; sometimes ignored
ContentPure content, no trackingOften has tracking pixels, UTM params
AggregationOne reader sees everythingInbox is mixed with personal mail

You can see they take pretty different approaches. Newsletters' upside is the push model and the closeness between author and reader. The downsides are inbox overload and your email being held by many parties.

RSS goes the other way: you decide when to pull, what to pull, what to read with. It's the more "data ownership stays with me" approach.

Why use RSS in 2026?

This part is more personal opinion, but a few reasons made me actually go back to RSS in recent years.

Algorithms have eaten most of your attention

You've probably hit this: open IG, scroll for 20 minutes, realize you've been watching videos from people you don't follow. Open X to check someone's latest post, the home feed is all recommendations. Open YouTube to look at your subscriptions, the home page recommendations outweigh actual subscriptions.

This isn't an illusion — it's the design.

Platforms want you on the platform as long as possible, so they push "things we think you'll keep watching" to you, and what you actually subscribed to gets pushed way down the priority list.

RSS doesn't do this. Whoever you subscribed to is who you see, sorted by publish date, newest to oldest. Clean.

You stop missing the things you actually care about

Following on from the last point — because algorithms "filter" for you, the niche sources you actually care about get hidden.

For me personally, I subscribe to a few personal blogs that publish maybe once a month. Authors like that can't compete with algorithm-driven feeds on IG or Twitter, but in an RSS reader, when they publish, it shows up in my timeline. Doesn't get buried.

One place for everything

Tech blogs, news sites, open-source project releases, Hacker News, specific subreddits, GitHub trending, even Mastodon and Bluesky — all of it can flow through RSS. Once you get used to it, you stop needing five separate apps. One reader handles everything.

AI-era data sources matter more, not less

This one doesn't come up much.

Training AI models tends to lean heavily on "structured, semantically clear" content. Sites that are RSS-friendly tend to also be semantically clean. So as you start using AI to summarize and process information, RSS feeds become a particularly clean source to point AI tools at.

In other words, RSS isn't just for human reading anymore — if you're going to wire AI into your information workflow, RSS is the easiest entry point.

What is Atom, and how is it different from RSS?

Quick aside on Atom.

Atom and RSS are two different specs, but they do essentially the same job — publish a site's latest content as XML. The differences:

  • RSS (especially 2.0): older, looser field definitions, so different sites produce slightly different formats.
  • Atom: came later, tightened up the spec — for example, date fields are required to be RFC 3339 format.

End-user wise, it doesn't matter. Modern RSS readers handle both, and sites typically offer both. Your reader figures it out for you.

Note Besides RSS and Atom, there's also a much older format called RDF (RSS 1.0), but it's rare to encounter in the wild now.

What RSS readers are out there?

Enough about the spec — let's talk tools. There are quite a few options:

  • Feedly: probably the best-known cloud RSS reader. Free tier is limited.
  • Inoreader: another cloud reader, a bit more featured than Feedly.
  • NetNewsWire: native macOS / iOS app, open source, free.
  • Reeder: longstanding paid app on Apple platforms.
  • Miniflux: self-hostable open-source reader, minimalist.

These are all solid in their own ways, but after trying a bunch I noticed a few shared issues:

  1. Cloud services all require an account, and your subscription data lives on their servers.
  2. Either you pay for the full feature set or you have to self-host.
  3. Native desktop apps don't have a Chrome-extension experience for "I'm browsing, I want to subscribe to this site I just found".

So I figured I'd just write my own.

My Chrome extension: Raydar

Raydar

Raydar is a Chrome extension I've been writing the past few months. The goal: a local-first, no-account-required minimal RSS reader, stripping away the "register first, use later" friction that cloud readers all have.

It's up on the Chrome Web Store: Raydar — Chrome Web Store.

A few features I personally find interesting:

RSS auto-detection while you browse

This was on the design doc from day one. You've probably hit a case where you find a great article on some blog, want to subscribe, and can't find the RSS link anywhere.

Raydar auto-scans any page you're on for <link rel="alternate"> tags pointing at RSS / Atom, and if it finds one, pops up a subscribe prompt — one click and you're subscribed. No digging through source code or guessing URLs.

If a site doesn't expose that link tag, you can also manually paste the feed URL from the toolbar popup.

Three reading modes

Reading preferences vary, so Raydar gives you three:

  • Reader mode: extracts the article body into a clean layout with adjustable font size and theme.
  • Web mode: embeds the original page inside the reading pane — no need to leave the tab.
  • Raw RSS mode: shows the feed's raw XML, for developers debugging.

I personally use reader mode most, because a lot of news sites have such busy layouts that reader mode is the only way to actually focus on the content.

Fully local-first, no signup

Raydar doesn't need an account, email, or password. Install and use.

Your subscription list, article cache, and settings all live in your browser locally. Cross-device sync goes through Chrome's built-in chrome.storage.sync, which is encrypted by Google — the extension author can't access your subscription data at all.

No Google Analytics, no crash reporting, no AI API, no newsletter bridge. Everything stays local.

Keyboard shortcuts

For heavy readers, keyboard shortcuts are non-negotiable. So:

ShortcutAction
J / KNext / previous article
SStar / unstar
MToggle read / unread
OOpen original in new tab
Shift + RRefresh all subscriptions
?Show keyboard shortcut help

Design-wise this follows the Gmail / Google Reader J/K convention, so anyone who's used those tools will feel right at home.

Full-text search is fully local

Once your subscription count grows, search becomes essential.

Raydar's full-text search uses MiniSearch. The index is built in browser memory, and search never talks to any server. Chinese and Japanese have special tokenization handling, so searching something like "Vue 元件" works fine — it doesn't get stuck on English-only word boundaries.

Data portability, leave anytime

Something I care about a lot: leaving shouldn't have friction. So Raydar nailed import/export from day one:

  • OPML import/export: seamless migration to/from any other RSS reader.
  • Full JSON backup: subscription list, article cache, settings, favorites — all in one bundle.

Translation: the moment you don't like Raydar anymore, export OPML and drop it into Feedly / Inoreader / NetNewsWire. You're not locked in.

Other bits

A few smaller details:

  • Traditional Chinese / English / System language toggle.
  • Light / Dark / System theme.
  • Unread count shown on the toolbar icon badge — see at a glance how many new items there are.
  • Tag categorization, source pinning, keyword muting, etc.

The whole thing is meant to be "install it and forget it exists, but every day when you open it, information flows smoothly".

How to install

Raydar is on the Chrome Web Store. Just click the link below and hit "Add to Chrome":

Raydar — Chrome Web Store

After install, click the Raydar icon in the toolbar — a popup shows up. First-time setup has prompts to walk you through. Follow them and you're subscribing.

If you were already using another RSS reader, export an OPML from the old reader and drop it into Raydar's settings page — one shot import, all your sources move over.

Wrapping up

RSS isn't dead — it just stopped being a mainstream topic.

For anyone wanting to step away from algorithms, take active control of their information sources, or stop being held hostage by recommendation systems, RSS is the kind of thing where once you try it, you don't go back. Hopefully this post gives folks who didn't know about RSS a clearer picture, and if you're already using RSS, hopefully Raydar is worth a try.

If you do try it, bug reports or feature requests are welcome. See you in the next post.
