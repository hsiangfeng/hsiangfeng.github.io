No Preview Image When You Share to LINE or Discord? Force-Refresh Social OG Cache with ?v=1

#Discord #Facebook #Open Graph #og:image #LINE #cache busting #query string #Social Sharing

Intro

This post starts with the classic problem of sharing your blog post to a social platform and getting a stale preview image, then walks through Open Graph tags, how platforms cache og:image / og:title / og:description, why Facebook / X / LINE / Discord still show old content after you update your OG tags, and how to force a refresh by appending a cache-busting query string like ?v=1 ?v=2. Also covers how to use the Facebook Sharing Debugger, rough cache lifetimes by platform, what to do about Twitter / X’s Card Validator being retired in 2022, and whether ?v=1 impacts SEO or canonical URLs.

If you’ve been playing with Cloudflare Pages deployments or trying to lock down preview URLs recently, you can pair this with Cloudflare Pages Preview URLs Stay Live Forever? Lock Them Down with Cloudflare Access.

The story

A few days ago I wrote a post about using Cloudflare Access to lock down preview URLs. Once it was deployed, I excitedly shared the URL to LINE for a friend to read — and the share card came back without a cover image. Just the default placeholder octocat showing up as the preview thumbnail.

That was weird, because the article has a cover image right at the top, and og:image should’ve picked it up. I went back to inspect the blog HTML — og:image was pointing at the right cover image, curl-ing the image URL returned 200 OK, but LINE just wouldn’t pick it up.

Tried Discord, tried X — same default image everywhere. I started laughing at myself. The article was clearly fine, but every social platform was stuck on stale data.

After some digging, I realized this isn’t a blog problem at all — it’s the social platforms’ OG cache doing its thing.

Why do social platforms cache the preview?

Bottom line up front: social platforms cache each URL’s OG metadata, and a second post of the same URL won’t refetch from your site — it just pulls from cache.

OG stands for Open Graph, a metadata spec proposed by Facebook in 2010. It lets social platforms quickly grab a link’s title, description, and preview image. Sites just put a few meta tags in <head> and social platforms pick them up:

So why cache? Basically cost savings.

Think about it: you post a link, 100 friends see that post. The platform isn’t going to hit your site 100 times — that’d be 100 requests. So the first time anyone posts the URL, the platform fetches OG metadata once and stores it; everyone else who sees the post (or reposts the same URL later) reads from cache. Your site doesn’t get hit again.

That design is reasonable on its own, but when you’re a content creator who just updated your og:image and want the preview to refresh, cache becomes the obstacle.

Rough cache durations per platform:

Platform Cache expiry Official debugger?
Facebook ~7–30 days Yes (Sharing Debugger)
LinkedIn ~7 days Yes (Post Inspector)
X (formerly Twitter) ~24–48 hours Preview function discontinued
Discord ~30 minutes None
LINE Not officially published None
Slack / Telegram Not officially published None

Discord has the shortest cache. Facebook and LinkedIn are on the longer end. LINE / Slack / Telegram have never published official numbers, so when you run into stale previews there, you’re stuck using the workaround below.

Forcing a refresh with ?v=1

The simplest way to force a refresh: append a query string, like:

To the social cache, different URL strings are different entries. The original https://israynotarray.com/other/20260513/8264193057/ is cached, but adding ?v=1 makes it a “new URL” — cache miss — and the platform refetches your latest OG metadata.

The number after v= is arbitrary. Next time you want to force-refresh, use v=2, v=3 — any value you haven’t posted before. It doesn’t even have to be called v — ?refresh=1, ?nocache=true also work, as long as it’s a query string the cache hasn’t seen.

Your site won’t break from ?v=1

You might worry: will ?v=1 cause issues on my site?

Don’t worry — most frameworks and web servers ignore unknown query strings by default. Hexo, Next.js, Vite, Cloudflare Pages, Vercel, etc., all serve the original path content when they get ?v=1. Readers see exactly the same page, no difference at all.

Note
If your site already uses query strings for something (like pagination ?page=2 or search ?q=xxx), just avoid colliding with your existing query keys. ?v, ?refresh, ?nocache are safe bets — extremely unlikely to clash with anything your site already does.

What works per platform

Facebook: use the Sharing Debugger

Facebook is the only platform that still has a working official debugger: https://developers.facebook.com/tools/debug/. Paste your URL, click Debug to see what FB sees, and if it’s stale, click Scrape Again to force a refetch.

The Sharing Debugger also tells you if og:image has problems (too large, unsupported format, unfetchable, etc.) — really helpful for debugging.

Note
As of 2026, Facebook’s official docs still only support JPG, PNG, and GIF for og:image — WebP is not supported. If you use a WebP image as og:image, FB will break. Use PNG or JPG for og:image, and reserve WebP for the rest of your site for performance.

Also, og:image’s recommended size is 1200 x 630 pixels. Anything below 600 x 315 gets shown as a small thumbnail by Facebook, or sometimes not shown at all.

Sometimes the first Scrape Again doesn’t work — clicking two or three times in a row often does. There’s no rational explanation for this. FB just gets weird sometimes.

X, LinkedIn, LINE, Discord: just use ?v=1

X’s Card Validator pulled the preview function in 2022, and the remaining pages keep degrading. LinkedIn has Post Inspector, but it’s more limited. LINE, Discord, Slack, Telegram have never offered a debugger.

So for these platforms, the fastest and most universal solution is ?v=1 cache busting. Next time you’re pasting a URL into a LINE group, slap a ?v=1 on the end first — the platform refetches your site and grabs the fresh OG.

Note
Already-shared messages in the group won’t update — social caches bind to the message that captured them. Whatever the old message has, it’ll keep showing. To fix, delete the original message and repost with ?v=1.

Alternative: third-party OG preview tools

If you want to check what the preview will look like before posting, there are also third-party tools that fetch your page’s OG in real time:

  • opengraph.dev
  • share-preview.com
  • microlink.io

These pull your OG meta live, with no relationship to any platform’s cache, so they’re useful for checking og:image works before publishing. But — a third-party tool succeeding doesn’t guarantee FB / LINE will also succeed. Trust the official debugger (FB Sharing Debugger) as the source of truth.

Does this affect SEO?

No, assuming your site has canonical URLs set up properly.

<link rel="canonical"> is a tag in <head> that tells search engines “this is the canonical URL for this page“. When Google fetches https://your-site.com/post/?v=1 and finds:

It treats ?v=1 as the same page as the original. Search rank doesn’t get split, and it doesn’t get flagged as duplicate content.

On top of that, ?v=1 is something you only use occasionally to force a social cache refresh — you’re not actively promoting that variant URL. In practice, Google probably never even sees it. Zero real SEO impact.

Note
If you’re not sure whether your site has a canonical URL, open an article, hit F12 → Elements, and look for <link rel="canonical"> — check whether the href matches the URL you’d expect. Most modern blog frameworks (Hexo, Astro, Next.js, Vite SSG) add it automatically.

Bonus: always test before posting

One habit I picked up after getting bitten enough times: run the URL through FB Sharing Debugger before posting.

Workflow is straightforward:

  1. After publishing, open Facebook Sharing Debugger.
  2. Paste your article URL, click Debug.
  3. Check that og:image, og:title, og:description are correct.
  4. If they’re wrong, fix them on your site and click Scrape Again.
  5. Once everything’s clean, share to your social platforms.

It adds a step, but it’s way better than posting first, realizing the preview is broken, having to delete the message, having to ask friends to click again — that 1 minute upfront pays for itself.

Wrapping up

Social caching is a good thing most of the time (saves your site bandwidth, makes previews snappy), but the moment you need to update og:image, it becomes the obstacle. The ?v=1 trick feels kind of low-tech, but it genuinely works — especially on debugger-less platforms like LINE and Discord, where it’s basically the only option.

Hopefully this gives you a clearer picture of social OG caching and cache-busting.
