My 100-Day Body Recomposition Experiment

Warning

Just a heads-up: this post is a personal experience share, not professional medical advice. Please don't follow it blindly. If you have any concerns, check with a doctor or dietitian first.

Intro

Kind of funny, right? A tech blog bringing up "body recomposition." This was an experiment I started on 2026/3/21, and the goal was to do body recomposition over 100 days — meaning build muscle and drop body fat percentage.

So why did I do this? And along the way, what did it cost me, how did I plan it, how did I execute it, and how did it turn out? This post is here to document and share the process and results of my experiment. If you're an engineer like me and you've been thinking about body recomposition too, maybe this post gives you a tiny bit of direction — and knowing that I've already gone and tested it myself, I'm confident you can do it too.

Why I started this experiment

Let's start with why I suddenly decided to do this.

Through 2024–2025 I was actually already working on one thing: weight control. So I'd built up an exercise habit, and back then what I did was at-home "High-Intensity Interval Training (HIIT)." Why did I pick that? Honestly the main reason was that I wanted something short, fast, and able to drop weight quickly. xD

But by the end of 2025 (around December), I realized it just wasn't working. My weight had crept all the way up to 82 kg and I'd become what people call a "skinny-fat" guy. Some physical stuff started showing up too — for example, I've always gotten up at 7 a.m. sharp, but it was getting harder and harder to get out of bed, my sleep schedule started drifting, and my skin kept getting worse.

82 kg

Note

"Skinny-fat" describes someone with thin limbs but a big belly and waist — the whole person looks kind of puffy, like a cream puff, with relatively high body fat.

Skinny-fat illustration

Because of all that, on 2026/1/1 I started getting ready to do body recomposition. Note that carefully — I only started getting ready.

I went back and reviewed my exercise, diet, sleep, and so on, and after doing my homework for a good while, I didn't officially start body recomposition until 2026/3/21. At that point my weight was 82 kg and my body fat was as high as 28%, and my goal was: "within 100 days, get weight down to the 6x kg range and body fat down to 15–16%."

Before I started, what did I do?

So during 2026/1/1 to 2026/3/21, I mainly did five things:

  • Read a ton of diet-related literature
  • Learned to read food nutrition labels and ingredient lists
  • Made a diet plan
  • Nailed down correct exercise form
  • Learned the body basics (muscle, fat, basal metabolic rate, calories burned, calories consumed, protein intake, carb intake, fat intake)

On top of that, I built an Awesome Gemini Gems (personal assistants built on Gemini) system, poured all this knowledge into Gemini, and made good use of RayFit (my fat-loss companion) to help manage my daily eating.

RayFit fat-loss companion

Then I went to a hospital to get an InBody scan, at least to know some of my own body numbers. At the time they looked like this:

  • Weight: 82 kg
  • Body fat: 28%
  • Obesity degree: 130%
  • Basal metabolic rate: 1597 kcal
  • Visceral fat level: 11

A lot of people honestly couldn't tell I'd gotten this heavy — even I didn't realize it. So before you start body recomposition, I'd really recommend getting a basic InBody scan first, because it helps your future body recomposition a lot. At minimum you need to know your current body state before you can plan the next step.

And a warning here: never, ever blindly chase a "low weight," because low weight doesn't mean you're healthy. What matters more is muscle mass, body fat percentage, obesity degree, basal metabolic rate, visceral fat level, and so on — those are the real indicators of a healthy body. Looking at weight alone is really inaccurate and pointless, because for the human body, a lot of movement and organ protection relies on muscle mass. So whether you have enough muscle is the key to your health.

Reading your InBody numbers

Assuming you've now measured your InBody numbers, you'll see the following:

  • Weight: the total weight of your entire body, including muscle, fat, water, bone, and all other tissues.
  • Skeletal muscle mass: the muscle attached to your bones — the muscles you can control, usually the ones you use when you work out.
  • Body fat mass: the weight of the fat in your body. Fat is how your body stores energy, but too much of it hurts your health.
  • BMI (Body Mass Index): a number used to assess whether your weight is in a healthy range. The formula is: BMI = weight (kg) / height (m) squared.
  • Body fat percentage: the weight of fat in your body as a percentage of your total body weight. This helps you understand whether your body composition is healthy.
  • Visceral fat level: the fat wrapped around your internal organs. Too much visceral fat raises your risk of cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and other chronic conditions.
  • Basal metabolic rate: the minimum energy your body needs to keep you alive at rest. This helps you understand how many calories you need per day to maintain your weight.

These are numbers you absolutely need to understand, because they directly affect your future body recomposition plan — especially body fat percentage, visceral fat level, and basal metabolic rate. Those three have a big impact on both your health and the results of your recomposition.

Some people will see their BMI is normal and just ignore everything else. Honestly, mine was normal too, but BMI isn't accurate. What you really want to watch are the three big numbers I just mentioned — that's the real focus, and it's exactly why people always say BMI isn't accurate.

The basic idea of body recomposition

The basic idea of body recomposition is "build muscle, lower body fat percentage," and these two things support each other.

Why you'd want to "cut fat" is easy to get — that big blob on you is oil, it's fat. "Building muscle" means increasing your body's muscle mass. Besides making you stronger, more muscle also significantly raises your basal metabolic rate, so you burn more calories even at rest. That's one reason some people seem to eat whatever they want and barely gain weight — they've got enough muscle, so their basal metabolic rate is a bit higher (though more often it's because their day-to-day activity level is just naturally high).

Of course, unless you're competing in bodybuilding, you don't need to train to that extreme level. You just need to keep yourself in a state of "enough muscle, low body fat."

Basal metabolic rate?

Next let's get to know the term "basal metabolic rate." Simply put, your basal metabolic rate is the calories your body needs to burn at rest. Even while you sleep, your body burns calories to stay alive — that's your basal metabolic rate (BMR, Basal Metabolic Rate).

But what we rely on each day isn't just BMR — there's also AMR (Active Metabolic Rate), the calories your body needs while you're active. This number is much higher than BMR, because when you're active your body needs to burn more energy to keep moving.

AMR shows up all the time — eating, walking, exercising, all of that falls under AMR. So the calories you need in a day are actually BMR + AMR, and that number is how much you should be eating in a day.

BMR that burns even while you sleep, and AMR that only kicks in when you move

When you want to do body recomposition, the goal is to create a "calorie gap" or "calorie deficit" — the calories you burn in a day need to exceed the calories you eat. That's what makes your body burn its stored fat, which gets you the fat loss.

Basal metabolic rate does have a formula you can use to calculate it, and it's different for men and women, but let's not overcomplicate things here — measuring InBody is faster.

Once you've got all that, next we need to understand another thing: the "calorie gap."

The calorie gap

Calorie gap... calorie gap... so what actually is a calorie gap?

This tripped me up at first too. The reason I made a point of covering the InBody numbers earlier is to introduce this concept of a "calorie gap" or "calorie deficit," because you need to know how many calories you need in a day, then control how many you eat so you actually have a gap.

So how big should the gap be? For example, say you need 2000 calories a day. If you keep your intake at 1500 calories, then you've got a 500-calorie gap, and your body will slowly start burning your stored fat.

And fat isn't purely evil — it's kind of like your body's emergency food reserve. If you've owned a cat you'll get this even better: it's like a cat's primordial pouch.

Think of the human body as a country. That fat is the wealth in the treasury, and when your calorie intake isn't enough, your body dips into those reserves.

Fat is like your body's national treasury

Because of this, I really recommend buying a "sports watch" or "fitness band" to track your daily calorie burn. That way you'll know how many calories you need per day, and figuring out your calorie gap gets a lot more accurate.

Body recomposition has something to do with... insulin?

Yep, they're related — because insulin is a really important hormone in your body, and its main job is to regulate blood sugar. After you eat, your blood sugar rises, at which point your pancreas secretes insulin to help your body convert blood sugar into energy, or store the excess as fat.

Any time you eat, you start secreting insulin. Insulin mainly helps glucose enter your cells, and on top of that it also helps "synthesize fat and reduce muscle breakdown." Feels a bit contradictory reading this, right? You want body recomposition, but insulin helps "synthesize fat," so now you're wondering what on earth you're supposed to do.

This is where we bring in another concept: "insulin resistance." When you eat too many carbs for a long time (I'll explain what carbs are later), your pancreas secretes a lot of insulin, and your body starts developing "insulin resistance" — meaning your body's sensitivity to insulin drops. At that point your body starts "storing fat," i.e. you start getting fatter, because your cells have a certain storage limit (a ceiling). Once your cells are packed with energy, the leftover energy naturally gets converted to fat and stored — and that's why you gain weight.

And don't underestimate insulin resistance — it's also the source of a lot of diseases, like diabetes, cardiovascular disease, obesity, and so on. So you really do need to watch out for it.

Reading this far, I want to add one reminder. When people dig through the research, a lot of them bring up "sugar" and start demonizing it.

But I have to be honest — sugar isn't inherently a bad thing. What matters is how much you eat and what kind of sugar it is, because sugar is one of your body's main energy sources. When you eat too much sugar over a long period, your body starts developing "insulin resistance," at which point it starts "storing fat" — and that's why you gain weight.

Speaking of which, let me fill in a hole I dug earlier: what carbs actually are.

Carbohydrates, put plainly, are nutrients made of three elements — carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen. They're the body's most important and most convenient energy source. Picture them as the body's favorite "quick fuel." The white rice, noodles, bread, sweet potatoes, and fruit we normally eat — even the fiber in vegetables — all count as carbs.

So how do sugar and carbs relate? Simple: sugar is just one type of carbohydrate. In other words, "all sugars are carbs, but not all carbs are sugar." Get that idea down first and everything after gets easy.

Carbs can also be split by molecular structure into:

  • Monosaccharides: glucose, fructose, galactose
  • Disaccharides: sucrose, lactose, maltose
  • Polysaccharides: starch, cellulose, glycogen

Monosaccharides and disaccharides are called "simple sugars," while polysaccharides are called "complex carbs." This distinction is based on molecular structure. That's about as far as this little tidbit needs to go — this post isn't here to write chemistry formulas.

Learn to read nutrition labels and ingredients

Next let's talk about "nutrition labels." Before you buy something, especially when you're doing body recomposition, you absolutely need to learn to read nutrition labels, because that's the key to controlling your calorie intake.

Let me use the "Americano" I drink all the time as an example. On most labels you'll basically see the following:

  • Amount per serving
  • N servings per package
  • Calories
  • Protein
  • Fat
    • Saturated fat
    • Trans fat
  • Carbohydrates
    • Sugar
  • Sodium

Of these, there are a few things you most need to watch:

  1. Calories: if you want stricter calorie control, watch this number, because if you don't manage it well you can easily exceed your daily calorie needs and start putting on fat.
  2. Protein: muscle is mainly made of protein.
  3. Fat: watch the type of fat, especially saturated fat and trans fat — both are really bad for your health. What do I mean? Saturated fat raises your risk of cardiovascular disease, and trans fat raises your risk of cardiovascular disease, diabetes, obesity, and other chronic conditions. So try to avoid these two. A lot of products make them hard to avoid, but at least watch the number and try not to overdo it.
  4. Carbohydrates: watch the type of carbs, specifically the "sugar" part. 1 g of sugar is about 0.2 sugar cubes, 5 g = 1 cube, 25 g = 5 cubes (the WHO's recommended daily upper limit for refined sugar for health), 50 g = 10 cubes (the WHO's general daily upper limit for refined sugar). Honestly, if you open any snack in your house and check, you might find it's got way more than 25 g of sugar.
  5. Sodium: basically "salt." Eating too much salt makes you retain water and puffy, because salt makes your body hold on to water. Watch this during body recomposition too, because maybe you normally eat too many high-sodium foods and end up bloated. When you start exercising, the early progress is fast but then it stalls — and the reason for that early speed is that you're "shedding water." So keep an eye on this.

A tower of sugar cubes stacked up from a single bag of snacks

Once you can read these, watch out for one more thing: nutrition labels have a margin of error, roughly ±20%. So don't assume that when it says "Calories: 100 kcal," you're eating exactly 100 kcal — it might actually be somewhere between 80 and 120. But basically, once you start reading nutrition labels, you're already ahead of most people, because most people never look at them — some don't even know what a nutrition label is.

Reading this, some of you might wonder about "refined sugar." It's simple — it's basically "processed sugar," the stuff in candy, desserts, drinks, and so on. Refined sugar spikes your blood sugar fast, which triggers insulin secretion and leads to fat storage, so try to avoid it. "Natural sugar" refers to fruits, vegetables, and the like — natural sugar raises your blood sugar slowly and stimulates insulin less, so relatively speaking, it has a smaller impact on body recomposition.

After all, refined sugar rarely makes you feel full, so your brain keeps signaling you to eat more. Natural sugars, on the other hand, are mostly "whole foods" like fruits and vegetables, and they usually come with fiber that makes your brain feel full — so you don't keep wanting to eat.

Whole foods? Processed foods?

For modern life, we're surrounded by all kinds of "refined" foods. These are foods reshaped through various processing methods. Bread, which everyone eats, is actually a "processed food" too, because it's made by processing wheat flour, adding all sorts of additives, and baking it — and most of these processed foods are "refined foods."

So does that mean you can't eat processed foods? This is a common misconception, because a lot of the food in our lives is processed — milk, yogurt, soy milk, bread, crackers, snacks, and so on are all processed. But processed foods aren't necessarily refined foods, because some are extensions of "whole foods." Milk, yogurt, and soy milk, for example, are made through processing, but they still retain the nutritional character of the original food, so these can be eaten in moderation.

I deliberately used "bread" as an example earlier to show you that what you think of as "processed" isn't always a refined food. What you really need to watch is the "additives," because additives are mostly there to improve texture, extend shelf life, and so on, which can mean a lot of sugar, salt, and oil packed inside. That's what you should really focus on — not every food is evil.

After learning about whole foods and processed foods, a lot of people end up with one question: "So how am I supposed to eat?" It's actually simple — basically just follow these principles:

  • Choose whole foods as much as possible, and avoid refined sugar and highly processed foods.
  • Eat processed foods in moderation, but watch the type and amount of additives.

On top of that, some things are whole foods but their production process has already stripped away their nutritional value. For example, I used to buy fruit drinks. Even ones labeled "100% juice" have had the "fiber" removed during processing for the sake of texture.

Note

Fiber is a carbohydrate your body can't digest or absorb, but it's really important for gut health — it helps your gut move things along, promotes bowel movements, and helps control blood sugar and cholesterol.

If you genuinely can't tell which foods are whole and which are processed, here's a simple rule of thumb:

  • If you can still recognize the food's original form, it's mostly a whole food — apples, bananas, carrots, chicken breast, steak, and so on are all foods whose original form you can directly recognize.

The difference between whole foods and processed foods

Then pair that with reading the "ingredients" + "nutrition label," and you can roughly tell whether a food is whole or processed.

Because... if a product has a whole pile of ingredients thrown in for no reason, it's probably not a whole food.

Diet planning for body recomposition

I think this part is the hardest for a lot of people. Plenty of exercise material talks about the "calorie gap," but to actually hit that gap you have to weigh your food and calculate calories.

Take white rice — one gram of white rice is about 1.3 kcal, so 100 grams of white rice is...

  • 100 g * 1.3 kcal = 130 kcal

And the calories you need per day — say 2000 kcal — means you have to calculate the total calories of everything you eat in a day and keep it under 2000, so you have a calorie gap.

But... most modern people eat out, so who's realistically going to weigh their food and calculate calories out of nowhere? So here are a few approaches for you:

  1. Prepare your own food:
    • Prepare your own food as much as possible, so you can control the type and amount and avoid overeating calories.
    • For example: cook your own rice, steam vegetables, grill chicken breast, and so on — that way you know exactly what you're eating and how many calories it is.
  2. Choose healthy takeout:
    • If you really don't have time to prepare your own food, then choose healthy takeout and avoid excess calories.
    • For example: pick salad, grilled chicken breast, steamed fish, and avoid fried food, desserts, drinks, and other high-calorie stuff.
  3. Use a food-tracking app:
    • Use a food-tracking app to log everything you eat each day, so you know your daily calorie intake and can keep it within your target range.

For me, I prepare my own food, but I don't specifically weigh it. Instead, I control calories through "portion control" — the "fist rule," where each meal's portion is roughly the size of your fist. That way you can roughly control your daily calorie intake.

One meal roughly controls to:

  • 1 fist of protein (e.g. chicken breast, fish, tofu)
  • 0.5 fist of carbs (e.g. white rice, sweet potato, oats)
  • 2–3 fists of vegetables (e.g. greens, carrots)

The fist rule illustration

This amount gradually increases as you build muscle, because once you have muscle, your body needs more calories to maintain it. If you don't eat enough, your muscle gets broken down instead.

When you start body recomposition, also watch your protein intake. There's basically a formula for it:

  • Protein intake = weight (kg) * 1.6–2.2 g

If you're doing weight training, I'd suggest bumping protein intake up to 2.2 g/kg, which helps you build and maintain muscle without losing it.

Exercise planning for body recomposition

Like I mentioned earlier, through 2024–2025 I was mainly doing at-home "High-Intensity Interval Training (HIIT)," and the reasons I never slimmed down were these:

  • No proper diet control
  • No understanding of nutrition labels
  • No understanding of the calorie gap or basal metabolism concepts

And the most important one: my body's muscle mass was insufficient. Stepping onto the InBody scan, my left side was in a muscle-deficient state. When your muscle mass is low, no matter how hard you exercise, if your basal metabolic rate doesn't go up AND your diet isn't controlled, it's basically wasted effort.

So the first thing I did in phase one on 2026/3/21 was... "build muscle" — I needed to increase my muscle mass first.

But I didn't go to a gym. I bought some fitness equipment from Decathlon myself:

  • 2, 3, and 5 kg dumbbells, one set each
  • 6 and 8 kg kettlebells, one each
  • A calf-stretch board

Then I paired it with a fitness band to track my daily calorie burn + activity level.

  • March–April was mainly focused on "building muscle"
  • May–June I started adding "cardio"
  • June–July I started ramping up the intensity

Here's the average rep count I did:

  • Bicep training: 150 reps each arm
  • Ab training: 250 reps
  • Back rows: 100 reps
  • Soleus (calf) training: 100 reps
  • Forearm training: 50 reps
  • Shoulder press training: 50 reps
  • High-intensity boxing + running: 30 minutes

(This is basically turning into a One Punch Man special.)

Overall it took at least around two hours.

Each week I'd plan 1–2 "rest days" + "refeed days," because muscle needs time to recover after exercise, and blindly exercising can actually make your body think it's hit a famine or danger and switch to energy-saving mode. Proper refeed days also give your body enough energy to repair muscle, so it actually grows. I'm not telling you to eat extremely clean forever — long-term that's just miserable — so I'll still eat salt-and-pepper chicken, fried chicken, and fried stuff, but I control it to "refeed days" and pick "healthier fried options" like chicken breast, chicken tenders, sweet potato fries, and so on. That way you can enjoy good food without wrecking your body recomposition plan.

On top of that, I also bought a home InBody scale to use at home. The accuracy isn't guaranteed, but at least it gives you a rough sense of whether yesterday's behavior was on track or off.

Note

One important concept here: you can't spot-reduce fat from a specific area — this is where a lot of people get it wrong. Fat loss is usually whole-body, and belly fat is usually the hardest to lose.

Can you do body recomposition without a gym?

Absolutely, but you have to nail correct exercise form first. This post isn't saying gyms are useless or that hiring a trainer is useless — it's that you don't need a gym to do body recomposition, because you can use at-home equipment. But you have to pair it with correct form to actually get the results.

Since I have some athletic background, pairing that with some YouTube workout tutorials let me pick up how to use the muscles pretty quickly. So I can't offer too much advice on this part — everyone's athletic base is different, so you have to find the workout style that fits you and pair it with correct form, otherwise you'll train forever and get nowhere.

Because if you haven't properly learned exercise knowledge, you'll strain something easily, and any strain basically means 1–2 weeks of recovery, which is a net loss. You might as well bite the bullet and pay for a gym and a trainer to teach you correct form — that way you avoid injury and skip a lot of wasted detours.

Plus a gym has lots of equipment you can't use at home — barbells, dumbbells, cable machines, treadmills, spin bikes, and so on. So if you have the time and budget, I'd still suggest going to a gym, because the environment also motivates you to work out more.

What it cost

In the intro I said I'd talk about the cost, so let me be honest with you here.

My costs mainly split into two parts. The first is the one-time equipment investment — the Decathlon dumbbells, kettlebells, and stretch board I mentioned, plus the home InBody scale and the fitness band. These are all things you buy once and use for a long time.

The second is the daily food budget I'm constantly burning through. I originally bought healthy meals out every day, then switched to cooking for myself, which runs about NT$1,000–2,000-something a week. That works out to roughly NT$300 a day, which saves quite a bit compared to buying healthy meals every day — and I eat plenty full.

Note

I don't eat breakfast much, so it's usually just lunch and dinner, because eating breakfast sometimes gives me an upset stomach — at most I'll have coffee.

That said, cooking for yourself has a downside: you have to hit the evening market to buy ingredients, and the whole thing demands more routine and discipline. You have to buy groceries at a set time and prep meals at a set time — you can't just eat whenever the mood strikes like with takeout. But flip it around, and that routine is exactly what body recomposition needs most, so for me it's actually a plus.

The process and results

So, earlier my starting numbers were:

  • Weight: 82 kg
  • Body fat: 28%
  • Obesity degree: 130%
  • Basal metabolic rate: 1597 kcal
  • Visceral fat level: 11

100 days later, on 2026/7/4, my home InBody numbers were:

  • Weight: 67.9 kg
  • Body fat: 16.5%
  • Obesity degree: 97%
  • Basal metabolic rate: 1635 kcal
  • Visceral fat level: 4

Of course, a home InBody isn't as accurate as a hospital machine, but let me be clear about one thing: weight is measured directly, so that number is accurate — and obesity degree, which is derived from weight, isn't far off either. What actually has error is the stuff "estimated" via bioelectrical impedance, like body fat percentage, visceral fat level, and basal metabolic rate. From comparing hospital and home machines myself, the gap is roughly 4–5%, and home machines usually estimate a bit optimistically, so I conservatively put my body fat somewhere around 17–18%.

Also, at the start my InBody showed my left side as muscle-deficient, but both sides have recovered to normal now.

100 days later

Overall though, I lost 14.1 kg, my body fat dropped by more than 10 percentage points even using conservative numbers, my obesity degree went from 130% to 97%, my visceral fat level dropped from 11 to 4, and my basal metabolic rate ticked up slightly too.

Losing weight this fast — didn't something go wrong with your body? The answer is... no. I got blood tests done throughout this period and the results were all normal. My blood pressure, blood sugar, cholesterol, and so on are all healthier than before.

Another change I really felt is my sleep schedule. Like I mentioned, I used to have a harder and harder time getting up at 7. Now I not only get up at 7 no problem, the first thing I do after getting up is take a cold shower.

But let me be honest about two things:

  • First, averaging close to 1 kg lost per week is actually on the fast side. I only dared do it because I paired it with blood tests and data monitoring. I don't recommend copying this pace directly — going slower is actually safer and easier to sustain.
  • Second, under a calorie gap this big, honestly "hanging on to as much muscle as possible" is already pretty good. So strictly speaking, what I accomplished in these 100 days is "lots of fat loss, holding on to muscle as best I could." And that's exactly what most people with high body fat should do in phase one anyway — only after your body fat comes down do you really focus on stacking muscle up.

At work I still eat takeout normally and order lunch boxes with coworkers, but I try to pick healthier contents — chicken breast, fish, and so on — and avoid fried food, desserts, drinks, and other high-calorie stuff. I'll peel the skin off braised chicken legs, and I stick to sugar-free drinks.

Conclusion

Through the whole process, I personally think the most important thing is treating it as a "long-term battle." Body recomposition isn't something you finish in the short term — it takes long-term effort and persistence, especially in diet control and building an exercise habit, which both take long-term learning and adjusting.

And you also have to "deliberately" schedule certain things in. If you had zero exercise habit before, you have to deliberately carve out workout time and build the habit, so you can keep it going.

The process is honestly painful. A big reason I bought a home InBody scale was to let myself see that today's me is better than yesterday's, paired with Gemini Gems to give me some advice and direction (and even encouragement, lol).

So this post is about wrapping up here. I hope it helps those of you wanting to do body recomposition, because it's a long-term battle — there's no shortcut, only continued effort and persistence. And for now I'm still maintaining my physique, because I really don't want to gain it all back.
