Migrating From Heroku to Render: A Deployment Guide

#Git #GitHub #Render #Heroku #PaaS #Deployment #Free Plan

Intro

Heroku was my go-to backend platform for years. But on 2022/11/28, Heroku killed the free tier — forcing me to migrate. This post covers the move from Heroku to Render.com and why I picked Render specifically.

Heroku

Heroku is a veteran PaaS — many people used it for practice projects and small apps because deploying was just a git push, and the free tier was generous enough for learning.

Unfortunately, Heroku killed its free tier on 2022/11/28, so a lot of people scrambled to find alternatives.

Why did Heroku kill it? From Heroku’s Next Chapter:

Our product, engineering, and security teams are spending an extraordinary amount of effort to manage fraud and abuse of the Heroku free product plans.

So that’s why.

Render.com

What’s Render? If you search “Render” as a dev you’ll get all kinds of unrelated results, so I usually search “Render.com” to land on the right thing.

Render.com is a PaaS like Heroku, but with some real differences. Render still offers a free plan (Heroku doesn’t, anymore), and Render’s deploys are Docker-based while Heroku’s are Git-based.

Does Docker-based mean you have to write a Dockerfile yourself? No. Render auto-generates one for your project. The flow is just slightly different from Heroku.

For a static site, pick “Static Site”. For a Node.js backend, pick “Web Service”:

On the “Create a new Web Service” screen, find your project under “Connect a repository”. If it’s not there, click “Configure account” — your GitHub account might not have given Render permission to see that repo:

On GitHub, pick “All repositories” or “Only select repositories” — I went with “Only select repositories”. Pick the repo to deploy and hit Save. You’ll bounce back to Render and the repo shows up:

Things to watch out for

Render has similar gotchas to Heroku — free instances sleep after 15 minutes idle, and waking takes ~30 seconds. From Render’s docs:

Web Services on the free instance type are automatically spun down after 15 minutes of inactivity. When a new request for a free service comes in, Render spins it up again so it can process the request.
This can cause a response delay of up to 30 seconds for the first request that comes in after a period of inactivity.

Render also gives you 750 free instance hours per month — but heads up, it’s shared across your entire account. Multiple projects on free tier eat through it fast. You can blow the 750 hours in a month if you’ve got 3+ services running 24/7.

To check current free-tier usage — Render Dashboard → click your avatar → Billing.

Closing thoughts

People ask “why not fly.io, Vercel, etc.?” Genuinely depends on your needs. Fly.io I didn’t quite get the hang of; Vercel’s serverless model doesn’t match every workload.

Worth reading for a PaaS comparison:

  • Best PaaS backend hosting: Heroku vs Render.com vs Fly.io vs Railway.app

Render quick recap of gotchas:

Sleep timer

Same as Heroku — 15 minutes idle = sleep. ~30 seconds to wake.

Shared 750-hour pool

Render’s 750 free hours apply to your whole account, not per-service. (Heroku, IIRC, was per-service.) So if you’ve got 3 services running 24/7, that’s 24 × 3 = 72 hours of usage per day. Free quota resets on the 1st of each month.

Build minutes are tracked separately

  • Instance hours: 750/mo
  • Build minutes: 400/mo

Check current usage at Render billing.

Deploy speed is inconsistent

I noticed deploys took anywhere from a few minutes to 20+ minutes. Per Render community:

Looked into it — free plan deploys are explicitly slower than paid:

Free plans are noted as having slower deploys than paid plans, that’s one of the limitations of Free Plans

Of course, slow deploys have other causes too — but free tier is one factor.

Web Service requires an HTTP endpoint

What does this mean? If you’re deploying Node.js, you need an HTTP server (e.g., Express). Render uses HTTP listening as the deploy health check.

If your app doesn’t open a port, Render gets stuck in “Deploying” and eventually fails.

Common case — Discord bots don’t need HTTP servers normally. So bolt on a tiny Express server to satisfy Render — see my Hermione Discord bot (article in Mandarin) for an example.

Deploying bots

For Discord bot-style projects, Render recommends the (paid) Background Worker service. Otherwise you’ll often hit a “bot sends every message twice” bug — Render runs two instances briefly during deploys.

I noticed this myself — every deploy, my bot sent two messages for about an hour, then settled back to single messages. I didn’t pay for Background Worker — just lived with the deploy-time double-send.

If you want clean behavior, use Background Worker for bots.

Other limits

Other restrictions are in the official docs. I didn’t hit many of them, so just linking: Render free plan limits.
