How to Use GPT-5.5 and ChatGPT Images 2.0: 30 Prompt Templates for IG Cards, Knowledge Decks, and Product Mockups

#AI #ChatGPT #GPT-5.5 #ChatGPT Images 2.0 #gpt-image-2 #Prompts #AI Image Generation #Instagram Cards

Intro

OpenAI dropped two updates back-to-back in late April 2026: GPT-5.5, the new language model, and ChatGPT Images 2.0 (codename gpt-image-2), the image generation model that ships alongside it. When I first saw the news I honestly didn’t think much of it — there’s a new model every few months, and most of them are basically the same thing repackaged. But once I actually tried it, the image generation side is on a totally different level than before. The fact that text — especially non-English text — finally doesn’t blur into mush makes a huge difference for anyone making content.

Of the two updates, the first one gets more headlines, but the second one is what actually makes content creators sit up.

Why is this round worth a dedicated post? Because the two things AI image generation has always been criticized for — text turning into garbled mush and ugly layouts — both got solved this time. Chinese characters render correctly, IG cards / knowledge decks / product mockups / infographics can all be used directly without going back into Figma to fix things up.

If you’re not totally familiar with what a Prompt is or how to interact with an LLM, you can flip through my earlier post on LLM, RAG, Prompt — A Look at the Common GenAI Vocabulary first. This one will jump straight into GPT-5.5.

The post breaks into four parts: clearing up a common misconception first, then what’s actually different between GPT-5.5 and GPT-5, then what makes ChatGPT Images 2.0 strong, and finally a universal prompt structure plus 30 scenario-specific templates — IG knowledge cards, carousel covers, blog headers, infographics, product mockups, quote cards, and itineraries — all copy-paste ready.

First, let’s clear this up: are GPT-5.5 and ChatGPT Images 2.0 the same thing?

No, they’re two separate models. This is a really common mix-up — even most news headlines mash the two together.

In short:

  • GPT-5.5 is a language model, released April 23, 2026. It reads your request, thinks, writes code, handles text. It supports image input (you can hand it an image to look at), but it doesn’t draw images itself.
  • ChatGPT Images 2.0 (codename gpt-image-2) is the model that’s actually doing the drawing this round. It shipped on April 21, two days earlier than GPT-5.5.

The two just happen to have shipped from OpenAI in the same week, which is why people lump them together — but technically they’re separate. When you ask ChatGPT “draw me an IG card”, behind the scenes two models cooperate: GPT-5.5 reads your sentence, figures out what you actually want, then hands the task off to ChatGPT Images 2.0 to draw, and the result comes back to you.

If I had to put it in everyday terms — GPT-5.5 is more like the editor at a publishing house: reads your idea, organizes the brief. ChatGPT Images 2.0 is the illustrator who actually puts pen to paper. You only talk to the editor, but the illustrator is the one drawing.

With that division of labor clear, let’s look at each model’s upgrades.

What’s the difference between GPT-5.5 and GPT-5?

Punchline first: GPT-5.5 is an iterative upgrade on top of GPT-5. OpenAI calls it their smartest, most intuitive model yet. It went live on ChatGPT on April 23, 2026, and the API followed the next day on April 24.

So what actually got better? For me personally, four things stand out:

Area What changed
Coding / debugging Hit 82.7% on Terminal-Bench 2.0; multi-turn fix-up is way stronger than GPT-5
Task comprehension Finishes the same tasks with fewer Tokens; latency matches GPT-5.4 but feels noticeably smarter
Image input Preserves up to 10.24 million pixels without resizing (single-side cap of 6000px), so screenshots, documents, and design files come through with way more detail
Context window 1M Tokens — you can fit a long document or an entire code repo in one go

In short, GPT-5.5 isn’t a generational architecture leap — it’s more like OpenAI iterating the GPT-5 line into a mature version. The most noticeable day-to-day difference is that it’s better at carrying tasks forward on its own. You don’t have to constantly chase it asking “and then what” — it’ll keep moving.

What makes ChatGPT Images 2.0 strong?

ChatGPT Images 2.0 is a substantial upgrade. OpenAI’s Sam Altman described the jump from gpt-image-1 to gpt-image-2 as comparable to going from GPT-3 to GPT-5. Sounds like hyperbole, but once you actually use it you’ll see what he means.

So what’s the deal? Four things:

1. Text rendering is finally reliable

The biggest tell of AI-generated images has always been text. You ask for a magazine cover with a headline and you get blurred, garbled, alien-looking strings. Images 2.0 fixed that. Headlines, subheaders, price tags, the date under a barcode — all rendered correctly. The Mexican menu samples it can produce now would not be flagged by anyone if printed and put on a restaurant table.

2. It actually writes Chinese (and Japanese, Korean, etc.) correctly

This part is huge for anyone making content in non-Latin scripts. Previously, generating Chinese IG cards via ChatGPT was basically a no-go — characters would be missing strokes, swapped, or just turn into garbled glyphs. Images 2.0 has made big improvements across Chinese, Japanese, Korean, Hindi, and Bengali.

It’s not 100% perfect — you’ll still occasionally get a stray wrong character. But you can use the editing mode to regenerate just that region instead of redrawing the whole thing. More on that later.

3. Layout and visual hierarchy got better

It’s not just that the characters render — the layout hierarchy is right too. Headlines big, subheads smaller, bullets aligned, whitespace reasonable. The whole thing looks like it was actually designed. You can generate brand kits with consistent color, logos, and typography across multiple pages in one shot.

4. There’s actual reasoning happening

Images 2.0 is the first OpenAI image model with built-in reasoning. Before it draws, it thinks through where text should go, how space should be allocated, whether the visual logic holds up — then it generates. So complex compositions and dense layouts come out way more stable than before, no more visual chaos.

Looking at all four, the upgrade direction this time isn’t “draw more artistically” — it’s “draw more usably”. Designers might feel a bit threatened at first, but in practice it’s more like compressing a two-to-three-hour layout job into three minutes. What’s getting saved is the repetitive grunt work, not the actual design judgment.

A universal structure for writing good prompts

Before you start copying templates, a quick note on prompt structure — otherwise you’ll just swap topics blindly and the output quality will keep bouncing around.

ChatGPT Images 2.0 prefers structured prompts. That means breaking down what should be in the image element by element, instead of just asking for “a pretty IG image”. The structure I usually use has six elements:

Element What goes here
Subject The core message or content the image needs to communicate
Use case IG post, carousel cover, blog header, infographic, etc.
Layout Portrait, landscape, square, aspect ratio, how the canvas is divided
Color Primary, secondary, background — concrete hex codes or mood adjectives
Typography Headline font style (sans-serif, serif, handwritten), size hierarchy
On-image text The actual text in quotes — title, subtitle, bullets

In short, don’t let the model guess. The more specific you are, the more precisely it draws.

Note
When you write prompts in any language with on-image text, wrap the exact strings in quotes and label which is the title, which is the subtitle, which are bullets. That way the model knows which words to actually render and which are just describing.

This change + preserve pattern stops the model from redrawing the entire image — it only touches what you want touched.

Practical tips when actually using it

Knowing the structure and templates is one thing — here are a few tips that have genuinely saved me time.

1. Saving Chinese (or any non-Latin) text that came out wrong

I mentioned earlier that Images 2.0 handles Chinese now, but it’s not 100%. If only one or two characters in an entire image are wrong, don’t redraw the whole thing — open the editor in ChatGPT, select the text region, and tell it “only change ‘XXX’ to ‘YYY’, keep everything else the same”. It’ll only touch that region; nothing else gets affected.

If the entire text block came out garbled, it’s usually because the prompt didn’t wrap the on-image text in clear quotes. Go back, wrap the strings, and rerun. Way more stable.

2. Style consistency across a series

When you’re making series content (e.g., multiple slides of one IG carousel), once you’re happy with the first output, the prompts for the rest need to explicitly say “keep the same color, typography, and layout style as the previous slide”, and re-paste the key style description from the first one (e.g., “off-white background, deep red text, flat design”). The model doesn’t automatically remember what the previous slide looked like — you have to carry it forward yourself.

3. Aspect ratios per platform

Different platforms have different image ratios. Spell it out in the prompt or the output won’t be directly usable. Common ratios:

Use case Ratio
IG post / card 1:1 (square) or 4:5 (portrait)
IG Story / Reels cover 9:16 (portrait)
Blog cover / OG image 16:9 or 1.91:1
YouTube thumbnail 16:9
FB post 1.91:1 or 1:1
Magazine-style cover 3:4 (portrait)

What this table tells you: 4:5 is the best deal for IG cards, because it takes the largest real estate on IG and the algorithm tends to favor it for reach.

4. Don’t pile too much into one image

If your prompt asks for “complex composition + multiple fonts + five paragraphs of text + three icons” all at once, the model loses track and usually sacrifices text accuracy first. Keep one main title, one subtitle, and at most five bullet points per image. Anything more, split into a carousel.

Wrap-up

That’s the whole thing — what’s new in GPT-5.5, what makes ChatGPT Images 2.0 actually powerful, the prompt structure, and 30 scenario-specific copy-paste templates.

Honestly, before Images 2.0, I treated AI image generation as a toy. Generating an avatar or a concept piece was fine, but trying to use it for commercial assets or IG-ready cards always fell short — either the text was ugly or the layout was off, and you’d end up back in Figma fixing things. Images 2.0 is the first generation where I’ve felt like “this can actually be used as-is”.

There are still limits, of course. Anything that requires precise brand compliance still needs designers doing the final pass. But for 80% of day-to-day content material, you can rely on these 30 templates to roll your own.
