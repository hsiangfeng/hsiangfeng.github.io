Is SEO Not Enough? Meet AEO — Getting Your Site Found by AI Search

Intro

This post is a quick walk through AEO (Answer Engine Optimization). With more and more people swapping Google for ChatGPT and Perplexity, the old SEO playbook needs an update — so let's talk about what AEO actually is, how it differs from SEO, and what you can do about it.

A quick recap on SEO

Before we get into AEO, let's quickly revisit what SEO is.

SEO stands for Search Engine Optimization. The short version: it's about getting your site to rank higher on Google, Bing, and other search engines so users can find you more easily.

The usual SEO playbook covers:

  • Keyword optimization: make sure your content includes the terms users actually search for
  • meta tags: dial in the title, description, etc., so search engines understand what your page is about
  • Backlinks: get other sites to link to you to boost authority
  • Site speed and experience: load times, mobile-friendliness, all of which impact rankings
  • Sitemap and robots.txt: tell search engines about your site structure and which pages should be indexed

Personally, I've written a few SEO-related posts before — like The three Core Web Vitals that matter for SEO: LCP, FID, CLS (article in Mandarin) and A quick guide to robots.txt (article in Mandarin) — go check them out if you're curious.

So at its core SEO is about ranking your site as high as possible on the results page. Higher rank = more clicks = more traffic. That logic has held for over a decade — but things are starting to shift, and that shift is where AEO comes in.

So what's AEO?

Picture this scenario for a second:

In the old days, if you wanted to know "what is CSR?" you'd open Google, type the keyword, and pick a decent-looking article from the results. But these days? A lot of people just open ChatGPT or Perplexity, ask "what is CSR?", and the AI hands them a clean answer — they never click through to anyone's site.

Which raises a problem: if users aren't clicking into your site anymore, what good is your SEO?

AEO stands for Answer Engine Optimization. "Answer engine" here means the AI tools that answer your questions directly — ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews, Bing Copilot, and so on.

So AEO is about...

『Making your content the source AI cites when it answers questions.』

Here's an analogy that might help. Traditional SEO is like getting your book onto the most visible shelf in the bookstore — people who walk in might notice it. AEO is like getting your book quoted by the teacher in class — every student sees it whether they ever set foot in the bookstore or not.

Note While researching this stuff you might also bump into terms like GEO (Generative Engine Optimization), LLMO, AIO, and friends. These are all basically describing the same thing, and the industry hasn't really settled on strict definitions, so I'm sticking with AEO throughout this post.

Why should you care about AEO?

You might be thinking: my SEO is doing fine, why bother with AEO?

Let's start with a phenomenon called the zero-click search. What does that mean? It means the user gets their answer right on the search results page — they never click into anyone's site.

How dramatic is this? Around 70% of Google searches are now zero-click, and the number is still climbing.

And that's just regular Google. Throw AI search into the mix and it gets wilder — ChatGPT has hundreds of millions of weekly active users at this point, and Perplexity's traffic has exploded several-fold over the past year.

So the way users get information is shifting. The old story was: write a post, do SEO, Google ranks you near the top, users click through. The new story is: user asks AI a question, AI hands them the answer, your post never even gets a chance to be seen. QQ

The good news is, when your content does get cited by AI, the upside is real — pages cited by AI Overviews tend to see meaningful boosts in organic clicks, and visitors coming via AI recommendations convert noticeably better than typical organic traffic.

So AEO isn't just a trend — it directly affects your traffic and conversion.

How SEO and AEO differ

Here's a table to make the contrast easier to see:

DimensionTraditional SEOAEO
GoalRank as high as possible on results pageGet cited as a source by AI
User behaviorBrowse results, click linksRead AI's answer directly
Optimization unitPage level (title, meta, backlinks)Fact level (extractable claims and data)
Content styleLong-form, keyword-denseStructured, answer-first per paragraph
Technical focusmeta tags, sitemap, page speedSchema Markup, structured data
MetricsRank, traffic, click-through rateCitation count, brand mention rate
Success indicatorRank #1 on GoogleBecome a trusted source AI cites

One important observation: less than 40% of sources cited by AI Overviews come from Google's top 10 ranked pages. Translation — being #1 on Google does not guarantee AI will cite you, because AI cares about slightly different signals than traditional search engines.

How do AI search engines decide who to cite?

Before we talk about how to do AEO, we need to understand how AI decides whose content to cite.

Most AI search engines run on an architecture called RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation). If RAG is new to you, my earlier post Heard of LLM, RAG, and Prompt? A walkthrough of common Generative AI terminology (article in Mandarin) has a fuller explanation.

The short version of RAG looks like this:

  1. The user asks a question
  2. The AI system searches for documents related to that question
  3. The relevant documents are filtered by authority, content quality, and other criteria — usually only the top few survive
  4. The language model generates an answer based on those filtered docs and cites the sources

Different platforms do this slightly differently. ChatGPT defaults to answering from the model's own knowledge, and only fetches the web when search is enabled. Perplexity searches the web on every answer and shows citations by default. Google AI Overviews plugs into Google's own search index and knowledge graph.

So what does AI look at when filtering sources? Typically these dimensions:

  • Trustworthiness: is your site a credible source
  • Content quality: is the structure clear, are claims backed up, does it actually answer the question
  • Freshness: is the content updated regularly
  • Structure: clear headings, lists, tables — anything that makes content easier for AI to extract

Now that you know how it works under the hood, let's get into what you can actually do.

How to do AEO

Lead with the answer, add context after

This is the single most important AEO writing principle.

When AI extracts content from your page, it usually only looks at the first sentence or two of each paragraph. If you open with a long ramp-up or background story, AI will likely skip past you and find a source that answers the question more directly.

So every paragraph should start by directly answering the core question of that paragraph, with explanation and context coming after.

For example, suppose you're introducing Schema Markup:

The not-so-good version:

Before we discuss Schema Markup, let's talk about the history of structured data. Back in 2011, Google, Bing, and Yahoo jointly launched schema.org... (and only then gets to what Schema Markup actually is)

The better version:

Schema Markup is structured data markup added to HTML to help search engines and AI understand your content. The common format is JSON-LD, which you can use to tag article authors, publish dates, FAQ entries, and more.

See it? The second version tells you upfront what Schema Markup is — AI can grab that paragraph as-is to answer a user's question.

Make every paragraph stand on its own

AI parses content paragraph by paragraph, not at the article level. So each paragraph should be able to answer one question on its own — even if it's lifted out of context.

The recommended approach is to give every H2/H3 heading a clear semantic — ideally one question or one concept — keep paragraphs to a reasonable length, and lean on lists and tables to organize information. AI has an easier time grabbing the key points that way.

Add structured data (Schema Markup)

Schema Markup is one of the most important ways to make AI understand your content. Pages with proper Schema Markup are noticeably more likely to get cited.

For blog posts, I'd recommend the following Schemas:

  • Article / BlogPosting: marks your article's title, author, publish date, etc.
  • FAQPage: historically used to mark up Q&A-style sections so AI can extract pairs directly. Quick update though — Google fully retired FAQ rich results on 2026-05-07, and the official AI Optimization Guide explicitly lists structured data under things you don't need to do, so this Schema is no longer worth adding
  • BreadcrumbList: marks your site's hierarchy
  • Person + Organization: marks the author and their organization

Schema Markup is written in JSON-LD and goes inside <head>. If you're on Hexo or WordPress, there's usually a plugin that generates it automatically — you don't have to write it by hand.

Back your content with data where appropriate

AI prefers content backed by data over subjective takes. So sprinkle in concrete data and citations where it makes sense, and link to the original sources.

Compare "AI search is growing fast and more people are using it" with something like "according to stats, Perplexity's traffic more than doubled in the past year." The second one is way more likely to get pulled into an AI answer as supporting evidence.

Build topical authority

AI prefers sources that have deep coverage on a topic. What does that mean? Imagine you've written one article about SEO, while another site has a full series covering SEO, AEO, Core Web Vitals, Schema Markup, robots.txt, and more. AI will lean toward the second site, because it's demonstrated broader and deeper knowledge of the subject.

So I'd recommend building topic clusters around your core topics — one pillar post linked to multiple supporting posts. Personally, I've written The three Core Web Vitals that matter for SEO: LCP, FID, CLS (article in Mandarin), A quick guide to robots.txt (article in Mandarin), and there's also a section on SEO inside the Hexo series (article in Mandarin). All of these cross-link to each other so AI can recognize the depth on this topic.

Update your content regularly

AI search engines significantly favor fresh content for time-sensitive queries — content cited by AI tends to be much newer than what shows up in traditional search. So at minimum, revisit your important posts every quarter, update the date, and refresh any data points.

llms.txt: a sitemap for AI

Most of what we covered above is about how you write your content. There's also a more technical play — drop an llms.txt file on your site.

What is llms.txt?

If you've read my earlier A quick guide to robots.txt (article in Mandarin), you know robots.txt tells search engine crawlers which pages they can and can't crawl. llms.txt does the opposite.

robots.txt is telling crawlers "you can't come in." llms.txt is telling AI "hey, the good stuff is over here, come look."

llms.txt is a draft proposal introduced by Jeremy Howard, co-founder of Answer.AI, in 2024. The core idea is...

『Proactively tell AI what important content lives on your site.』

Why do we need this? Because when AI reads your webpage, it runs into noise — navigation bars, ads, JavaScript, all kinds of clutter — and pulling out the actually-valuable content is hard. AI's context window is also finite, so it can't just slurp your entire site. And AI may land on a single article without any way to know what other valuable content you have.

So llms.txt provides a Markdown-formatted site guide that proactively tells AI your site structure and where the key content is.

Quick comparison between robots.txt and llms.txt:

Dimensionrobots.txtllms.txt
PurposeTell crawlers which pages not to crawlTell AI which content is most important
MindsetExclusion-driven (Disallow)Discovery-driven (good stuff here)
FormatPlain text directivesMarkdown
AudienceTraditional search crawlersLLMs

What does the llms.txt format look like?

The format is simple — it's just a Markdown file:

markdown
# Site name

> Short description of the site (in blockquote)

You can put extra notes here.

## Docs

- [Article title](https://example.com/page.md): short description of this page
- [Another article](https://example.com/other.md): what this page is about

## Blog

- [Blog post](https://example.com/blog/post.md): short description of the post

## Optional

- [Supplemental material](https://example.com/extra.md): content AI only reads if it needs more info

A few key points:

  • H1 (required): your site or project name
  • Blockquote description (recommended): describe what your site is about in a sentence or two
  • H2 sections (optional): group your content by topic, with links and short descriptions under each
  • Optional section (optional): links here are non-essential — AI reads them only if it needs more

It's also recommended to point links to .md files, since Markdown is easier for AI to parse than HTML.

llms.txt vs llms-full.txt

Beyond llms.txt you might also see llms-full.txt. Short version: llms.txt is like a book's table of contents, while llms-full.txt is the entire book. The first lets AI quickly understand what's on your site; the second packs the full content of all docs in so AI can read everything end-to-end.

I'd recommend doing both — llms.txt as the index for fast lookup, and llms-full.txt for when AI needs to dig in.

How do you implement this on Hexo?

If you're on Hexo like me, there's no dedicated plugin to auto-generate llms.txt yet, but the implementation is pretty simple.

The most direct approach: drop an llms.txt file under source/. When Hexo builds, it copies that file straight to public/, so it'll be accessible at https://yourdomain/llms.txt.

Heads up though — Hexo by default renders Markdown files into HTML, so you need to add this file to skip_render in _config.yml:

yaml
skip_render:
  - llms.txt
  - llms-full.txt

That tells Hexo to leave the file alone and just copy it to the output directory as-is.

If you don't want to maintain it manually, you could also write a small Node.js script that runs after hexo generate and scans your posts to produce llms.txt — conceptually similar to what hexo-generator-sitemap does.

Does AI actually read llms.txt?

To be honest — llms.txt is still very early-stage. It's not an official standard.

If you want to know whether AI is actually reading your llms.txt, you can check server logs or analytics tools. Some site owners have spotted OpenAI's crawlers (OAI-SearchBot, ChatGPT-User) hitting llms.txt, but a hit doesn't mean the AI is actually using it when answering questions.

Google has been more reserved on this. Google's John Mueller even compared llms.txt to the long-deprecated meta keywords tag, suggesting no AI system really uses it right now.

That said, it's interesting that several hundred sites already implement llms.txt, including Anthropic, Stripe, Cloudflare, Vercel, Supabase, and other well-known names. So even if the effect is unproven, the industry is at least taking it seriously.

My take: setting up llms.txt takes very little time — basically zero cost. Even if AI doesn't officially support it today, there's a high chance support comes later, so you might as well put one up and let your site be ready in advance.

Common AEO terminology

If this is your first time touching AEO, the jargon can feel a bit much. Here are the common ones.

E-E-A-T

Stands for Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness — the framework Google uses to evaluate content quality. AI search engines look at similar signals when picking sources to cite.

The short version: AI checks whether you (or your site) have enough experience and expertise in this domain, whether others recognize your content, and whether your content is trustworthy. So if your site has clear author info, relevant credentials, and backlinks from other authoritative sites, your E-E-A-T score is higher.

Schema Markup

Already covered earlier — basically structured data added to HTML, usually in JSON-LD format, that machine-readably tells search engines and AI what your content is, who wrote it, when it was published, and so on.

Featured Snippets

That big box at the top of a Google results page that displays an answer directly. Pages with a Featured Snippet typically pull in more traffic than other ranked pages, and AEO strategy also boosts your odds of landing one — so doing AEO helps traditional SEO too.

AI Overviews

The AI-generated summary Google now shows at the top of search results. The frequency at which it shows up has been steadily increasing. AI Overviews stitches information from multiple sources, and when it appears, click-through rates on traditional search results drop significantly.

Zero-Click Search

The user gets the answer they wanted from the results page or AI's response, without clicking into any site. Around 70% of Google searches are zero-click today.

Topical Authority

The depth and breadth a site demonstrates on a particular topic, built up through topic clusters and consistent output of related content. AI prefers sites with deep coverage on specific topics.

Are there tools to track AEO performance?

If you're doing AEO, you probably want to track results. There are a few tools that help:

  • Semrush AI Toolkit: track your visibility in AI search and compare against competitors
  • Ahrefs Brand Radar: track brand mentions on AI platforms
  • HubSpot AEO Grader: free tool that grades how AEO-ready your content is
  • Google Search Console: not AEO-specific, but you can see impressions coming from AI Overviews

The most direct way is the manual one — go to ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini and search for your target keywords yourself, and see whether the AI's answer cites your content. It's the most low-tech method, but also the most direct.

AEO and SEO are complementary

One last thing: AEO isn't here to replace SEO — it's an extension of it.

The core strategies overlap heavily. Clear content structure, answering user questions, structured data, good page experience — all of these lift both sides. And AI search engines lean on the same signals traditional search cares about — domain authority, content quality, backlinks, etc. — so the stronger your SEO foundation, the better your AEO results tend to be.

The current advice is to keep doing SEO while folding AEO thinking into your content strategy. Per Gartner's projections, traditional search volume will drop significantly over the next few years, while generative AI search usage will keep climbing.

The earlier you start positioning, the better your odds of holding ground in the AI-search world.
