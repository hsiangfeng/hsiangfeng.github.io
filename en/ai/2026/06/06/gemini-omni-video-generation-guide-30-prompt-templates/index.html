How to Use Gemini Omni Video Generation: 30 Prompt Templates for Reels, Product Demos, and Logo Reveals

Intro

Honestly, I've been pretty skeptical of AI generation for a while. Fingers, on-screen text, audio — these are the details AI has always tripped on, and the output usually screams "yeah, this is AI" instead of looking like something actually shot on camera.

But I spent some time with Gemini Omni recently and... the improvement is real. The overall picture quality is pretty impressive. Some small details still aren't perfect, but it's hit that fuzzy zone between "this is clearly AI" and "this could be real footage" — especially this clip I generated the other day:

A bunch of friends saw it and were honestly thrown — they thought I'd actually shot a promo for "Code Review of My Youth" myself ( not happening ). So I'd say Gemini Omni has made some real, tangible progress on the "looks shot" front.

If you're not totally familiar with prompt structure or how to interact with an LLM, you can flip through my earlier post on LLM, RAG, Prompt — A Look at the Common GenAI Vocabulary first. This one will jump straight into Gemini Omni.

This post breaks into four parts: first clearing up a common mix-up (Gemini Omni in the Gemini App vs. Veo 3.1 on the developer side — not the same thing), then what makes Omni stronger than Veo, then how to actually write prompts that hold up, and finally 30 scenario-specific templates — IG Reels hooks, product demos, Logo Reveals, Cinematic B-roll, Before-After transitions, knowledge-deck animations, quote cards, and Lifestyle / travel / food cuts — all copy-paste ready.

First, let's clear this up: video in the Gemini App — is it Omni or Veo?

A lot of people ask "where did Veo go?" — and honestly the confusion makes sense. Early 2026 the video generation inside the Gemini App actually was Veo 3.1. But after the May I/O it all got swapped over.

In short:

  • Gemini Omni is the new unified multimodal model Google launched at I/O 2026 (May). It's now the main thing powering video generation inside the Gemini App. 10-second clips, 9:16 or 16:9, native synced audio, accepts a mix of text + image + video + audio as input.
  • Veo 3.1 / Veo 3.1 Lite didn't go away — they just got reassigned. They've been pushed to the developer side and are mainly available through the Gemini API, Vertex AI, and Google AI Studio, for engineers building products against the API or running large batch generations.

If I had to put it in everyday terms — Gemini Omni is more like a fully automatic camera: you tell it what to shoot and it handles the aperture and shutter itself. Veo 3.1 is more like a manual camera: more control, but you have to know what you're doing. For most people hopping into the Gemini App to generate a Reels clip, an IG short, or a Logo Reveal, Omni is enough.

What makes Gemini Omni strong?

For me personally, four things about Omni actually stand out: native synced audio, multimodal mixed input, Chinese text rendering finally being stable, and conversational editing. Walking through each one.

1. Native synced audio

The most painful thing about AI video used to be the audio, not the picture.

The picture would come out okay, but you'd have to hunt down voice / SFX / ambient separately on Pixabay or layer them in your editor afterwards.

Omni just swallows that part. You write things like "Dialogue: the female lead says 'we have to leave now'", "SFX: distant thunder", "Ambient: low murmur of a late-night café" into your prompt, and it generates all three audio layers synced to the picture. Lip-sync lines up, SFX timing hits the right beats (yeah, sometimes things slip slightly, but it's hard to tell now).

2. Multimodal mixed input

It's not just text descriptions.

You can hand it a photo and say "make this photo come alive — coffee steaming, someone walking past the window", and it'll use that photo as the first frame and extend forward. You can also hand it an existing video and say "same scene but switch to dusk lighting" — that's a style transfer. Image, audio, video, text can all be mixed in as input.

In my own case, I just handed Omni the cover of one of my books, and it dropped the cover straight into the video where the book was supposed to appear. That way I don't have to re-describe what the cover looks like in every prompt, and it's more accurate too.

3. Chinese text rendering is finally there

This is the same lineage as the leap Images 2.0 made on static images (if you haven't seen what Images 2.0 can do on static images, take a quick look at How to Use GPT-5.5 and ChatGPT Images 2.0: 30 Prompt Templates for IG Cards, Knowledge Decks, and Product Mockups).

Omni brings that same capability over to motion. Chinese subtitles, opening titles, logo text in videos all come out correctly. Occasionally a character will still slip, but it's hit the threshold of actually being usable.

4. Conversational editing

This is the one I personally find most useful. If a clip comes out wrong, you don't have to rewrite the whole prompt — you can just say "change the lighting to warm dusk colors, keep everything else" in the next turn, and it'll only touch that part. Really helpful for series content (same character, same style, different action) — you don't have to re-describe every detail each time.

For me, the difference this round isn't about "drawing more artistically" — it's about "looking like it was actually shot".

Post-production (pacing, captions, brand colors) still has to happen, but the "shooting" part has basically been eaten 80% by AI (probably?).

A universal structure for writing video prompts

Before you start copy-pasting templates, a quick note on the overall structure — otherwise you'll just swap topics blindly and the output quality keeps bouncing around.

After working through the official prompt guide and a fair bit of trial-and-error, for the prompt to hold up these six dimensions all need to be described:

DimensionWhat it coversExamples
Composition / cameraHow the shot is framed, how the camera moveswide shot, close-up, dolly forward, orbit, POV, handheld
StyleThe overall aesthetic directioncinematic, IG vlog, anime, Pixar, documentary, studio
LightingLight source, color temperature, qualitygolden hour, neon, natural window light, soft studio, backlight
SceneLocation and background objectswooden table, café, Taipei street, minimalist white background
ActionWhat the subject is doing and how it moveswalking past, turning, reaching for a cup, hitting the keyboard
On-screen textText that appears on screensubtitle content, position, entrance animation

On top of those six, video has two extra dimensions that static images don't — timing and audio. Those need their own slot:

ItemNotes
Timeline / TimestampFor multi-shot, use [00:00-00:03] to mark which segment happens when
AudioDialogue, SFX, Ambient — label all three layers separately

Note Two things are outside your prompt's control: aspect ratio (16:9 / 9:16) is selected in the Gemini App UI, and length is currently capped at 10 seconds by Google (their stated reason is a deliberate product decision, not a model limitation). So you won't see "please generate an X-second clip" in any of the 30 templates below. Pick the aspect ratio based on the scene — talking head and Reels-style go 9:16, cinematic and product demo go 16:9.

In short, don't let the model guess. The more specific you are, the more precisely it generates.

Note When you write Chinese dialogue, explicitly specify "clear Mandarin female voice (Taiwanese accent)" or "natural Mandarin male voice (Taiwanese)" in the prompt. Otherwise the voice occasionally drifts into an unintended accent or heavily English-leaning Mandarin — this is just a function of how the training data is distributed.

If you're editing an existing video (local change instead of full regen), add a "preserve" vs "change" pair to your prompt:

txt
Preserve: the original character, wardrobe, scene, composition.
Change: shift the lighting from midday to warm dusk orange, and add a female voice saying "alright, this is the place".

The logic of this structure is "which parts of this clip do I want untouched, which parts do I want changed" — that way the model only modifies what you asked for and leaves the rest as-is.

30 recommended prompt templates

30 prompts organized into eight categories, all copy-paste ready.

For each one I'll briefly explain the use case, then give the prompt body, and below that the video I actually got from running it. Swap whatever's in [ ] brackets for your own topic.

Note The example videos below were generated using the Chinese versions of these prompts (partly the point — to show off Omni's Chinese rendering). The English templates here produce the same layouts with English text. Mirror the field names ("Composition:", "Style:", etc.) when you write your own.

Note Video prompts work best in the 20–50 word range (or equivalent in Chinese) — too short and the model has nothing to go on, too long and the key points get diluted. I've tried to keep each template below in that range.

1. IG Reels / TikTok hooks

These are the clips that grab people in the first 2 seconds while they're scrolling Reels. The key is to throw the conflict, question, or contrast out immediately — don't waste time on setup.

1. Question-style hook (subject talking to camera)

The most direct opener — main character looking into the camera, asking a question that makes you want to keep watching.

txt
Composition: clinic-style close-up, front-on view of a 25-year-old Taiwanese woman sitting at a white desk with her arms crossed and a puzzled expression.
Style: realistic, IG Reels feel, natural-light look.
Lighting: soft window light from the front-left.
Scene: minimalist white background, a laptop on the desk.
Action: she looks straight at the camera, frowns, sighs, then says: 'Why does your code keep getting messier?'
On-screen text: in the last 1 second, bold Chinese subtitle pops in at the bottom — 'Swipe for the answer ↓'.
Audio: dialogue 'Why does your code keep getting messier?' (clear Mandarin female voice, Taiwanese accent), Ambient quiet office white noise.

2. Third-person narrator hook

No real face on screen — just hand-drawn or symbolic close-up with narration. Good for creators who don't want to show their face.

txt
Composition: top-down angle, close-up on a hand writing code on white paper at high speed.
Style: hand-drawn animation, stop-motion feel.
Lighting: a warm yellow desk lamp above the paper.
Scene: wooden desk, white paper, black coffee in the top right.
Action: the hand writes 'if, else, for, while' rapidly, then draws a huge question mark.
On-screen text: at 4 seconds, a bold headline fades in at the top — 'Why does code get messy?'
Audio: pencil-on-paper scratching, SFX page-flip (last second), warm lo-fi piano BGM.

3. Before / After two-second switch opener

Compress the contrast straight into the first two or three seconds — strong visual impact.

txt
Split into two halves.
[00:00-00:04] Composition: close-up on an ugly 1990s login page (rainbow background, Comic Sans, marquee headline), CRT scanline texture over everything.
[00:04-00:08] Composition: same position cuts to a modern minimalist login page (white background, serif title, clear hierarchy, light-blue CTA button), crisp and clean.
Style: UI design tutorial, high contrast.
Action: at exactly 4 seconds, glitch transition.
On-screen text: [00:00-00:04] top-left '2005', [00:04-00:08] top-left '2026'.
Audio: [00:00-00:04] old dial-up modem sound, [00:04-00:08] modern crisp UI tap sounds, SFX glitch transition.

4. Countdown opener

A countdown naturally creates "what's about to happen?" tension — great for listicle-style content.

txt
Composition: large bold number centered on a solid background, counting down from 5 to 1.

Lighting: flat, single light source.
Scene: pure black background.
Action: each second cuts to the next number, then after '1' the last beat switches to a large headline '5 Skills You Need to Learn in 2026'.
On-screen text: numbers in neon red, final headline in neon white; each number gets a slight zoom-in animation as it appears.
Audio: a deep drum hit each second, the last beat replaced by a clear bell, SFX subtle whoosh.

5. POV scenario hook

First-person POV pulls viewers straight into the scene — maximum immersion.

txt
Composition: first-person POV, looking down at your own hands typing on a laptop keyboard.

Practical tips

Knowing the structure and templates isn't quite enough — here are a few tips that have actually saved me time.

1. When Chinese fails (subtitles or accent)

If only a character or two in the subtitles slip, don't regenerate the whole thing. Just go back to the chat in the Gemini App and say...

txt
Please change only the subtitle at 02 seconds from 'XXX' to 'YYY', keep everything else.

It'll touch only that spot.

If the entire subtitle is garbled, it's almost always because the prompt didn't put the text in quotes — go back and wrap it in 「」 or 『』 and try again. Way more stable.

As for accent — Chinese dialogue occasionally drifts into an unintended accent or English-leaning Mandarin. Adding an explicit "clear Mandarin female voice (Taiwanese accent)" or "natural Mandarin male voice (Taiwanese)" to the prompt visibly improves the accuracy.

2. For seamless loop videos

For product mockups, logo backgrounds, SaaS hero motion — anything meant to loop endlessly — the prompt has to explicitly say "loops seamlessly" and "first and last frame should align". The action also has to be periodic (rotate once, cycle through a motion, etc.) — don't write something one-off like "a coffee cup gets knocked over".

3. Aspect ratio per platform

Different platforms want different aspect ratios. Remember to set this in the Gemini App UI before generating (it only offers 16:9 and 9:16 — anything else you'll have to crop after). Otherwise the output may not be directly usable. Common ratios:

Use caseRatio
IG Reels / TikTok / YouTube Shorts9:16 (portrait)
IG feed video1:1 (square) or 4:5 (portrait)
YouTube main video / ads16:9 (landscape)
Blog embed / OG animation preview16:9
Facebook feed1:1 or 16:9

You can see from this table that most short-form content is now 9:16 portrait. For Reels, set 9:16 in the App first; for YouTube main videos, set 16:9 — don't generate everything at 16:9 and then crop to portrait, the framing gets thrown off.

4. Don't cram too many actions into one shot

Honestly, 10 seconds is pretty limited.

If your prompt tries to do "walks through the door + sits down + opens laptop + starts typing + sips coffee" all at once, the model can't fit it in, and usually drops motion smoothness or object consistency at the end.

A safe ceiling is 1–2 main actions per clip. If you need more, split using timestamp and use multiple shots — or just generate two separate clips and cut them together.
