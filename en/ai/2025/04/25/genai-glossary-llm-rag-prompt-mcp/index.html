Heard of LLM, RAG, Prompt? A Quick Guide to Common Generative AI Terms

Intro

Generative AI isn't just engineer territory anymore — it's seeping into our daily lives. If you use ChatGPT, Grok, Midjourney, or similar tools, these technical terms will start showing up in your world. If you're an engineer, I'd highly recommend understanding them — future development will lean even more on these technologies to build better products.

What are these?

  • NLP
  • LLMs
  • GenAI
  • RAG
  • Prompt
  • Fine Tuning
  • Function Calling
  • MCP
  • AI Agent

These are all common terms in the generative AI space. They exist to help us describe and understand these things — but since they're technical terms, they can be a bit hard for regular people to grasp. This post gives a simple intro to each, so you can get a better feel for what they mean.

NLP

NLP (Natural Language Processing) is a technology that lets computers understand human language.

So in simple terms, NLP is…

"The ability for computers to understand human language."

Why bother with this? Because human language is really complex. For example:

"I'll take care of business for a sec!" (here "business" euphemistically means going to the bathroom)

"I'm having instant noodles."

"Mind if I take up a moment of your time?"

In each case, the same kind of word is being used in really different ways — even humans get confused sometimes. Imagine a computer that originally only handled program logic, with zero linguistic ability — how is it supposed to make sense of this?

So the core spirit of NLP is to first let the computer "understand" human language, then convert it into something the computer can process. Only then can the computer better understand what we want and do what we ask.

That's why NLP is the foundation of generative AI — without NLP, there's no LLMs, no RAG, none of it.

You're already using NLP without realizing it, in things like:

  • Google Translate
  • Siri
  • Customer service bots

These are all NLP-based applications, helping us interact with technology more effectively.

LLMs

Compared to NLP, LLMs are kind of the next level up. LLMs stands for Large Language Models — an advanced technology for letting computers understand language.

So how is LLMs different from NLP?

In short, NLP gives computers a basic ability to understand human language, but LLMs go further — by training on a massive amount of text and data, computers can answer more precisely and meet our needs.

Using a more everyday analogy: NLP is like learning the alphabet (or pinyin) at school — you understand the basic linguistic building blocks, plus some basic vocabulary.

LLMs are more like learning grammar and semantics — you can understand exactly what someone is trying to say without getting confused about which meaning of a word they're using.

So LLMs are trained on huge amounts of data to learn language structure and context, which boosts their ability to understand and generate text.

GenAI

GenAI is also an abbreviation. The full term is Generative AI. This term is more widely used and more often heard — pretty much everyone in this generation has heard of ChatGPT, and GenAI refers to the kind of content these large language models (LLMs) produce.

So what is GenAI? In short, it's based on LLMs combined with other technologies, letting computers generate the content we want — text, images, music, etc.

If you've used ChatGPT, you might have noticed that "generative" actually means it's not picking from a fixed set of pre-canned answers like traditional chatbots — it's "generating brand-new content from scratch."

Given current AI technology, we're more in the territory of guiding AI to "produce" the content we want, rather than letting AI generate content autonomously. If it could generate autonomously, that would mean AI has its own consciousness — and at that point we wouldn't need to guide it anymore.

Which is also the point at which humans should probably start worrying

RAG

RAG stands for Retrieval-Augmented Generation. Although LLMs are already incredibly powerful, they still have some limits — and the most obvious one is the data limit.

What does that mean? I mentioned earlier that LLMs are trained on huge amounts of data, but data is constantly being added and updated every single second. In that situation, LLMs have a problem:

"Stale data."

It's similar to how at school, the school always has a textbook for you to learn from, but those textbooks often can't keep up with the times and don't match what the industry actually needs. So you end up having to learn extra stuff on your own and Google for info to keep up.

Wait — that paragraph above already describes RAG. The "learning extra stuff and Googling for info" concept — RAG lets LLMs enhance generation by retrieving data, so when LLMs generate content they can apply the latest data, making the output more relevant to current needs.

The RAG concept is actually pretty simple. Without RAG, the flow looks like this:

  • The LLMs were trained on data from 2023
  • You ask the LLMs a question about 2025 data
  • The LLMs reply with 2023 data, or just say "I don't know"

But with RAG, the flow becomes:

  • The LLMs were trained on data from 2023
  • You ask the LLMs a question about 2025 data
  • The LLMs go retrieve the latest data (e.g., Google search)
  • The LLMs combine the retrieved data with their 2023 data
  • The LLMs reply with 2025 data

So RAG is essentially a data retrieval concept. It strengthens the LLM's natural generation ability further, solving the stale-data problem.

After all, training a new LLM costs a huge amount of time and money each time, so RAG can effectively reduce these costs and let developers focus more on creating and applying AI tech.

Prompt

Prompt is a really important concept. Some people call it "incantation", "spell", or similar — they're all talking about the same thing. But the Prompt is also extremely important, because it's the key to letting LLMs understand what we need.

If we use a more everyday example: imagine your parents asking you to do something. They have to tell you clearly what to do. Like if your parents want you to go to the supermarket to buy groceries:

"Go to the supermarket and buy some groceries for me."

You'd immediately think: "Buy what groceries? Which supermarket? When should I go?" And you'd have a hard time meeting what they actually wanted.

But if your parents instead said:

"At 3 PM, go to Aiguo Supermarket and buy a bunch of bok choy for me."

Now you know where to go, when to go, and what to buy — much easier to satisfy what they need.

That's the Prompt concept. The clearer and more detailed your Prompt, the better LLMs can understand what you need, and the better the content they can produce.

This is also why a new role called "Prompt Engineer" has emerged recently — how well a Prompt is written directly affects the LLM's output. Prompt Engineers are specialized in writing Prompts, helping LLMs better understand what we need.

On top of that, a new attack method has appeared in response to this — "Prompt Injection". This attack involves injecting malicious content into the Prompt, causing the LLM to produce content we didn't want users to see. This kind of attack can lead to wrong information or inappropriate responses, so to defend against Prompt Injection, developers usually limit the model's accessible instructions or filter user input.

If you're interested in Prompt Injection and want to try it out, check out this post: A fun little game — Gandalf Adventures Prompt Injection guide (article in Mandarin).

Fine Tuning

Fine-tuning means further training an already-trained LLM, which lets the LLM better fit our needs. The concept is similar to how teachers at school adjust their lessons to fit the students' level, so we can understand and learn better.

Fine-tuning is essentially a retraining process for LLMs. To use a more everyday analogy: imagine a college student who's already graduated, but they majored in three fields — Electrical Engineering, Information Management, and Foreign Languages. You want them to specialize in Electrical Engineering, so you specifically send them to take more EE courses, so they can deepen their EE knowledge.

This makes a student who already understands three fields even more proficient in EE — better at meeting our specific needs.

But heads up — using Fine-tuning generally costs about 50-60% more.

Fine-tuning suits mid-to-large companies or developers who want to build a model with "internal company language and style" — for example, a customer service model that specifically answers insurance policy questions.

Smaller applications, by contrast, might consider using "RAG + Prompt design" to achieve a similar effect at lower cost.

Function Calling

In short, Function Calling is the ability for LLMs to directly call functions. This lets LLMs invoke functions to fetch data while generating content, so they can meet our needs faster.

The basic idea is that we send our natural language to the LLM. The LLM analyzes it, decides "this is a question about the weather", and then calls the corresponding function to fetch the latest weather info.

This way we can directly call specific functions to fetch and return data, instead of relying on the LLM's internal logic to infer it. The LLM acts more as a middleman analyzing what the user needs.

Function Calling isn't about making LLMs "learn to write code" — it's about letting the model, based on your input sentence, automatically pick an API function to call and return the parameters in JSON format, which the backend then uses to execute logic or query databases.

AI Agent

AI Agent is a really interesting concept. What does it mean?

We just covered GenAI, right? But GenAI just generates the content we ask for based on our needs — it doesn't actually do things for us. So GenAI gives a result, and we still have to do things manually.

For example, if we ask ChatGPT a question about an Excel formula, ChatGPT can generate the formula, but we still have to manually type it into Excel for it to work.

But with an AI Agent, after we ask, it would automatically type the formula into Excel and execute it for us — getting results faster.

Hard to picture? Even simpler: you'd see your computer running programs by itself, kind of like someone is remote-controlling it. That's the AI Agent concept.

Behind the scenes, an AI Agent usually combines LLMs, tool calling (Function Calling), Memory, and Goal Planning modules to do the whole "understand task → break into steps → execute automatically" flow.

MCP

MCP stands for Model Context Protocol — an open standard proposed by Anthropic.

Its purpose is to let the LLM, during runtime, no longer rely solely on the input Prompt, but to dynamically query external data, invoke tools, and even interact with apps.

This protocol defines a universal format and ruleset, letting LLMs request external data or perform tasks in a consistent way.

For example, if you're building an AI assistant, with MCP it could:

  • Query the contents of a Notion document
  • Call a CRM API to fetch customer information
  • Trigger an automation flow to help you organize a spreadsheet

MCP isn't a specific platform — it's a communication protocol that lets AI models work with all kinds of tools as if they were USB devices. Plug in and use. This means AI applications go from being "Q&A" models to systems that can really interact with their environment.

Wrap up

Here's a quick recap table for the terms covered above:

TermMeaningKey point
NLPNatural Language ProcessingLets computers understand human language — Siri, translators, etc.
LLMsLarge Language ModelsTrained on tons of data — can deeply understand language and respond.
GenAIGenerative AIGenerates text, images, music, etc. from instructions, from scratch.
RAGRetrieval-Augmented GenerationThe model searches for fresh data before answering, avoiding stale knowledge.
PromptPromptTells the AI what you want — clearer = better answers.
Fine-tuningFine-tuningRetraining a model for a specific domain to boost expertise — but costs more.
Function CallingFunction CallingLets the model directly call APIs or functions to grab fresh data.
AI AgentAI AgentAuto-understands tasks, breaks into steps, and operates tools to get things done.
MCPModel Context ProtocolDefines a format for LLMs to dynamically grab data, use tools, and trigger flows.

This post was a quick, simple intro to some AI concepts and terms — not a deep dive into how they work or how to implement them.

Hope this gave you a better understanding of these tech terms!
