Intro to Generative AI and Machine Learning (1) — It's All Just Text Prediction

#LLM #Context Engineering #Generative AI #Hung-yi Lee #How ChatGPT Works #LLM Tutorial #AI Hallucination

Intro

AI has moved insanely fast over the past few years — to the point where even regular folks can get AI to help them do things. Vibe Coding is a great example. Hung-yi Lee at NTU is running a Generative AI and Machine Learning course in 2025, so this post is my notes and takeaways from the course.

What is Generative AI?

ChatGPT, Google Gemini, Claude — these are all examples of Generative AI in practice. These AIs produce output based on what you give them as input.

So how does Generative AI actually pull this off? The core technology behind it is:

“Large Language Models (LLMs)”

LLMs sound complicated, but the principle is actually simple — “text prediction” (literally “word chaining” in Chinese).

What’s text prediction?

If I want to ask “What’s the capital of Taiwan?”, I might type “The capital of Taiwan is”. The AI then uses what it learned to fill in the next character, then the next, then the next, eventually producing “The capital of Taiwan is Taipei”.

But during this whole process, we only gave the AI an opening prompt “The capital of Taiwan is”. The AI’s response actually unfolds like this:

The user never sees the intermediate steps or the [END] token — they only see the final result: “The capital of Taiwan is Taipei”.

Wait — isn’t this just a word chain game from when we were kids? Exactly. An LLM uses a huge amount of text data to learn how to “chain words”, and based on your prompt, it generates a plausible next token until forming a complete sentence.

It’s like if I say “nin-“ (忍), you might think of “ja” (者), “not” (不), “patient” (住) — those associations come from what you’ve learned in the past.

For the AI, it predicts the next character based on the surrounding context, which lets it produce coherent sentences.

The predicted next “piece” is called a “Token”. A Token can be a character, a word, or even a punctuation mark — the exact definition varies between models. In the examples above I marked each character with [] to indicate one Token for clarity.

Note
Both input and output consume Tokens. For example, if we input “The capital of Taiwan is” (or its Chinese version), those characters consume some number of Tokens. The AI’s response “Taipei” also consumes Tokens. Different models tokenize differently — some might treat “Taipei” as 1 Token, others as 3 Tokens, depending on the model design.

The thing we’re inputting is called the “Prompt” — the AI predicts the next token based on the Prompt.

So you might wonder — how does the AI know what to put next? Let’s talk about how it actually works under the hood.

How language models work under the hood

Here’s the fun part — how does a language model figure out what character should come next?

For example, let’s say we feed the model this Prompt:

Our brains intuitively fill in e, but how does the AI know to put e?

Internally, there’s a huge table that tracks “how many times in the training data did apple- get followed by e“ — and similarly for any other character that might follow.

The table might look like this:

Prefix Next character Occurrences Probability (Score)
I love eating appl e 5000 0.8
I love eating appl s 1000 0.16
I love eating appl y 500 0.04
I love eating appl … … 0.00…

So what a language model actually does is assign a probability (Score) to every possible next character based on these counts, then decide what comes next based on those probabilities.

So with this probability distribution, why does the model give a different response every time you ask the same question N times? Because the model performs “Sampling” on top of that distribution — it picks one randomly based on the probabilities. A casual way to think about it: it rolls dice, and characters with higher probability are more likely to come up.

Common sampling controls include: temperature, top-k, and top-p (nucleus). These let you tune the trade-off between “creativity” and “stability”.

That probability table has a name — it’s called the “Vocabulary”. The Vocabulary holds every token the model has learned, along with their continuation probabilities under different prefixes.

A language model’s vocabulary is typically tens of thousands to hundreds of thousands of tokens, using BPE/SentencePiece-style segmentation to cover multiple languages and symbols. What actually runs into the tens of billions is the model’s parameters — that’s why these are called “large” language models.

You might wonder — if it’s literally rolling dice, won’t the model occasionally output weird answers? Like, if I input “I love eating appl”, would the AI ever respond with “I love eating apply”? That would be strange.

In practice, the probability of that is super low, because “I love eating apple” appears in training data way more often than “I love eating apply”, so the model is overwhelmingly likely to respond with “apple”.

So at the core, a language model is just a “text prediction” system that learns how to predict from massive amounts of text data, then uses your prompt to predict the next token, eventually forming a complete sentence.

Does that mean the language model is dumb? Not really — quite the opposite. It has to understand human language knowledge — what’s a noun, what’s a verb, what’s an adjective, plus grammar rules, sentence structure, etc. — to produce coherent sentences.

But language knowledge alone isn’t enough. It also needs world/common-sense knowledge, otherwise it might use the right nouns and verbs but the content is wrong — like saying water boils at 90°C when it actually boils at 100°C. That’s lacking world knowledge.

Of the two, language knowledge is comparatively easy to learn — Google search, grammar tools, etc., all help with that. World/common-sense knowledge is harder, because it’s effectively infinite.

Like the course’s example: water boils at 100°C, but if you ask “at what pressure does water boil at 90°C?” — that requires more specialized physics knowledge.

You can also think of a language model as a function:

$f(x)=ax+b$

The unfinished sentence (the tokens) is the x, and the output probability distribution after passing through the model is f(x). The parameters a and b inside the function are the knowledge the model learned — and these parameters aren’t manually set, they’re “learned” automatically through training on massive amounts of data. That’s why a large language model has hundreds of billions to trillions of parameters.

Speaking of learning — what are the language model’s main learning sources? Three main ones:

  1. Internet data: Google, Wikipedia, ebooks, etc.
  2. Labeled data: datasets annotated by experts.
  3. User feedback: improvements from user interaction — the thumbs up/down on ChatGPT.

Through these three sources, language models keep getting smarter at predicting the next character and producing more coherent sentences.

The platform adds an “User asks:” prefix to your input, and a “AI answers:” prefix to its response. This is called a Chat Template. The template tells the language model “you should answer the question, not turn it back around”. The actual content fed to the model looks more like <system>...</system><user>...</user><assistant>...</assistant>.

So when you use any ChatGPT-like platform, your input is being quietly enriched behind the scenes, which is how the AI knows it should be answering questions.

How does a language model handle multi-turn conversations?

When using ChatGPT, you’ll notice it remembers what you said earlier in the conversation. How?

This is done through “Context”. When you have multi-turn conversations, the system includes the prior turns as part of the Prompt, so the AI can predict the next character based on the whole conversation history.

One thing to be aware of: each chat window can’t share memory with other chat windows. Each window has its own Context — so if you open two windows, the AI in one can’t remember what you said in the other.

From all of the above, you can see that what a language model actually does is take your prompt and produce a plausible next character until a full sentence forms. The whole thing is just “text prediction” — and that’s why language models can “hallucinate (make stuff up)” so easily, because they’re just predicting based on past data, not really understanding the question.

This making-stuff-up phenomenon is called “AI Hallucination”, and it has nothing to do with a backing database, because the language model itself doesn’t have a “database” — it’s just chaining tokens based on training data.

Note
Of course, you can use techniques like RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) to let the language model reference an external database when answering — but that’s a different topic, not covered here. Generative AIs like ChatGPT usually come with RAG built in by default, which is how they reduce hallucinations.

The classic AI hallucination example is “What’s today’s date?” Since the language model is basically locked in a small room without knowing what’s happening outside, when you ask the date, it can only randomly produce a plausible-looking date — which may or may not be correct. So it’s up to us humans to give the model the info it needs.

How do you solve this? Easy — add “Today is November 2, 2025” to the System Prompt, and the model can use that to answer your question. Giving the AI the full context it needs is called “Context Engineering”.

How does the model generate audio and images?

The concept is super similar to generating text. You give a Prompt:

The AI predicts using pixels (for images) or waveforms (for audio) instead of characters, eventually producing an image or sound — videos are just multiple images stitched together.

The AI generating songs or audio is actually super interesting — it uses “sample points”, which represent the amplitude of the sound wave at different time points. The AI predicts the next sample point based on the prior ones, eventually constructing the full waveform.

But generating images and audio is expensive in terms of Tokens (resource consumption). For a 1024×1024 image, you need roughly a million predictions to produce one image — what does a million predictions mean in concrete terms? Roughly the word count of Dream of the Red Chamber (a classic Chinese novel).

The AI first encodes the image — splitting it into 16×16 blocks and converting each into a Token. So one 16×16 might be labeled “grass”, another “sky”. When you prompt “a cute cat playing in the grass”, the AI predicts the next Token. So “grass” gets picked, then the next, and the next, until the full image is assembled.

And all of the above keeps coming back to one thing — “text prediction”. A language model is basically a really powerful text prediction system, learning from massive amounts of data and then, given your prompt, producing a plausible next token until a sentence is complete.
