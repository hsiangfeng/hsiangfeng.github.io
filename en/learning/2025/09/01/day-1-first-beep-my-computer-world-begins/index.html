The boot beep that started my engineering journey

It all started with a game

Wolfenstein 3D screenshot (from the web)

In my memory, there was this one time I went over to my uncle's place. He saw I was bored and led me into a small room — that's where I entered the mysterious world. (Back then, this kind of "mysterious world" was generally not something a kid my age should be touching.)

I remember a bunch of stuff on the table I'd never seen before. To my young eyes, it looked like:

  • A big square box with a black thing in the middle
  • A long box with lots of stacked layers
  • A flat, long board with lots of little squares
  • And... a mouse?????

My uncle walked over to the desk, sat down, and pressed a button on that long box.

"Beep!" The long box let out a short, crisp sound.

After the "beep", a light on the surface of the big box came on, and it started making a high-speed spinning sound. I genuinely thought it was about to explode.

"Uncle! It's going to explode!" I shouted nervously.

For pre-kindergarten me, that unfamiliar sound was a big shock. I had no idea this thing was just a "computer". I remember my uncle looked super proud, like he was about to flex his knowledge, and started introducing this thing to me.

"This (the long box) is called the computer case. This (the black glass box) is the monitor. This (the long white board) is the keyboard. And this (the mouse) is the mouse." He pointed at each thing as he explained.

Computer (source: Wiki)

Seeing this stuff for the first time was genuinely impressive. Even though boot times back then were 2–3 minutes minimum, I still thought it was super cool. Lines of unknown English text scrolling out felt like magic.

Just as I was about to ask what this computer could do, my uncle started typing on the keyboard, then said:

"You'll get to play a game in a minute!"

What?! You can play games on this?! I'd loved games since I was tiny — my dad would take me to his company to play game consoles, and I remember the famous "Nintendo Famicom".

Nintendo Famicom (source: Wiki)

When my uncle said games, I got hyped. After a bit, the screen showed something — incredibly vivid colors (for the time anyway — by today's standards...). The game was probably "The King of Fighters", since I have a vivid memory of the fighting scenes.

Once it fully loaded, my uncle taught me how to play. Without realizing, I ended up playing for 2+ hours. We both got kneel-punished by my aunt afterwards QQ (if there's a chance later, I'll go deeper).

Years later, I learned my uncle was typing commands in MS-DOS to load the game — not just smashing keys randomly. And game graphics back then were mostly 8-bit, which was already pretty advanced.

The tech we chased back then

To make this post easier to read, I'll lead with my own small stories, then explain the tech topics they bring up. Some are things I encountered while learning, things I solved, or just fun trivia. Section length will vary — some topics need more space, others wrap quickly.

For this story, I'll cover:

  1. Monitors (CRT)
  2. Game graphics (8-bit)
  3. MS-DOS

Ready? Let's go.

Monitors (CRT)

CRT monitor (source: web)

CRT stands for Cathode Ray Tube. Compared to modern slim displays, CRTs contained vacuum tubes and super thick glass, making them huge and incredibly heavy. Refresh rates were low, image quality was rough, and they easily caused eye strain and dryness — even nearsightedness over time. (Usage habits factored in too — too close, low lighting, bad posture.)

Note When refresh rate (Hz) is too low, frame delay increases and strains your eyes. By modern standards, refresh rate should be 60Hz+.

What's refresh rate (Hz)?

In short, it's how many times your screen updates per second. Higher = smoother. 60Hz means 60 updates per second — your screen "flickers" 60 times a second.

A lot of things in life have a refresh rate — TVs, phones, light bulbs. They're high enough that our eyes don't catch the flicker. Once the rate drops too low, our eyes catch it — that's "screen flicker", and it's visually uncomfortable. (Like a dying light bulb.)

Back to CRTs — how they display is actually pretty interesting (is it?). Inside a CRT is a color electron gun (circled in the image).

Electron gun (source: web)

This gun fires 3 electron beams — red (R), green (G), blue (B). The 3 beams hit phosphors on the screen, producing colors. So the colors you saw on a CRT were built from those 3 primary colors.

Eventually CRTs got replaced by LCDs.

LCD monitor (source: web)

LCD advantages vs CRT:

  • Slim: LCDs come in flat and curved variants. Some older ones are still thick, but overall LCDs are way thinner than CRTs.
  • Higher refresh rate: LCDs are typically 60Hz+ since they use liquid crystal tech, not electron guns.
  • Lower power consumption: LCDs don't need to fire electron beams, so they naturally consume less power.

Now you see why people moved on from CRTs.

Comparison table:

ItemCRTLCD
WeightHeavyLight
Eye strainHighLow
Underlying techElectron beam → phosphorLiquid crystal modulation

When buying a monitor today, how do you pick? Searching online you'll see "LED", "OLED", "QLED" etc. What's the difference? Quick explanation:

LED, OLED, QLED are all kinds of LCD-family displays:

  • LCD (Liquid Crystal Display): LCD itself doesn't emit light — it needs a backlight. Early LCDs used CCFL (Cold Cathode Fluorescent Lamp) or later LEDs as backlight.
  • LED (Light Emitting Diode): LED displays are LCDs that use LEDs as backlight, making them thinner, lighter, and lower-power.
  • OLED (Organic Light Emitting Diode): OLED pixels self-emit — no backlight needed. Better contrast, wider viewing angles, more color accuracy in low light (no backlight interference).
  • QLED (Quantum Dot Light Emitting Diode): QLED isn't self-emitting — it uses quantum-dot tech to enhance brightness, color gamut, and stability.

Confusing? Let me put LCD vs OLED in plainer terms:

LCD is like painting on a transparent canvas (the liquid crystal layer). It doesn't emit light, so you need a lamp (the backlight) behind it to see anything. That's LCD. OLED, on the other hand — each pixel is its own tiny light bulb that lights up on its own, like glow-in-the-dark stickers in the dark, no extra lamp needed.

"Is there a display that's all LEDs, not mixed with LCD?"

Yes — Micro LED. Currently common in big displays like video walls and digital signage.

Quick analogy of the four:

  • LCD: Old liquid crystal. Tube-backlit, thick, dim, low color quality.
  • LED: LCD upgrade — smaller, slim, low-power.
  • OLED: Self-emitting, super-thin, can be curved, deepest blacks, vivid color, expensive.
  • QLED: Advanced LED — high brightness, vivid color, long life.

Game graphics (8-bit)

What does "8-bit graphics" mean?

Bit is short for binary digit. 1 bit = 1 binary digit (0 or 1).

Due to hardware limits, early games could use at most 8 bits per pixel for color info — 8-bit color depth. 8 bits can encode 2^8 = 256 different colors. That's where "8-bit games" comes from, also called "pixel art".

8-bit game (source: web)

8-bit games created plenty of classics:

  • Super Mario
  • Contra
  • Battle City
  • Space Invaders
  • Raiden
  • Mega Man
  • Pokémon

And many more. Have you played any of these? Which ones stuck with you?

Personally I think the reason these games became iconic is that the developers had to overcome hardware limits, using limited colors and resolution to design graphics, then carefully thinking through gameplay, levels, and characters — to create fun, creatively challenging, replayable, timeless games.

You might ask:

"What about modern games? How many bits?"

Hardware has advanced, so games are no longer locked to 8-bit color. We're at 24-bit, 32-bit, even higher. That's why modern games look more refined and colorful — the color space is way bigger.

Quick math: 24-bit = 2^24 = 16777216 combinations. 32-bit = 2^32 = 4294967296 combinations. Far past 8-bit's 256.

Astronomical numbers are hard to feel, so here's an 8-bit vs 24-bit color comparison:

8-bit vs 24-bit color comparison

Now you can see the difference between 8-bit and 24-bit color — kind of like a 256-color colored pencil set vs a much bigger one.

MS-DOS

Honestly, I'd rather save this one for later, but MS-DOS is an ancient operating system. For now, you just need to know it was one of the kings of old computer systems, and you operated it entirely via text commands.

Quick-and-dirty intro — it's the command-prompt-style screen everyone's seen (Bash, CMD, PowerShell). All of those were designed based on the MS-DOS concept. You type pure text commands (dir, cd, copy, del, etc.) to operate the computer. This style was the norm pre-Windows.

MS-DOS (source: web)

With Windows 95, MS-DOS faded. "Faded" isn't quite right — Windows 95 actually bundled MS-DOS into the OS. Windows' graphical UI was a massive breakthrough at the time — users didn't have to memorize commands anymore, just click with a mouse. That's why Windows 95 was so popular.

(Mysterious voice: if memory serves, my first computer was Windows 95 too.)

Closing thoughts

Honestly, digging up these old stories is hard — it's like excavating long-buried memories and reorganizing them. Kind of fun (in a self-torturing way)?

While writing the tech bits, I was also reviewing them. Some I'd genuinely forgotten and had to dig through Wiki to re-confirm I had things right XD.
