【Day 4】Argh! Why Is the Screen Blue and White?! My BSoD and BIOS Memoir

Argh! Why is the screen blue and white?

Earlier I mentioned my dad brought home a retired company computer, and it broke down not long after I started playing on it. There’s actually a small follow-up to that — let me walk through it.

Note
“Lag” means your computer or network slows down and gets choppy. Common causes are low performance or network latency — basically, what you do and what shows up don’t sync. We’ll cover this more later.

The blue-and-white screen was full of dense English. I had no idea what was happening — did I mishandle something? Hit the wrong key? Watch something I shouldn’t have (x)?

I just remember the moment I saw it, “Argh!“ slipped out automatically.

When you hit a problem, the most direct response is force-shutdown and a couple of slaps to the case? Usually that resolves it. Unfortunately this time it didn’t — actually it got worse ಥ_ಥ.

“Ugh! Why is it back again!” — then more force-shutdown / restart cycles.

No matter how many times I repeated it, the boot screen kept showing blue and white.

For elementary-school me, I couldn’t read this at all — the English meant nothing to me. (My English was terrible, lol.)

After random key-mashing, I figured out that pressing Enter would auto-reboot, and after reboot Windows would come back — but I didn’t dig further. Eventually the computer couldn’t even boot, and I went back to internet cafés.

Lag

I gave a quick intro to Lag earlier. Let’s dig in — why does it happen? What causes it? How do you fix it?

So when does Lag happen? Things like…

“Ugh, the network’s lagging!”

“Messages are lagging!”

“The game’s lagging!”

“The video’s lagging!”

Basically anything can lag.

Let’s use online games as the example:

When you play shooters (CS) or tower defense / MOBA (League of Legends), you’ve probably hit: characters moving with a delay, shoot/skill button lag, enemies teleporting instantly. Worst case — the game cuts entirely and you get kicked from the server. All of that is “Lag”.

Why does this happen?

Because while your computer is processing the game’s frames, network or server delay puts your computer’s clock out of sync with the server’s. Hence the symptoms.

It could also be the other person using “cheats”, but we won’t go into cheats — off-topic.

Note
Cheats: third-party software / programs players use in-game to modify behavior or data, usually to cheat.

What can cause Lag?

  • Insufficient computer performance
  • Network latency
  • Server latency
  • Too many background processes
  • System or program issues

You might ask:

“Networks are fast now, devices are great, games are well-optimized — why does Lag still happen?”

Great question, but Lag causes are wild and nobody can pin it precisely. Common things to check:

  1. Check network status — use SpeedTest to test if your bandwidth is normal.
  2. Check server status — use the Ping command to measure latency from you to the server.
  3. Check if your device is overheating or under-spec.

Speed testing tools

A speed test, in plain terms, uploads a bunch of data to a server, measures round-trip time, and calculates your network speed.

How does SpeedTest work? Quick breakdown:

  1. Pick the closest server: based on your location and network, picks the lowest-latency server.
  2. Measure latency (Ping): sends a small packet to the server and measures round-trip time — that’s latency.
  3. Download speed: server sends multiple files, average becomes the download speed.
  4. Upload speed: you send multiple files to the server, average becomes the upload speed.
  5. Show results: ignores the initial server-selection time, then displays latency, download, upload.

That’s roughly how SpeedTest works — a quick way to gauge network speed.

Ping command (most basic network testing tool)

If you don’t want to use SpeedTest, you can use the Ping command to test latency.

Ping commands are covered in more detail in later chapters. For now, know that Ping is a command for testing network connectivity — it helps you understand the speed and stability of your connection to a specific site or IP.

If time is high, your latency is high — that affects gameplay and browsing.

Ideally time should be small. Under 100ms is generally considered normal latency~

Device overheating

Any electronic generates heat — and heat can cause Lag.

For PCs, as long as CPU temperature stays below a certain threshold, Lag is less likely. Emphasis on “less likely”, not “won’t happen”. There’s also another common cause — insufficient device performance (the device can’t handle the load) — but we’ll skip that today.

Back to CPU temp — what happens when CPU temperature crosses the threshold? The system enters “throttling mode“ — reduces CPU frequency to lower heat output. Without this mechanism, your CPU could literally fry meat.

Note
If you’re curious what dishes you can cook on a CPU, check out たれみみChannel.

What’s “throttling mode”?

All CPUs have set min and max frequencies. Too hot — frequency drops to lower heat. Cool enough — frequency rises to boost performance. Depends on the CPU design.

“What’s overclocking, then?” you might ask.

In short, overclocking pushes the CPU past its designed frequency for more performance — but heat goes up too. Enthusiasts add stronger cooling like water cooling or liquid nitrogen.

That’s the most basic Lag triage. There are more advanced angles, but we’ll stick to the basics today.

Blue screen of death

You’ve probably seen this screen at some point — maybe not exactly this one:

But maybe this one with a sad face :(

Honestly, starting with Windows 8 it became this blue background with a :( — it makes you want to slap the designer, especially mid-game or mid-work.

Back on topic — this blue-and-white screen is Windows’ message when a serious error occurs. The screen typically shows an error code and related info — useful for users or engineers to trace the root cause.

The blue-and-white screen has its own proper name: BSoD (Blue Screen of Death). Self-explanatory.

When you hit a BSoD, don’t curse — quickly grab your phone and snap a photo. The computer auto-reboots shortly. If you captured the screen, you have a chance to figure out why. (Depends on the person though XD.)

Typical info shown:

  • Error code
  • Error message
  • What to do next

Each Windows version has slightly different error codes, usually requiring extra info to analyze.

BIOS

Why bring up BIOS here?

Some BSoDs are caused by BIOS settings or BIOS itself, but that’s relatively complex. To not stray too far, I’ll do a simple intro to BIOS, and tie back to a thread from the previous post.

BIOS isn’t stored on your HDD / SSD — it’s on a small chip on the motherboard, usually called ROM (Read-Only Memory). So BIOS is read-only memory. Its main job is to provide basic I/O during boot and to run hardware self-checks, ensuring all components work properly. If hardware errors exist, it emits beep tones (if your computer has a beeper).

Different BIOS vendors emit different beep patterns. If you hear beeps, look up your BIOS vendor first and then the beep code meaning.

For AMI (American Megatrends Inc.), a RAM failure might be “1 long, 3 short“.

“Can I look at BIOS?” you might wonder.

Sure! But don’t poke around unless you know what you’re doing — wrong settings can prevent boot. Entering BIOS is easy — press a specific key during boot (commonly F2, F10, Delete, Esc — varies by motherboard vendor). The boot screen usually shows the key.

If your computer is auto-entering BIOS on boot, that means there’s an issue — best to consult a professional engineer.
