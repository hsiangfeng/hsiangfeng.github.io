【Day 2】Hooked on the First Login — Walking into the Web through Slime's First Home

Stumbling through my first internet experience

After my uncle let me touch a computer, the thing I looked forward to most every weekend was going to his place. Why weekends? Because on weekdays my family had work — only weekends had time to take me there.

“Ray, you want to go to uncle’s this weekend?” my family would ask.

“Yes!” I’d answer, hyped.

Go?! When have I ever not gone! I was basically being raised into computer addiction — how could I not want to go?

Note
“Computer / internet addiction” refers to excessive, hard-to-control use of the internet / computer that negatively affects schoolwork, relationships, and mental and physical health.

(Computer addiction isn’t a throwaway mention — in middle school, I genuinely got addicted to computer games and it affected my grades.)

One Tomb Sweeping Day weekend, after sweeping graves, it was pouring rain so we had to wait at a relative’s place. While waiting, I went upstairs to find a older cousin (about 10 years older than me) to hang out with…

Hold on! I know the moment you saw “older cousin……“ something weird probably popped into your head. Let me stop you before you keep imagining things!

After heading upstairs, this older cousin (since I forget which side of the family he was on, I’ll just call him “older cousin”) was using a computer. By now I knew: that metal box is the computer, the black glass is the monitor, the white-key board is the keyboard, and the mouse controls the computer.

“Older cousin! What are you playing?” I asked, walking up behind him.

In hindsight, this cousin had zero patience for kids — especially when I was machine-gunning questions…

“What are you playing?”

“What’s this?”

“What are you doing?”

“Is it fun?”

You read that right — in less than 30 seconds, this little brat was firing off questions nonstop. His face had reached peak grumpy mode.

“I’m not playing anything, I’m browsing the web. Stop bugging me!” Annoyed, not even looking at me, just kept using his computer.

You should know — once you respond to this brat, he just keeps going whether you engage or not.

“So what’s browsing the web?”

“What can you do online?”

“What’s that thing on the screen?”

This cousin, despite the annoyance, still explained a couple of things:

“I’m on a website called ‘Slime’s First Home’. It’s a place to download free software. Stop bugging me, I want to focus.” Then back to his stuff.

“The internet?”

“Download free software?”

“What’s free software?”

One reply, three follow-up questions. Eventually I pushed him to the point where he literally shut the computer off and walked downstairs. The line I remember most clearly:

“You’re so annoying! [expletive]!” he shouted, slammed the monitor off, shoved the chair back, and stormed downstairs.

But years later, this same cousin would come begging for tech help every time he hit a computer problem. That’s another story.

Single-player games

Single-player games — as the name says — are games you can play without internet. You only need a computer or game console (FC, etc.). The first MS-DOS game I touched, “King of Fighters”, was single-player.

Note
FC = Family Computer, Nintendo’s classic console. Also known as the “red and white” console because the casing was red and white.

You might ask:

“What if a game has a bug? Do single-player games not get bugs?”

They get bugs, but less often. Mechanics back then were simpler, so problems were rarer.

“What’s a bug?”

Bug is a programming term — an unexpected error during execution. Bugs can cause crashes, lost data, system instability, etc.

For games, examples: characters walking through walls (clipping past map edges), the game crashing at a specific spot or after a specific input, items disappearing, etc.

“What about fixing the bug?”

Back then, once a game shipped, that was that — no take-backs. Mechanics were simple enough that bugs were rare anyway (probably?)… Super Mario, Tetris, Contra, etc. Sometimes bugs even became “special techniques” or “secret moves” players exploited to clear levels faster.

Note
Some game “bugs” aren’t really bugs — they’re intentional mechanics left by developers for testing, the so-called “cheat codes”. Unlimited lives, money, items, etc.

“What about today? What happens when modern games hit a bug?”

Networking is everywhere now, so most “single-player” games include online connectivity. That means updates can fix bugs or improve the experience. The update process includes auth, ensuring you’re updating through legit channels — that’s why some people get banned for updating via shady channels.

Side note: fixing bugs is also called “debugging“ or “Debug“.

Going online

The older cousin from before… (lol)

The older cousin from before mentioned he was going online, browsing the web…

“What’s ‘going online’?”

It’s the act of browsing web pages through the internet. That action is “going online”.

“What’s a web page?”

A web page is built from HTML, CSS, JavaScript, etc. The browser parses it and renders it for you. A page can hold text, images, videos, music, etc.

Note
HTML / CSS / JavaScript are web dev’s three cornerstones:

  • HTML: structures the page (the skeleton)
  • CSS: styles the page (the look)
  • JavaScript: adds interactivity

Don’t worry about this — we’ll cover them later. For now, just know a web page is built from these three.

Back then, going online meant: looking up info, downloading software, watching videos, etc. — all done via a browser. The biggest, most famous browser at the time was IE (Internet Explorer) from Microsoft. IE was our main internet tool back then.

It’s six feet under now, QQ.

IE was a critical foundation for modern browsers. But honestly, IE’s market share dominance was largely due to it being preinstalled in Windows. At its peak, IE had 90%+ usage!

What browsers do we have today?

  • Google Chrome
  • Mozilla Firefox
  • Microsoft Edge
  • Safari
  • Opera
    … and more

Honestly, Google Chrome leads in share:

Curious — which browser do you use? Early on I was on Firefox, but as Firefox’s worldwide share dropped below 2%, I switched to Chrome. Firefox was getting close to the “2% rule” territory.

“Huh? 2% rule?”

The US government runs the “U.S. Web Design System“ (USWDS), which provides standards for government websites:

  • Accessibility
  • Responsive Design
  • Other design conventions (color, typography, layout, etc.)

USWDS has a “2% rule“ — when a browser’s usage drops below 2%, developers are allowed to stop supporting it. Below 2% basically means it’s almost in the grave XD. (Can move in next door to IE.)

Dial-up

Continuing the “going online” topic — back then we connected via dial-up.

“Dial-up? Never heard of it.” You might know it as “dial-up internet”. Same thing, different name. For clarity, I’ll stick with “dial-up”.

Back then, we used phone lines to dial into the ISP (Internet Service Provider), which connected us to the wider internet. At home, you needed a Modem (Modulator-Demodulator) to handle the dialing.

Note
A server is a dedicated computer system that provides network services. It can be physical or virtual. Websites you use, games you play — they’re all served by servers.

During dial-up, you’d hear that “beep beep beep” sound from the Modem — the process of converting digital signals to analog.

Most people probably haven’t heard it, so here’s a sample:

(Audio source: Wikipedia)

What’s wrong with dial-up?

Plenty — otherwise it wouldn’t have been phased out, right?

Main dial-up problems:

  • You have to dial in first (time spent dialing before going online)
  • It ties up the phone line (incoming calls cut the internet during dial-up)
  • Super slow (max 56Kbps)
  • Expensive (billed by time = long sessions = high bill)

In short: slow, expensive, painful, and unstable.

Vs broadband (ADSL, fiber, etc.) later on:

  • No dialing (just plug in and you’re online)
  • Doesn’t tie up the phone (talk and browse at the same time)
  • Fast (Mbps — hundreds of times faster than dial-up’s Kbps)
  • Reasonable pricing (flat monthly fee regardless of usage)

By every comparison, dial-up’s demise was inevitable.

Broadband still needs a Modem to connect to the ISP, but instead of phone lines it uses dedicated lines (ADSL / fiber / coaxial).

“Can’t computers read network signals directly? Why need a Modem at all?”

Because raw network signals aren’t 0s and 1s a computer can directly understand. The Modem’s job is modulate / demodulate — converting raw network signals into the digital 0s and 1s computers can process.

Today we’ve evolved:

Dial-up → Broadband (ADSL, VDSL) → Cable internet (Cable Modem) → Fiber (FTTH)

Each step delivered better, faster, more stable internet.

But we can’t fully escape the Modem — without it, raw network signals look like alien language to your computer. No communication.

Free software

The older cousin mentioned “Slime’s First Home“ — an ancient site mainly hosting free resources. Lots of people still use it (including me). If you’ve heard of or used it, you’re probably around my age.

Note
Since “Slime’s First Home” doesn’t serve HTTPS, you’ll see browser security warnings. That’s normal.

Quick aside — what’s free software?

Free software: software you can use without paying. Usually provides basic features, but often pushes ads or paid tiers to unlock the full feature set. That’s why some software offers itself free.

“Get them hooked first, then they’ll open their wallet” — you know how it goes.

When using free software, watch for licensing:

  • No commercial use
  • No redistribution
  • No modification
  • Etc.

License types vary:

  • MIT
  • GPL
  • Apache License
  • Etc.

Each has its own rules. Be careful when using free software.

Read, write

Every action a computer takes involves Read and Write:

  • Opening the browser
  • Launching a game
  • Watching a video
  • Etc.

When downloading, writing, saving files, etc. — all Read / Write.

Read / Write are also fragile, especially during writing. If interrupted, files can corrupt or fail to open.

Has this happened to you — yanking out a USB drive mid-write, only to find your file is corrupt or unopenable? Writing is a continuous process. Interrupt it and you get corruption.

Note
“Continuous process” is like writing a letter — if you tear the paper in half before finishing, sealing it, and mailing it, the recipient gets fragments that don’t make sense. That’s file corruption.

So during boot, computers are constantly doing massive Read / Write operations. Proper shutdown is about cleanly ending those operations to make sure files don’t corrupt mid-write.

For computers, “writing” a file is like writing and sealing a letter:

  1. Data segmentation (paragraphs)
  2. Packaging (envelope, recipient address)
  3. Storing at destination (mailbox)

Interrupt step 2 or 3 (power loss, USB removal, system error) and the file structure breaks.

File formats have strict “headers” and “footers”. Miss any chunk and the system can’t parse it — file becomes corrupt or unopenable~

Closing thoughts

This one got a bit long — lots of memories (lol).

Have any of you used dial-up too? Or was your first internet experience already broadband?

I used to hate it when someone called during my internet session, because it’d kill my connection immediately QQ.

Thankfully these days the only network risk is the cat chewing through the cable (sigh).
