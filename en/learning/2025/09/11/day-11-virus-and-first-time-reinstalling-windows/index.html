【Day 11】Virus! My First Time Reinstalling the System

Oh no, the computer’s infected

During my middle-school-to-high-school years, USB drives were the file-transfer hero. But because they kept getting plugged into different computers, they also became the ultimate “Gu jar“ (virus incubator). Especially at internet cafés — the variety of viruses on those computers was like opening blind boxes; you never knew what you’d catch next. Café owners tried to defend with tape over USB ports or removing front sockets, but the rear ports were still wide open — infection happened anyway.

Plenty of devices connect via USB — keyboard, mouse — so some cafés locked the entire tower in a cabinet and ran cables out for keyboard / mouse. That way users couldn’t plug stuff in.

But the café I frequented didn’t cage their towers. The whole machine was right there exposed. So we’d bring our own USBs, plug them in, and take the now-infected USB home, and then to school. The USB basically became a “virus delivery service“, shipping viruses from cafés to homes to schools.

“Wouldn’t the school computers block it?” you might ask.

Honestly… no.

It’s not that school computers had lax controls — it’s that homework had to be submitted via USB, so USB ports had to stay open. Result: school computers caught the same diseases as café computers. Speaking of which, reminds me of a story.

“Hey! XXX, you got Warcraft?” I asked the classmate across from me in computer class.

“Yeah, here, you want to play?” he handed me the USB.

“Yeah, let’s go!” I plugged the USB into the computer and copied Warcraft over to the school computer.

Side note — back then our computer-lab seats were assigned, so we could always tell whose computer was having issues.

After playing, I opened the browser, and something felt off…

The browser had become:

Normally school browser homepages were either:

  • School official site
  • Yahoo! homepage

But mine was now “hao123.com“. I didn’t think much at the time — closed it, reset homepage, kept going.

But then things kept feeling off. The computer started getting slow, browser kept opening to “hao123.com“. Then it clicked.

“Argh! My computer’s infected!” I said to the classmate across.

“Yours too?!” he stared at me.

Naturally, the teacher had walked over without us noticing. He saw both browsers were on hao123, and immediately told us to swap computers — then reported it to the IT center.

Why bring this up? Because one day my dad brought home a new computer, and I plugged that infected USB right into it. As you can predict, that computer got infected too.

Between the slowdown and the homepage hijack, I was forced to learn how to reinstall the OS.

Computer viruses

In short, a computer virus is a malicious program. The concept is like a flu virus — needs a host to embed in, then spreads from there to other computers.

The main goal of computer viruses is to break the system, steal data, or render the computer unusable.

“The most severe computer virus of recent times was ransomware. It encrypts all your files and demands ransom to unlock them. Particularly nasty because once you’re hit, files are locked and completely unusable.”

Infection vectors

  • File downloads: Inside executables (.exe) or installers (.msi). Some sites trick you into downloading “safe-looking” files that hide viruses.
  • Email: Viruses spread via attachments — masquerading as Word, Excel, or PDF docs. Open and you’re infected.
  • USB drives: Most common at school or cafés. Often via autorun.inf auto-execute, USBs become the perfect virus vehicle.
  • Web browsing: Early Flash and JavaScript vulnerabilities were easily exploited — viruses could slip in. This is why browsers phased out Flash.
  • Software vulnerabilities: Outdated systems or apps let hackers in via exploits — that’s why regular updates matter.
  • Social engineering: Attackers impersonate friends or coworkers and send files to trick you into opening them, spreading the virus.
  • Network sharing: P2P apps (BT, eMule) often pull in viruses alongside the file.
  • Public Wi-Fi: Different angle — hackers set up fake hotspots for “man-in-the-middle attacks“, stealing data or planting viruses.

Impact

  • Performance: Eats CPU, RAM, disk — slows the computer or even causes BSoD.
  • Data loss: Deletes files or, like ransomware, locks them.
  • Privacy leaks: Steals accounts, passwords, credit card numbers — used for scams or fraud.
  • Network security: Turns your computer into a “zombie” (botnet member) used by hackers for DDoS or spam. (Your computer becomes a tool for hackers to attack other computers or servers.)
  • Hardware damage: Excessive resource use → overheat → in severe cases, damages motherboard or GPU. (Rare.)

Virus types

  • Trojan horse: disguises as normal software, tricks the user into running it.
  • Worm: self-replicating, spreads rapidly across networks.
  • Ransomware: encrypts files and demands ransom.
  • Adware: forces ads to pop up.
  • Spyware: steals user private data.
  • Rootkit: hides itself, gains highest system privileges.

Prevention

  • Regularly update system and software: the most basic defense. Many viruses exploit system vulnerabilities — updates reduce that risk.
  • Install antivirus: real-time detect and clean. Basic protection.
  • Don’t randomly download files / from unauthorized sites: exploits the user’s “cheap-shortcut“ mentality. Cracks and free software often hide viruses — be careful with sources.
  • Enable firewall: monitors network traffic, blocks unknown connections.
  • Don’t randomly plug in USBs: don’t plug in unknown drives — they may carry viruses.

But no matter how careful, accidents happen. So regularly back up to prevent data loss.

Note
Backup methods include local backup, off-site backup, cloud backup. Pick based on your needs.

Reinstalling the system

Reinstalling the system means “wiping the computer and starting fresh“.

But “starting fresh” doesn’t mean grabbing a knife and stabbing the computer…

It means deleting all data and the OS, then reinstalling the OS and software. Basically restoring to “factory settings“. If that’s still hard to grasp, think of it as “formatting the hard drive“.

How do we reinstall? Before starting:

  1. Back up important data: the most important step. Reinstall wipes everything, so save important docs, photos, etc.
  2. Prepare install media: prep a USB drive or DVD, and put the OS image file (ISO) on it.
  3. Get the OS: for Windows, download the ISO from the official site, then make a bootable USB or burn to DVD.
  4. Configure BIOS: whichever install media you pick, enter BIOS and set USB or DVD as first boot device. Common BIOS keys: F2, Del, Esc at boot.
  5. Start install: once the above is done, boot from install media, you’ll enter the install screen. Follow the steps to complete OS install.
  6. Install drivers: older OSes needed manual driver installation; modern OSes auto-bundle common drivers and boot up fine. For unsupported hardware, manually download and install.

That’s the OS reinstall flow, though specifics vary by OS — Linux, macOS, etc.

Reinstall benefits:

  • Clear viruses: can effectively remove most viruses. But ransomware-encrypted files can’t be recovered even by reinstalling.
  • Boost performance: Windows slows over time due to install/uninstall residue. Reinstall returns it to initial state, performance improves.
  • Fix system issues: BSoD, can’t boot — usually system file corruption. Reinstall typically resolves it.
  • Update system: reinstall typically upgrades to the latest version, with new features and security updates.

Closing thoughts

Have you ever reinstalled your computer?

I reinstall mine about every two years. Windows accumulates junk files over time, and cleaning them is a pain, so periodic reinstall keeps things tidy.
