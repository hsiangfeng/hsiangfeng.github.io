Submitted your homework yet? Hurry up and burn it to a CD!

Back in school, teachers always assigned homework — computer class was no exception. One assignment was to "burn" files to a CD and bring them to school to submit.

First time hearing it, I was confused — burn a CD? How? With actual fire? (Lol.) Later I learned it meant writing files to a CD — that process is called burning.

"Today's homework is to finish a movie reaction essay and burn it to a CD. Hand it in next week!" the teacher said before the bell. That class the teacher played a movie. Why we watched it and which movie it was — I've totally forgotten.

"Burn a CD over fire? No way..." my brain drew this picture instantly — putting the CD over a flame.

Burning CD over fire

"Hey! XXX, how do you burn a CD?" Nothing about my mental picture felt right, so I turned to the classmate behind me.

"Doesn't your computer have a burner? You have a computer, don't you?" he stared at me.

"Yeah, but I don't know what a burner is. Also mine broke," I answered.

"Broken? Or how about you email me the assignment when you're done and I'll burn it for you?" he offered.

"Sure!" Free labor — perfect.

But I got pranked badly. I thought he'd burned the assignment to a CD for me, but he made me hand in a blank CD!!!

History of optical discs

Optical disc history is actually pretty long, going back to laser optical storage research in the mid-1960s. But it wasn't until 1982 that Sony and Philips commercialized it, initially as a music storage medium. Some confuse it with vinyl records, but they're fundamentally different — optical discs are digital, read by laser.

"What's the difference between optical discs and vinyl?" you might ask.

Vinyl is analog audio storage — a needle reads physical grooves on the record surface. Optical discs are digital — data stored as binary (0s and 1s) via pits and lands on the disc surface, read by laser.

Note

Optical discs really do have pits — invisible to the naked eye, measured in micrometers, very, very small.

So while both were major music media, the underlying tech and user experience are completely different.

Over time, optical discs evolved from music storage to general data storage — that's why we have CD-ROM, CD-R, CD-RW formats. The disc wasn't originally designed for data.

The main formats:

  • CD-ROM (Compact Disc Read-Only Memory): content pre-written at the factory, not modifiable, read-only.
  • CD-R (Compact Disc Recordable): user can burn with a writer once, but can't delete or modify.
  • CD-RW (Compact Disc ReWritable): can be written, deleted, modified multiple times — reusable.

Some might wonder: if CD-ROM and CD-R are both "read-only", why have two formats?

Reason: CD-ROM has data pressed at the factory via physical pits/lands, similar to vinyl pressing — so it can't be modified. CD-R is a blank disc with an organic dye layer underneath. The burner uses laser to "burn" data into the dye, changing reflectivity to simulate pits/lands — that's why CD-R is one-write-only.

A CD holds about 700MB — sizable at the time. 20GB hard drives were considered big back then.

Now let's talk briefly about "burning".

The principle: the burner uses laser to "etch" data onto the disc. The process resembles burn ablation — hence the English "burn a disc", translated to "燒錄" in Chinese.

Back then, having a CD burner at home was a flex. Burner specs often showed "16X" and "supports CD-RW" — meaning burn speed and supported disc formats.

16X meant 16x standard speed — burning a minute's worth of data at 16x — solid spec back then.

But just having a burner isn't enough — you also need burning software to write data to the disc.

Common burning software at the time:

  • Nero
  • Alcohol 120%

The most-used was Alcohol — nicknamed "酒精" (alcohol) in Mandarin.

Did burns sometimes fail? Yes, in many ways:

  • Burner ejected mid-burn — disc ruined.
  • Bad disc quality — finished burn unreadable, often due to disc coating quality.

To avoid failures, pick higher-quality discs and burn at lower speeds. Effective for reducing failure rate.

As time passed, more disc formats emerged:

  • VCD: Video CD — for video storage.
  • DVD: Digital Versatile Disc — for HD video.
  • Blu-ray: Blu-ray Disc — for super-HD video.

These are mainly for video, so later disc classifications split into "data discs" and "video discs".

Later, with USB drives and cloud services becoming common, optical disc usage dropped. Discs also had environmental issues. The arrival of DVD and Blu-ray brought a temporary revival, but ultimately couldn't compete with USB and cloud.

MB and GB

I've been throwing around MB and GB, so let's quickly talk about computer storage units.

A CD held about 700MB. A DVD held 4.7GB. Big at the time. By today's standards, a 4K movie often needs 10GB — can't fit on one DVD. Even if it did, you'd need two.

Ever had this: watching a movie, the TV pops up "Please change disc" — yeah, that was the experience. Brutal.

Computer storage units are based on the Byte. Other than 1 Byte = 8 bits, everything else uses 1024 as base:

  • 1 Byte = 8 bits
  • 1 KB = 1024 Bytes
  • 1 MB = 1024 KB
  • 1 GB = 1024 MB
  • 1 TB = 1024 GB
  • 1 PB = 1024 TB
  • 1 EB = 1024 PB

Classic exam questions: "1 GB = how many MB?", "1 TB = how many GB?", or "4.7 GB = how many MB?"

For 4.7 GB:

Text
4.7 GB = 4.7 × 1024 MB = 4812.8 MB

Above is the correct conversion, but you've probably hit this: you buy an 8GB USB drive, plug it in, and only see ~7.45GB. Reason: computers use base 1024, but manufacturers usually label using base 1000.

Not the manufacturer skimping — just a calculation difference.

Closing thoughts

When you used to buy games, did you buy them on disc?

Early on, every online game update meant a trip to 7-11 to buy their update disc and install it. Eventually my house was piled with one-use discs — once updated, they never got used again XD.
