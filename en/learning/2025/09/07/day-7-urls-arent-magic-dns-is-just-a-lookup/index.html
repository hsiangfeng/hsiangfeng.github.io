Did you Google today?

“Everyone, starting today let’s try using Google to search for info!” the teacher said in front of the class.

Actually, when I first started using computers, I didn’t use Google — I used Yahoo! Kimo Search Engine. Yahoo! was one of the most-used search engines in Taiwan back then.

“What’s Google?” I thought.

“Open your browser, type G…o…o…g…l…e…, then .c…o…m, and press Enter!” the teacher said.

I still remember Google’s homepage being super minimalist and clean — you couldn’t tell what services they offered.

Compared to Yahoo! Kimo’s site at the time, Google felt way less impressive.

So I still mostly used Yahoo back then. So when did I switch to Google?

I think it was around middle school. My classmates started using Gmail one by one. To keep up, I signed up for Gmail too, and gradually got used to Google Search.

The teacher had said something memorable:

“Google will probably become the main search engine for everyone in the future, so you really should learn how to use it.”

That teacher’s foresight was sharp. If I’d had money to buy stocks back then, I’d be rich now (lol).

What’s a Domain?

Let me use a real-life example to make the abstract Domain concept easier.

Imagine you’re mailing something or trying to find a place. You’d usually describe it like:

“123 Songshan Rd, Xinyi District, Taipei City”

That’s a familiar address — easy to remember, easy to describe.

But in reality, that address gets converted into specific coordinates for map services, like:

“25.04740930776882, 121.57805454648769”

Those coordinates pin the location precisely, but can you remember them? I definitely can’t. Our brains prefer meaningful info over long meaningless numbers.

Same with the internet. Take Google — we know its Domain is google.com, but do you know its IP?

From this, we can see what a good domain has:

  • Easy to remember
  • Related to the brand or service
  • Short and easy to type

Classic examples: Google, Facebook, YouTube. Good domains matter for brand-building and marketing, which is also why quality domains are hard to get and pricey — sometimes thousands or tens of thousands of dollars a year. But for brands, the image and marketing value is usually worth it.

Because of this, domain names follow “first come, first served“. If the domain you want is already taken, you can’t reuse it — pick another or try negotiating with the current owner.

This setup led to a particular business behavior: “Domain Squatters“ (also called “Cybersquatters”, which I’ll just call domain squatters below).

Domain squatters register large numbers of domain names, then resell them at high prices to people who actually need them. They typically grab “popular or easily confused“ domains, sit on them, or use them for parking-page ads, waiting for someone to pay up.

“Aren’t squatters illegal?” you might ask.

Answer: not illegal, because domain names themselves don’t have copyright or trademark protection. As long as you can pay, you can register any domain you want.

A side note on buying — domains are bought through “Domain Registrars“, e.g.:

  • GoDaddy
  • Namecheap
  • Google Domains (ended in 2023, migrated to Squarespace)

These registrars apply for domain names through ICANN (Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers) and resell them to end users. Note: buying a domain doesn’t give you ownership — it gives you usage rights, usually measured in years.

A domain can be registered for up to 10 years at a time, with continuous renewal allowed — so as long as you keep renewing, you can keep the domain. If you don’t renew, the domain gets released and someone else can re-register it. That’s when domain squatters might grab it and resell to the original user at a high price.

If you’re considering buying your own domain, here are quick pointers:

Item Notes
Purchase channel Domains must be bought through Domain Registrars, e.g., Namecheap, GoDaddy, Google Domains.
Usage rights Domains aren’t one-off purchases — usage is per year.
Max registration period Up to 10 years at a time; renewal required after.
Renewal flow If not renewed: Grace Period → Redemption Period → finally released for others to buy.
Renewal pricing Watch the pricing model — sometimes first year is cheap and renewal is expensive (e.g., $10 first year, $20+ renewal).
Domain quality A good domain: memorable, brand/service-related, short, easy to type, hard to misspell.
Buying registered domains If your target domain is registered, negotiate with the owner — usually via Domain Marketplaces, typically at higher prices.
Top-Level Domain (TLD) The ending part of a domain, e.g., .com, .tw, .org. Different TLDs have very different pricing, renewal, and availability.
Privacy protection When buying, add WHOIS privacy protection to avoid your personal info (name, phone, email) being public.
DNS management After registration, you need to set DNS records (A, CNAME, MX, TXT…) to make the site and email work.

Let me single out “WHOIS privacy protection“ — it’s really important for personal data protection.

Before buying, confirm the registrar offers WHOIS privacy protection. This service hides your personal info from the public WHOIS database. Without it, your name, phone, email, etc. are public — anyone can look them up. Very unsafe for personal privacy.

“Why does WHOIS expose personal info? What gets exposed?” Probably what you’re wondering.

Short answer: this comes from the WHOIS system’s design. ICANN (Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers) explicitly requires registrars to provide registrant info, for transparency and abuse prevention (scams, spam).

Note
Some regions, due to GDPR, may auto-hide WHOIS data (e.g., .eu domains), but it doesn’t apply to all TLDs — .com, .tw, etc. may still expose personal info.

What’s DNS?

Last note — earlier I mentioned:

“Your computer’s DNS server is usually your home router’s IP.”

You might have questions:

“What’s DNS?”

“My home has DNS?!”

Yes — you use it every day, you just don’t know it. But this isn’t a real DNS server.

DNS (Domain Name System) is like the internet’s phone book — it converts human-friendly URLs (like Google.com) into the IPs computers understand. Critical infrastructure for the whole internet.

Without DNS, you’d have to use long number strings to visit sites — like having to use coordinates to order delivery.

Your home router isn’t a real DNS server — it’s a DNS relay.

When you type a Domain into the browser and submit, your router sends the Domain to a real DNS server. The DNS server returns the IP to the router, and the router returns the IP to your computer.

This is called “DNS forwarding“. With this design, all devices at home (computers, phones, tablets) only need to query the router — no per-device DNS setup — making browsing faster.

This design also lets the router cache DNS, avoiding repeated queries to the DNS server, speeding things up.
