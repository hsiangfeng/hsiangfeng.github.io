How I Became the 2023 Node.js Software Engineer Enterprise Capstone Program Coach

#Node.js #Teaching

Intro

My company’s half-year Node.js Software Engineer Enterprise Capstone Program just wrapped up, so this post records the six-month process and my takeaways. Hopefully it helps future students of this program understand what it’s about. Also, this is for my students.

About this program

Warning:
Before you read further, I want you to know — this post doesn’t represent “HexSchool”‘s position. This is my personal position and personal sharing.

This is HexSchool’s first advanced-tier program. It’s significantly harder than past programs, QQ.

But you’ll get to experience:

  • Build a project in half a year
  • Plan and design a project from scratch
  • Pick up new tech in a short time
  • Remote collaboration with strangers

These are guaranteed experiences. At the end, you also present your capstone — but not to HexSchool staff. You’re presenting to external partner companies we invite. So this isn’t just a teaching program; it’s an enterprise capstone, and you really experience a project from zero to one.

This program is more suited to people with some work experience or a solid foundation. If you’re a beginner, I personally don’t recommend it — you’ll be in a lot of pain.

Who am I

Quick intro about me so people aren’t lost, QQ…

I’m Ray
I’m a frontend engineer at HexSchool, also a TA and instructor,
and now also a coach for the Node.js Software Engineer Enterprise Capstone Program (another slash role, Orz)

This capstone program is the first time three coaches (Liao, Casper, and me) co-led students through capstone. So cost-wise, it’s actually pretty expensive.

The program had about 130 students total, split into 25 groups (5–6 people each). The three coaches split into 7–9 groups each.

For me, I covered 9 groups. Each group had a different topic. What does that mean?

Students choose from these topics:

  • Productivity tool (Trello, Jira)
  • Info aggregation site (Interview-fun, Salary-comparison)
  • Digital signature system (KDan Mobile Tech)
  • Job board
  • Online learning platform
  • Event ticketing service
  • POS system
  • Crowdfunding site
  • Blog platform
  • URL shortener

The groups I led, by topic:

  • North Group 1, topic: Info aggregation site
  • North Group 3, topic: Project-bidding system (similar to info aggregation)
  • North Group 7, topic: Online learning platform
  • North Group 9, topic: Productivity tool
  • North Group 11, topic: POS system
  • North Group 13, topic: Productivity tool
  • North Group 15, topic: Productivity tool
  • North Group 17, topic: Crowdfunding site
  • South Group 1, topic: POS system

You’re probably curious what a coach actually does in this program. I’ll cover that later.

The program’s process

How does the program flow? We planned seven milestones:

  • Milestone 1: Topic confirmation
  • Milestone 2: Project finalization
  • Milestone 3: Tech upskill
  • Milestone 4: API spec
  • Milestone 5: Environment setup
  • Milestone 6: Role-side implementation
  • Milestone 7: Presentation phase

Students must pass the coach’s review before proceeding to the next milestone. The review ensures their progress meets our expected goal. If not, we give advice to help them push the milestone forward.

Each milestone has an owner. We hope each milestone has a different owner. Why? Because we want every team member to experience project management at some level, which gives everyone more growth. The process also trains communication skills heavily.

Let me briefly describe each milestone’s content so you have a general sense.

Milestone 1: Topic confirmation

Before this milestone, we help with grouping, so students fill out a survey. Please please please don’t fill it out carelessly, because this hugely affects your group assignment and teammates. Don’t say “no work experience” if you have it 🫴🫴🫴

After students are grouped, HexSchool hosts the first offline discussion meeting. We hope offline gives people who don’t know each other a chance to meet face-to-face and break the ice, and to discuss with teammates which topic interests them. (Also my first time meeting my students.)

In this milestone, besides “confirming the topic”, you also write user stories. User stories sound simple, and you’re all experienced engineers — writing user stories should be easy, right? Right?!

But in practice, some groups got stuck here for a while. Why?

Even experienced engineers don’t necessarily have experience planning a project end-to-end. So plenty of groups got stuck here.

Common situations:

  • Don’t know how to write user stories
  • Requirements too big or unclear
  • No role separation

So the first few online progress meetings were about clarifying requirements and rewriting user stories. I’d ask students to reference existing systems for their user stories, then cut some requirements to avoid the project growing too big. This is part of what we help with (cutting / clarifying requirements).

My stance is to preserve as many of the students’ wanted features as possible. So if a student has features they want to keep, I’ll turn them into “wish list” items and let them ship core features first. That ensures the project actually delivers (the MVP approach).

Side note: at the end of the offline meeting, half of my groups had picked POS systems. But by the first online progress meeting, almost everyone had switched topics XD.

(The first online progress meeting was on my birthday XD)

Milestone 2: Project finalization

This milestone is where every group draws their own wireframes. We initially asked them to use whimsical, but later found whimsical isn’t great for wireframes because of free-tier limits — students would get blocked mid-drawing, QQ.

So we switched to Miro, which I personally think is way better than whimsical.

Most participants are engineers (some have design backgrounds), so drawing wireframes was unfamiliar. When stuck, I’d ask them to reference existing services. Existing services are great reference templates, and pairing them with the earlier user stories helps avoid getting stuck.

Did the milestone end there? No.

Besides wireframes, an external designer arranged by HexSchool also gets involved. Each group gets one designer, and the earlier you finish wireframes, the earlier you get the design mockups. So this milestone is very important.

But! Wireframes have constraints. Basically you can only hand 5 pages to the designer to mock (design fees aren’t cheap), so students have to analyze which 5 pages are the most important core pages and submit to the designer after discussing with the coach. The coach also provides feedback on those 5 wireframes, and the designer offers wireframe suggestions to make the design more complete.

Oh! Almost forgot — this milestone also requires flowcharts and swimlane diagrams to verify the code’s logic and flow are correct.

Milestone 3: Tech upskill

This milestone looks chill because the main goal is for students to pick the tech they want to use for this capstone. We give input on their tech choices.

You might think…

“What about students who want to learn everything?”

No worries. To prevent “I want to learn everything”, we use a point-based concept for self-analysis. 1 point = 4 hours. Each person’s total points cannot exceed 15 points — i.e., 4 * 15 = 60 hours (15 days). For any single tech, the max per person is 10 points. This prevents students from listing too many techs and failing to ship.

Why is 1 point = 4 hours? Because we assume every student is a working engineer, and they probably have about 4 hours of usable time after work. So 1 point = 4 hours.

What if a tech’s points exceed 10 per person? Example reference:

Please ignore that NextJS got typed as NestJS (lol).

You can see Ray’s column estimates 10 points for “Vue3 or React or Angular environment” — that’s at the ceiling. If only Ray is researching, I’d reject this as the coach. But if other members are co-researching (e.g., Liao + Casper + me), then points become 10/3 = 3.333…, which doesn’t exceed the cap. Not the prettiest workaround, but it lets students know tech research can be shared — and avoids over-divergence.

This milestone is hard to balance, because everyone’s circumstances and learning ability differ. It’s hard to know in advance whether the student will actually use this tech in the capstone — a real risk. So as a coach, I ask students to think carefully and provide opinions. My stance is to respect what each student wants to learn, but if they list too many techs or techs unrelated to the capstone, I’ll reject them.

This milestone also asks teammates to discuss whether they want frontend or backend ownership

This milestone might feel chill, but it’s chill for a reason — milestones 1 and 2 are extremely brain-burning, so milestone 3 is breathing room. Because the next milestone is the start of the real pain.

Milestone 4: API spec

Now we’re getting close to dev. Before dev, each group plans their API doc. Based on their wireframes and following RESTful API conventions, they write out API specs.

(That’s just a screenshot snippet — many students opened 50–80 endpoints.)

Simultaneously, groups that submitted earlier and passed milestone 2 start receiving design mockup deliveries during milestone 4. Final mockups land during milestone 5, so this stage also involves a lot of designer-mockup adjustment communication.

I try to help and clarify whether students are missing or over-opening APIs, but I don’t review the API content. Why?

Because during dev, students will keep adjusting. If I intervene, students might over-anchor on what I said initially and be afraid to adjust. So I avoid touching API content. I want students to figure out a mutually agreeable API spec — that trains their communication skills.

Milestone 5: Environment setup

This milestone is genuinely simple. Deploy the environment, open the routes, implement signup and login APIs — basically you pass.

The goal: get students into a “build mode” mindset. Like a ritual. Lots of prior time was discussion and planning, so if students don’t enter dev mode here, fatigue starts kicking in. That’s why we plan this milestone — to get students into dev mode.

By this point, almost two months have passed.

Milestone 6: Role-side implementation

This is the milestone every engineer is excited about — the dev phase!!!!!

After entering dev, things should be smooth, right? You’re all engineers — dev should be no problem, right?

If you think that, you’re very wrong!

This milestone is when students realize dev isn’t as easy as imagined XD.

So our baseline pass criteria for students is to focus on ONE role first — e.g., for a crowdfunding site, finish the proposer role first, then do the second role. Avoid implementing multiple roles in parallel.

To accelerate dev, we sometimes tell students they can use stub data (just static, no APIs) to speed things up and enrich the screens. But ideally I’d rather not do that, because it makes format adjustments harder later. Only when time is super tight.

Milestone 7: Presentation phase

This phase is basically… making slides. Yep, slides.

As a coach, I look at how students’ slides are written, how they present, whether the content is engaging. So this milestone is important — they’re presenting to external partners. We also ask students to fill the project with simulated data to add realism.

Passing this stage means you’ve completed the results event, the final milestone of our program 👏👏👏👏

About the coach

I’ve talked a lot about milestones — what does the coach actually do? Let me tell you.

Every Monday night 9:00 PM – 12:00 AM, I do online progress check-ins with students. Each group gets 20 minutes. I try to control time, but occasionally things spill past 1 AM. When that happens, I’ll ask later groups to switch to the next night, so it doesn’t impact students who have work the next day.

In check-ins, beyond reporting milestone progress, I sometimes help clarify dev or requirement issues. Each group only has 20 minutes, so if it’d eat too much time, I ask students to use Discord for asynchronous discussion to avoid affecting other groups.

From the first check-in on 3/13 to the last on 6/19, I did 13 online discussions with students (excluding long weekends), each about 3 hours, total 39 hours. Sounds little, but it’s a rough estimate — many sessions went past 3 hours or ended early. Plus this doesn’t include async communication time.

How many groups did I lead? As mentioned, 9 groups, each with 5–6 people (6 * 9 = roughly 54 people). Honestly it’s a lot. Future programs will have each coach take 3–4 groups, otherwise the mental load is huge, RRRR Orz.

Besides tracking milestone progress, a coach has to understand each student’s situation. Students are all working, so sometimes they have to do overtime suddenly. I have to track every student’s situation to make sure no one drops out XD.

(That said, some students did drop out — some at the start, some midway.)

What if a student wants to drop? I tell them: before you drop, talk to me about what’s happening. At least let me understand your situation. Sometimes the reason you want to drop is just falling into a negative spiral, and a little advice gets you out and pushing forward again. If you really want to drop, I ask you to clarify what work you have on your plate and how to redistribute it — that way teammates’ progress isn’t blocked.

My stance is: don’t cut requirements unless necessary. We already cut requirements in milestone 2. If we cut again because of a drop, team morale tanks — sometimes the rest just don’t want to continue either. So unless absolutely necessary, I avoid re-cutting.

Of course, not every group starts smoothly. As I said, everyone is a working engineer — fatigue is real. Some groups fell behind in late dev. My job: figure out how to push them forward.

OK, let me list what I, as a coach, did this round:

  • Track milestone progress
  • Help clarify dev / requirement issues
  • Online discussions / async communication with students
  • Understand teammate coordination
  • Provide tech advice
  • Share career and personal experience for the future

The last point is “burning the midnight oil with students“ XD.

So 4–5 days of every week I was on coach-related work. Some might see this as burning out — and yes — but I personally think it’s worth it, especially when I see your final outputs.

What I did wasn’t only the above. The curriculum can’t cover everything, so I also wrote extra articles for students, e.g.:

  • Token in localStorage? sessionStorage? Or Cookie? (article in Mandarin)
  • Demo time and your machine fell asleep — embarrassing. Use UptimeRobot or cron-job.org to help! (article in Mandarin)
  • You should probably understand CI/CD a bit — deploy to Render.com with CD (article in Mandarin)
  • Can’t use TypeScript? No worries — you can annotate types with JSDoc (article in Mandarin)
  • Build a simple WebSocket chat room with Express (article in Mandarin)
  • Stop learning testing with 1+1=2 — let’s learn unit testing with Vitest! (article in Mandarin)
  • Let’s learn how to draw a flowchart from scratch! (article in Mandarin)
  • The light and shadows of being a frontend engineer — think carefully before stepping into this field (article in Mandarin)
  • Engineer life at a gambling company: the mystery, opportunities, and challenges (article in Mandarin)

All written during this period — students would ask, courses might not cover, and to accelerate milestone passage, I wrote these.

A few last words to my students

Lots of stuff about this capstone above. To the 9 groups I led, I want to say a few things.

First, thanks for being my students. I don’t consider myself a great coach, but I learned a lot from you. The process was genuinely painful — weekly online check-ins with me, plus me chasing you Thursday or Friday in text for progress reports. Felt like I was hounding you, but I just wanted you to not give up. And to tell me if anything came up, so I could help solve it.

Some of you got hit with company OT or had to learn new tech at work and after work — pressure topped out 🫠.

But ultimately, you all shipped your projects. The final result might not match what you envisioned at the start — plans never survive contact with reality. Often you had to give up features to hit the deadline. But I believe you got what you wanted from this program, and I believe you can solve anything that comes up later. You survived this enterprise capstone — nothing in the future is going to take you down 💪.

I also believe many of you felt something deeply: the hard part isn’t tech, it’s team collaboration and communication. After this experience, I believe each of you knows better how to talk to your company team and partners and how to find higher-priority tasks more efficiently.

Last:

“You’ve all worked hard. This program’s milestone is over, but the end of one milestone means the start of another — just like our capstone planning. Sometimes it’s brutal and brain-burning, sometimes it’s smooth and fun. But as long as you persist and keep going, the final result will be what you wanted. Keep at it!”

This post is wrapping up. I can finally bow out of the coach role and return to being just a frontend engineer / TA. But please remember…

If you need my help, I’ll always be online waiting for your DM 😊.
