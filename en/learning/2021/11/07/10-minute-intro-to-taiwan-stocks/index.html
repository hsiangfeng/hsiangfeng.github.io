A 10-Minute Intro to Stock Trading — Using Taiwan Stocks as an Example

#Skills #Investment #Passive Income #Stocks #Salary #Taiwan Stocks

Intro

This post is mainly for friends — a bunch of them keep asking how to get started with stocks, and stocks have a lot of jargon. So I figured I’d write it up as both a friend-pointer and a personal note.

Why learn to trade stocks

A quick word on why we should learn to trade stocks. Most of us live on a “fixed” salary, and basic personal-finance knowledge can effectively give you a raise — or even let you bonus-fund yourself each year.

You can’t realistically rely on a job forever. You might want to retire, buy more stuff, or improve your quality of life. So personal finance matters a lot. There’s a saying:

“If you don’t manage money, money won’t manage you.”

But before getting into stock trading — if you have zero financial habits, I’d recommend reading my earlier post “Prep Work Before Building Passive Income, and a Budgeting Intro“ first. The main reason: I want you to see your spending habits straight on. You may find you’re actually quite well-off — you just haven’t planned your money seriously.

(I’m definitely NOT talking about a friend who earns NT$1xx,000/month and still has zero savings.)

Before investing, get this mentality straight:

“Investments go up and down. Evaluate risk yourself.”

Don’t yolo your savings — that’s just gambling. Stocks can win big and lose big. Good investing builds you an extra income stream. Throwing money in without understanding it is gambling. The definition of “investing” is “within your manageable risk range”. Outside that range is “gambling”.

Unless you want to be Itou Kaiji (the gambling-anime protagonist).

OK, let’s get into the basics of stock trading.

(Oh — no sponsorships in this post. If there are any “links”, they’re to my own articles.)

Opening a brokerage account

Before you start trading, you need two things:

  • Bank account (for debits)
  • Brokerage account (holds the stocks)

Opening these isn’t hard. If I had to recommend, I’d suggest opening at a bank you already use.

The bigger domestic brokerages: KGI, Jih Sun, Taishin, CTBC — any of them work (I’m with Cathay).

Why pick one you already use? Mostly for easier asset management. But one note: if you open at Taishin, by default they convert Richart into a brokerage account, and that loses you some digital-bank perks (like 2% deposit interest). Keep that in mind.

Opening a brand-new account is also less convenient under current AML regulations — banks need to verify you’re not money-laundering. So existing accounts are usually easier.

Trading fees

Trading fees differ per brokerage. Honestly, unless you’re a high-volume trader, these fees won’t move the needle. For a normal salary-saver, the difference is small. Worry about it when you become a whale.

There’s a standard rate. As of 2021/11/07, the standard stock trading fee I’m seeing is 0.1425%. May change later, but for now: 0.1425%. Each brokerage also offers fee discounts — 50–70% off is common, since most trading is electronic. Let’s use 30% off (i.e., 70% of base) as an example.

One thing to note: if the fee comes out below NT$20, brokerages charge a NT$20 minimum.

Fees apply on both buys and sells.

If you can’t be bothered to remember, just look at your statement.

Trading hours

The common trading periods:

  • Pre-market
  • Intraday
  • Closing
  • After-hours

In Taiwan stocks, trading happens on weekdays — Monday through Friday (no trading on holidays).

Pre-market (8:30 ~ 9:00)

Pre-market is 8:30 to 9:00, also called “pre-market auction”, “pre-market trading”, or “simulated matching”.

The idea: simulate where today’s prices might land. Think of it as “not real” — actual trading begins after the 9:00 open.

(But… I personally don’t trade here. Mornings are for sleep.)

Intraday (9:00 ~ 13:30)

Then the “official open” — 9:00 to 13:30. Also called “intraday” or “regular session”. This is when you’ll see prices moving up and down.

This window is also called “regular trading”.

Closing (13:00 ~ 13:30)

You may hear jargon like “closing push” or “closing dump”. These describe price action in the last 30 minutes before regular session ends.

These terms cover 13:00–13:30 — the 30 minutes before close are called “closing”.

Heads up: 13:25–13:30 is a pre-close auction window, so strictly speaking, regular session ends at 13:25.

After-hours (13:30 ~ 14:30)

After 13:30 intraday ends and the “after-hours” session begins. Only two things trade after-hours:

  • After-hours fixed-price trading
  • Odd-lot trading

We’ll cover these. For now, just know the timing.

(Odd-lot trading is now available intraday too. In the past it was after-hours only.)

Round lots and odd lots

What’s the deal with round lots vs odd lots?

In Taiwan, 1000 shares = 1 “lot” = round lot. Odd lot is 1–999 shares — anything under 1000.

So if you buy 1500 shares, that’s 1 round lot + 500 odd-lot shares.

For salary-savers, odd lots are a great entry point. Long-term, regular odd-lot purchases can compound into a round lot. The yield generally beats a bank time deposit by a wide margin, because stocks can appreciate, you get dividends (a slice of company profit), etc.

The math is simple. If 0050 is NT$30/share and you buy 1000 shares: 1000 * 30 = NT$30,000. Don’t forget the fee:

So you’d be debited NT$30,030 on purchase.

Side note: odd lots are now tradable intraday. Used to be only after-hours (13:30–14:30). Rules changed. Note that intraday odd-lot trading starts 10 minutes later than round-lot trading.

Orders, after-hours fixed-price trading

What’s an “order”? Also called a “trading instruction”.

E.g., you place an order to buy a stock at NT$30/share, 1000 shares. The system auto-matches you with someone selling at NT$30/share. This is also called “regular trading” or “auction trading” — because if someone else bids higher, they win the share.

After-hours fixed-price trading uses the closing price. During intraday you can name your price freely (e.g. NT$100/share), but after-hours fixed-price only works at the day’s last price. So if close was NT$200/share, you can only buy at NT$200/share after-hours — not NT$100/share.

Up/down limits

Taiwan stocks have a daily 10% up/down limit. Meaning: a stock can move at most 10% in a day. Hit +10% and it’s “limit up” or “limit up board”. Hit –10% it’s “limit down” or “limit down board”. Taiwan uses red for up and green for down (opposite of Western convention).

So a red background with white text = limit up (+10%). Green background with white text = limit down (-10%).

T-1, T-2

You may see people say “T-1” and “T-2”. “T” stands for “Trading Day”.

What’s that mean?

  • T-1
    • Trade execution day
  • T-2
    • Settlement day

The day your buy executes is T-1. T-2 is the day after — settlement day. Important: on settlement day, you MUST have the settlement funds in your debit account by 9:00 AM. If the brokerage can’t debit you, it becomes “default on settlement”.

Default on settlement

Default on settlement is a HUGE deal — avoid it at all costs. It can trigger civil and even criminal liability. Usually the bank temporarily fronts the settlement funds, then chases the defaulter for the loss. So remember: don’t default on settlement!

OK, wrapping up. Hope this helps if you’re getting into stocks.
