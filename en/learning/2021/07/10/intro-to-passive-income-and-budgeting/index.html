Budgeting Intro

Intro

This post is a different angle from my usual — more about the thinking around passive income. A friend asked me "how do I start building passive income?", so I figured I'd write down what we talked about. Next time someone asks, I can just point them here.

Why people want passive income

First, let's talk about why people want passive income. Passive income is basically tied to investing. The concepts here are shallow, but you have to actually act on them to get anywhere.

People who want passive income usually have one of these wishes:

  • Current salary isn't great, want extra income
  • Want to reach the point where they don't need to work to live
  • Want a bit more to splurge with (?) ...

All kinds of variations.

All of these ultimately come back to improving your current life situation — passive income to make life happier, freer from money constraints.

If you Google "how to build passive income" or "investing passive income", you'll find a lot. But is it really achievable by anyone? Honestly, no — it's not that simple. Don't let that scare you off though. Try this thought experiment: you earn NT$30,000/month, you're 30, you want to retire at 60. So you have to save enough in 30 years. Rough math:

js
(30 * 12) * 30000 = 10800000 // 12 months/yr, 30*12=360 months, 360*salary = lifetime earnings

NT$10.8M looks substantial — but can you actually save that? Probably not, because there are a ton of expenses missing from this math.

So this post will walk through what you should prep before chasing passive income.

Understand your income and expenses

Personal finance is the cliché topic that's hard to dodge, but no question — before you build passive income, budgeting is a hard prerequisite. Without money, how do you invest?

Step one in passive income is NOT going to buy stocks, funds, ETFs. Step one is understanding your own money plan. Before any passive income move, fully understand your income and outflow. If you can't, you're just blindly entering the market, and that may cost you your life savings.

So step one: understand your income and expenses. How? Simple — track them. Roughly estimate a year of expenses. Say your monthly salary is NT$40,000, with other expenses separate. Roughly list them:

Calculate roughly what you can spend each month. Food and transportation are the hardest to nail down — just rough estimates. Say breakfast NT$50, lunch NT$100, dinner NT$150, that's NT$200/day, or NT$9,000/month ((50+100+150)*30). Transportation, roughly NT$300/week, four weeks/month = NT$1,200. The month you crank the AC, or the month with a lot of human-to-human connection, those spike. So I'd recommend pinning down your fixed expenses — the stuff that's locked in short-term.

Fixed:

  • Internet — 999
  • Phone — 1,388
  • Rent / Mortgage — 7,000
  • Misc / Student Loan — 3,000

  • Water — 500
  • Electric — 2,500
  • Food — 9,000
  • Transportation — 1,200
  • Entertainment — 5,000

So annual fixed expense is NT$148,644 ((999+1388+7000+3000)*12). That's fixed-only — variable can swing wildly depending on habits. But your fixed income is NT$40,000/month → NT$480,000/year, so after fixed expenses you have NT$331,356 (480,000-148,644).

You might think "OK so I could save 100k a year if I'm thrifty!" — but I said "maybe", because we can't lock down our variable expenses. So next, a method to force structure on the variable part.

n-jar budgeting / money control method

I personally love this method. Whether you want 6, 8, or whatever number of jars, the core idea is dividing your money by ratio to control monthly spending.

What's that mean? We figured out fixed expenses, but couldn't pin down variable ones — because, well, humans have desires. So it's hard to force a strict monthly cap. The n-jar method works around this. The article "6-Jar Budgeting" (article in Mandarin) by Ms. Lipai has helped me a lot. The idea is to split your salary into:

  • Financial Freedom Jar (10%)
    • For monthly investment — funds, forex, stocks, etc.
  • Education Jar (10%)
    • Most passive income requires learning first. This jar funds courses.
  • Necessity Jar (50%)
    • Rent, utilities, internet, credit cards, food — daily essentials.
  • Entertainment Jar (10%)
    • Telling you to not go out for fun all year is impossible (it'd be for me). Allocate a chunk to fun.
  • Long-term Savings Jar (10%)
    • For occasional big purchases — phone, computer. Avoid eating into other jars.
  • Emergency Jar (10%)
    • For surprises like illness or sudden expenses. You need this buffer.

Each jar has meaning. Do I have to follow this exact ratio? Not necessarily — adjust to your situation. Just try to keep Necessity below 55–60%. If it's higher, audit your spending.

Pre-prep for passive income

Now that you understand your expenses and use the n-jar method, time to prepare for actual passive income.

Most investment courses and methods say to keep an emergency fund covering 3–6 months of expenses before you start. As the name says — it's emergency-only. Don't touch it.

How much? Roughly 3–6 months of your monthly expenses. If your monthly is NT$20,000, your emergency fund should be at least NT$120,000 (20,000*6). Otherwise you might run before any passive income arrives.

Money beyond the emergency fund is what you can actually deploy.

Once emergency fund is set, start studying your target passive income vehicles — stocks, funds, forex are common. But is passive income only those? No.

You could become a YouTuber, streamer, or blogger too. YouTuber/streamer income obviously: ads, sponsorships. Blogger income? Mainly post writing + Google Ads (hoping you don't have AdBlock on my blog (ఠ్ఠ ˓̭ ఠ్ఠ)). Some get sponsored posts, but small-time bloggers mostly live on Google Ads. AdBlock cuts hard, so revenue is often low. Google Ads has two revenue types:

  • Impression revenue
  • Click revenue

Impression revenue requires thousands of views before it's meaningful. Click revenue scales with clicks. Another option: add a Liker (likes-as-payment system) at the bottom of articles to earn small amounts. My blog has one. A single like is roughly NT$0.1.

So if you want to make passive income as a blogger, food, travel, and finance topics are easier to surface and click through.

Another distinctive route: be a landlord. As a broke person, I have no house, so this option's out.

You might think — can't the n-jar method itself produce passive income? Think about it: without investing, how do you make sure you get extra money each month (say NT$30,000 extra)? That's tied to investing — using money to grow money. By 60, you'd have far more than the NT$10.8M baseline — multiples more. The how-to-invest part I'll skip — I'm just a beginner myself (lol).

Starting passive income

Now that prep is done, don't just throw money at any investment. Don't watch some random online tutorial and dump money into stocks/funds. That's not how you get the passive income you want. Before that, understand your vehicle. For stocks, two typical approaches:

  • Jump in and start trading
  • Pay for a course

Jumping in straight is "learn by doing on the battlefield". If you take losses, you have to dig into why — and you'll learn how trading works. Safer route is buying a course. Some courses are pricey (NT$20k–30k), but think about it — if you trade blind, you might lose multiples of that. Tens of thousands, hundreds of thousands.

Long-term operation

Whatever vehicle you pick — stocks, forex, blogging — understand this: passive income requires long-term commitment. There's no overnight wealth. Passive income and investing are inseparable. You have to commit long-term to learning and operation. Before investing, one principle:

"Investments go up and down."

So set a loss tolerance you're comfortable with, because all investment is tied to human psychology. Stock movement, forex movement — all human-driven. Before chasing passive income, hold this principle: "Stop talking, start doing." Don't say "I'll sell at 10% loss" and then never sell, hoping it'll come back. Common pattern: it falls until you cry. You need a stop loss point and you have to act on it. Otherwise losses grow, and that mindset is gambling.

As long as you're within your control range, it's investing. The moment you can't control it and you're praying for a moonshot, that's gambling.

Invest in yourself

Last — if your time + money plan really doesn't leave room for passive income, I'd straight up recommend switching to a higher-paying job, or putting your current salary toward courses that build a money-earning skill. Engineer, designer, barista, architect — any "-er" works, as long as it grows your income.

You might wonder — investing in yourself counts as passive income? Think about it: if a skill takes you from NT$40,000 to NT$50,000 or NT$60,000, that means more money to invest. More invested → more return. So self-investment is an indirect passive income path.
