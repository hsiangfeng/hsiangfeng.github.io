Intro

As a career-switcher into engineering, there's one really common mistake juniors make — putting course assignments in their portfolio.

The slash life

We're in an information-explosion era where everyone's a slash-something (basically, a multi-tool human). The reason a lot of people gravitate toward this is that many jobs will probably get replaced, or hit a salary ceiling pretty fast. So a lot of people go online looking for learning platforms, hoping to retrain into engineering — engineer salaries are less capped and harder to phase out (although if you don't keep growing, you'll still get phased out).

Some online learning platforms out there right now:

  • Udemy
  • TibaMe
  • Lidemy
  • IT Handyguy Academy
  • HISKIO
  • Hahow
  • HexSchool
  • Yotta
  • 5xruby
  • ALPHA Camp

That's just a list of platforms I know about or have used, as examples. Definitely not sponsored x 3 — though I do work at HexSchool (huh?! I just said no sponsorships).

Back on track — as you can see, there are a lot of platforms. People usually spend a ton of mental effort on the foundational courses, so they end up shopping around like they're at a wet market, comparing three sellers, hunting for the highest bang-for-buck class that'll land them a job.

Coursework vs portfolio — easy to mix up

After finishing those courses, you enter the job-hunt phase. Before applying, everyone has to prep a resume — and this is where a lot of junior engineers make a major mistake: using course assignments as their portfolio.

Let me say this plainly: assignments ≠ portfolio. Why? Let's start by understanding what an assignment actually is.

The definition of "assignment"

You can search "assignment" on Wikipedia:

Homework, also known as an assignment, refers to tasks assigned by teachers to students at school, typically to prepare for or review knowledge. Common types of homework include reading specified chapters of textbooks, reading designated books, writing or typing essays, solving math problems, etc. Homework differs from quizzes or exams in that you can usually consult textbooks, reference books, dictionaries, etc., discuss with classmates and teachers, and look up information online.

The key part: "typically to prepare for or review knowledge". So we can see that an assignment is something that lets the teacher confirm you've absorbed and reviewed the concepts they taught. Which means assignments are genuinely not a good fit for a portfolio.

The definition of "work" (in the portfolio sense)

Next, the Wikipedia definition of "work":

A work, also called a creation, creative product, or composition, is a finished product that has creativity and can be reproduced in some form. Copyright law protects the expression of these creative activities. The creator of a work is the author.

Key takeaway: a "work" must be creative. The biggest difference between a work and an assignment: works carry copyright and are something you came up with yourself.

A work generally isn't something a teacher hands you. It's a creative thing you had to dream up yourself — basically, creating your own kid. Wikipedia makes it pretty clear that works and assignments are different.

What should the resume have?

Now we understand the difference. So when you're prepping your resume, you might ask:

"Can I use my course assignments as portfolio pieces?"

I'll be direct: No. (Did you not just read the whole thing above?)

Why not? Take HexSchool — there are 20,000+ people learning online and submitting assignments. So you can imagine: there are up to 20,000 copies of the same project floating around.

Doesn't that sound terrifying? Try Googling "HexSchool Western Restaurant", "Dessert E-commerce", "Kaohsiung Travel Site", etc. The number of results is huge. Now imagine you're a hiring manager. You've invited 100 engineers to interview, and all 100 of them hand you the same "HexSchool Western Restaurant", "Dessert E-commerce", "Kaohsiung Travel Site" projects. Wouldn't you get exhausted?

The first time you see them, you might think "wow, this engineer looks pretty sharp!" After 10+ submissions, it shifts to "ugh, these again?" High similarity across portfolios causes two problems: "no way to gauge actual skill level" and "portfolio fatigue". Every project follows the same SOP, so you really can't tell where this engineer's skill ceiling is.

So a lot of interviewers will start digging into details: "How does this line of code work?", "Why did you write it this way?", or "Can you walk me through what was hard about building this project?" These seem basic, but if you treated the course as something to just "get through" without actually understanding it, you'll get nailed on these questions. The interviewer will conclude you basically copied the template, and a lot of interviewers will just stop inviting people whose portfolio is the same as everyone else's. Honestly — would you rather interview 100 people with the same portfolio, or someone with something creative?

One more thing: do not use assignments as portfolio. You're just setting yourself up for tougher questioning. I did this at one point — used assignments as my portfolio — and got grilled even harder. I had an interviewer pull up the code on the spot and ask me how a specific line worked. They literally compared mine to the previous candidate's: any extra effects? Different layout? Different images? Is the HTML structure HTML5? Etc.

I've experienced all of that. One time the interviewer opened the previous candidate's portfolio and said: "Do you think your work is better than theirs? Look, they added effects — CSS3 animations, prototype-chain logic. Where do you actually win?" No joke — I really did get face-washed like that.

After I removed all the course assignments from my resume and replaced them with stuff I built myself, that kind of harsh interviewer was rare. Not every interviewer is brutal — they've just seen these portfolios N times, and they're being tough to figure out where your real ceiling is.

These takes are from a frontend perspective and might not be perfectly correct, but I really hope you stop using course assignments as your portfolio — unless you really enjoy getting face-washed.

Bonus tip

One more thing — if you genuinely don't know how to come up with portfolio ideas, check out The F2E - Frontend Chamber of Time. Pick a design mockup and build it. The hotel-themed week is a particularly solid topic, and The F2E has nearly 689 design mockups, so you don't have to worry about over-similarity. Every mockup is CC-licensed, so you can use them for your own portfolio. Building from a The F2E mockup is way better than using your course assignments.
