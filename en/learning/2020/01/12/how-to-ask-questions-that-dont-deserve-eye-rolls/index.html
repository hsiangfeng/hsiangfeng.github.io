Let's Talk About Questions That Really Deserve a Roasting

Intro

As an engineer, hitting problems and needing to ask for help is super common. But a lot of people ignore "how to ask questions the smart way", and "how to ask well" is itself a skill. Some people's question style is honestly cringe-inducing, so today let's talk about how to ask well.

Search skills

Before you ask, please do one thing first: Google. As long as you do this, basically no one will roast you give you grief.

Google

Probably 70–80% of what you want to ask is already on Google, which is why engineers today are sometimes jokingly called Google engineers (no objection from me).

This isn't unwarranted. We're in an information-explosion era — most problems really are on Google, you just might not know how to search.

When you can't find what you're looking for, try these keyword-tuning tricks to adjust your search query — you might find what you're after:

  • Reorder your keywords. Swapping the order can give different results.
    • e.g. RE: Zero Starting Learning Life JS → RE: Zero Starting Learning JS Life (product placement).
  • Reduce keyword count.
    • e.g. ThinkPHP Basic Learning → ThinkPHP Basic.
  • Switch to English. A lot more engineering knowledge exists in English. If your English is shaky like mine, use Google Translate to convert Chinese to English.
  • Use site:specific-domain + keywords to focus the search on a specific site (I most often focus on Stack Overflow).
  • Wrap a keyword in " to keep it intact in search — otherwise the keyword can get split.
    • e.g. "JavaScript".
  • Use - to exclude content.
    • e.g. PHP -basic tutorial.
  • Use * as a wildcard when you're unsure about part of the keyword.
    • e.g. PHP *path.
  • Search within a year range with keyword year..year.
    • e.g. ithome help 2016..2018.
  • Combine keywords with site-specific search.
    • e.g. JS pass by reference site:https://israynotarray.com/.

That's just some of the search tricks. If you use these well, a lot of questions you wouldn't need to ask others at all — and you don't have to worry about whether your phrasing was right, or being ignored like an outsider (huh?).

A good opening to your question

A lot of junior engineers skip this basic-but-foundational thing. Let me first explain what a good opening looks like. Slipping in a quick intro about your background is a solid start:

text
Hi everyone~
I'm a beginner who's been learning for about XX months. I have a question
about CSS & HTML...

Why is this a good opening? You might think it doesn't describe what you've learned or what you understand, but typically a normal person can't fully articulate everything they understand. So I'd recommend telling people how long you've been at it, so they get a sense of your level. If you've been learning JavaScript for three years, someone helping you can size you up reasonably well — three years is three years.

So adding your learning background up front helps others know what level you're at, and that significantly affects what kind of answer you get. If you don't mention any of that, and just ask:

text
Hi everyone~
I have a question about CSS & HTML...

You'll usually get suggestions, but you'll notice some advice is too advanced, with no examples — just directions and concepts. (The "give a fish, not teach to fish" trap, in reverse.)

Why? Because you didn't tell them your level, so the kind-hearted helpers will assume you're at the same level as them. To avoid this, I'd recommend you always state your level range, so your question gets a more focused, more digestible answer not the "armchair coder" treatment.

Quick pause for a sec. Try to imagine: I'm a complete stranger to you. You might know me, but I don't know you reading this right now. If you don't tell me your handle, experience, and level in your opening — how am I supposed to know from what angle to answer you, and whether I should write an example? Unless you really want me to do the armchair-coder thing.

Last thing: if you're a junior engineer or just career-switched, include what you've already Googled and what your level is. People willing to answer will be way friendlier. You've shown you tried first and you only ask after hitting a wall — you're basically not going to run into beep aggressive people.

Choose where to ask carefully

Suppose you're a frontend engineer like me (this applies to other domains too, I'm just using my own as the example). And you have a frontend question. If you go ask it in a PHP, Python, or C++ community, there's a 90% chance your question gets ignored or downvoted. The 10% that gets a response is probably something like:

"You can't even tell where to ask? Are you 87?" (Taiwanese slang — like calling someone clueless.)

"Please go ask in a frontend community, OK?"

If you're into being yelled at, I genuinely don't know what to say.

So before asking, grab these two principles:

  • Don't post your question somewhere it doesn't fit.
    • e.g. Python question in a Ruby community.
  • Mind the level of people in the community you're posting to.
    • e.g. beginner question in a hacker community.

I most often see people asking advanced questions in beginner communities. Think about it — if you're in a beginner-heavy space asking mid-to-advanced questions, what guarantee do you have that anyone there can answer? The skip-probability is easily 87%. The remaining chance of a real answer is mostly senior people lurking who feel like helping. So you're basically not going to get an answer — don't take impossible missions, you're not Tom Cruise.

Image source: web

I once saw someone in a React forum asking a Vue question (asking for it, right?).

You can usually tell whether someone's asking in the right spot by some signals. On Facebook communities, posts in the wrong place usually have:

  • Low reaction count.
    • Side note: some people use a like to mean "read".
  • No answers.
  • The question sinks fast.
  • A pile of roast posts

So choosing where to ask carefully speeds up answers and cuts down on roasts.

Adjust your mindset before asking

Before asking, get your head right. You need to understand: you can't demand that anyone reply to you, and especially not that your problem gets solved immediately, in real time. As I said: "I'm a complete stranger to you". So why am I obligated to spend my time? In that time I could be playing Assassin's Creed (huh?).

Like Yu Yuxi (creator of Vue.js) said:

Why should I sacrifice time with my wife and kids to save you time learning English?

(Source here — article in Mandarin)

A few things to also watch when asking.

No typos

Please — typos in your question are dumb. If you want someone to help you, but your message is full of typos, "Martian script", and bopomofo shorthand, that basically says "while reading your question, please also correct my Chinese for me?" At which point I feel bad for your language teacher.

text
This problm I...
text
Dis prblm I...

If you commit this mistake, the thread is going to look like:

"Suggest you go review some grammar first"

"Your language teacher is crying"

"Fix the typos and then I'll respond"

"I'm starting to doubt my own grasp of Chinese"

Etc., etc.

If the typos aren't egregious, no one usually says anything — IMEs sometimes confuse stuff like "再" and "在".

Be clear and start with a clear opening

People love starting questions with "#need-help", "#sos", "#i-dont-get-it", "#i-dont-understand", "#waiting-now". If I see these I subconsciously skip the question, because they're forbidden openings. Pointless openings make people not want to help — they just feel like dealing with a clueless person.

A clear opening lets people get into your problem fast. With these forbidden phrases, plenty of seniors will just skip you.

For me personally, I really dislike these phrases. The reason is simple — when I'm helping, I need to know your level and your problem directly. Not see you wailing in the void. Plus, unclear openings hurt your question's searchability.

Clearly describe your logic and process

Beyond not using those forbidden phrases, you also need to clearly describe your code logic. If you're not sure how, I'd recommend laying it out as:

  • Provide the source code
  • The file and line numbers you think are buggy
  • Your expected result
  • The actual result
  • What you've already tried and what you've already searched for
  • What you understand after trying

Beyond these, don't include anything else. I mean it.

(If, after providing these, the helper still can't pin down your need, add context to help them think. Context matters too.)

"How to Ask Questions the Smart Way" calls this out: padding your question with redundant phrasing makes people think you're disrespecting them.

From the smart-questioning essay:

Avoid finishing your question with meaningless filler like "Can anyone help me?" or "Is there an answer?" First: if your description of the problem is weak, this filler makes it worse. Second: because the filler is redundant, hackers will get annoyed, and often reply with technically correct but useless answers like "Yes, someone can help you" or "No, there's no answer" — to express their disdain. Generally, avoid yes/no, right/wrong, exists/doesn't-exist phrasings unless you actually want a yes/no answer.

Also: your first attempt at asking might not be understandable to others. Unless you're already an experienced question-asker who can precisely articulate needs and problems, expect to need to add follow-up clarification.

Try not to provide source code as screenshots

Providing an error message as a screenshot is fine — even reasonable. Sometimes after we reproduce your project with the source code you provided, we don't see the bug. Your screenshot becomes the key data, because everyone's dev environment differs.

But please don't screenshot the part of your code you think is buggy. That's really dumb.

A lot of bugs aren't in the area you think they are. Maybe the actual issue is just outside your screenshot. So again: "Don't screenshot your code". Screenshots of error messages or error screens are fine — those are two separate things.

The key here is "Don't screenshot your code". I'm repeating it because too many people make this mistake. If you're reading this, I assume you want to build good asking habits — don't make this one.

Another reason not to screenshot code: imagine you're the one helping. Would you rather copy-paste the asker's code into your editor and run it, or retype the whole thing from a picture? Of course you'd want to copy-paste. The retype path is also error-prone — you might mistype, which means the bug doesn't reproduce or introduces new bugs.

So one more time: "Don't screenshot your code". Error messages and error screens — those are fine.

Avoid using your phone camera for error messages

Note! This is about error messages, not source code!

Don't conflate "Try not to provide source code as screenshots" with "Avoid using your phone camera for error messages". Two different things!

You should avoid using your phone to photograph error messages, because phone photos vary with camera quality, angle, and clarity. Here's a terrible example:

OK, you might think...

What's wrong with this picture? Looks fine!

Please — this was deliberately taken to look decent. If your phone has low resolution, a bad angle, or broken digital stabilization, the code's gonna look terrible, not to mention you might miss the key area entirely.

Beyond pasting code, there's another option: online editors

In addition to avoid using your phone to photograph error messages, please use an online editor when possible.

Why? Imagine — your code is super long, and you paste it into a community or chat. Doesn't that suck to read?

I think it does. Being a developer is hard on the eyes already. Having to read a wall of unstyled, no-syntax-highlighting, sprawling code? I check out and ask you to use an online editor instead.

When people commit this mistake, the response is usually:

text
Can you use an online editor?
text
I can barely read this

That's your signal — your asking style needs fixing.

If you don't know any online editors, here are two of my own posts:

  • Get Started with CodePen in 10 Minutes (with Chinese subtitles) (article in Mandarin)
  • Get Started with CodeSandbox in 10 Minutes (with Chinese subtitles) (article in Mandarin)

Online editors also reproduce your error reliably, which makes the problem faster and more focused to solve.

Even if it's urgent, don't say it's urgent

Common one:

text
This is urgent for me...

I see "urgent" and I skip. You need to understand: "this is YOUR problem, not MINE — I'm not obligated to drop everything to fix it for you". Helping you doesn't make me more money.

Also seriously, don't write "#waiting-now". If I see it, I will absolutely make you wait until the end of time.

Don't pretend homework is a real question

Don't try to sneak homework into a community thinking nobody will notice. Communities are full of seniors and hackers — they spot homework instantly. You need to understand: homework is something you have to figure out yourself. You're meant to learn from it. I'm not saying you can never ask about homework — but you need to identify which concept you're unclear on, then ask about the concept, not "how to do it". Those two are very different.

If the homework is genuinely too hard, ask the teacher who assigned it, not toss it into the wild ocean and hope someone finds your message in a bottle.

Politeness gets faster answers

The more polite you are, the less weird stuff or hostility you'll run into.

But many people, after getting an answer, don't actively thank the helper. That's serious. You need to understand: people are spending their free time helping you, even hunting for phrasings you can understand and writing examples for you — and that takes time. If you don't say "thanks", "thanks for your help", "thanks for the explanation~" — or describe the process you took to digest the answer — next time you ask, you'll find nobody bites. Or replies will be way slower than the first time.

The most expensive thing is the free thing.

Respect everyone's time

When asking, respect every bit of time. Every stranger has their own schedule. While they're helping you, the original plan might have been showering, eating, or family time — but they sacrificed that to help. That kind of sacrifice can lead to actual household conflicts (I'm serious — check this post).

So they can't be your personal tutor available 24/7. If your question takes 2–3 back-and-forths just to figure out what you actually mean, you might burn days or weeks before getting an answer. So write your question well and submit it once — the more detailed the better.

Common one I see:

text
Ray, I have a problem.

And then? Nothing.

I notice the message two or three days later. I'll respond "What's your problem?" You take another two to three days to notice. By the time I help, it's been almost a month. So when you ask for help, send the question completely and in detail the first time. Unless you have unlimited time, or a "Doraemon time-stop tool", I really don't know what to say.

Opening with politeness isn't bad — you are asking for a favor:

text
Hi TA,
text
Hi TA, sorry to bother you — I have a question to ask

If you're worried about being too curt, expand into:

text
Hi TA, sorry to bother you — I have a question to ask.
It's about Vue3 deployment to GitHub having some config issues, I've already tried...

This style gets you an answer way faster than just the polite greeting alone, without sacrificing politeness.

Also important: don't send one question in five separate messages. That's just spam, e.g....

text
Hi TA, sorry to bother you...
text
I have a question
text
It's about Vue3 deployment to GitHub config issues
text
I've already tried...

Send the whole thing at once. I personally don't love getting notification spam either — my company chat is already noisy, no need to turn my phone into a massage chair.

Replying after getting an answer

This is super important. When you get an answer, please don't just reply:

text
Thanks, got it!
text
Thanks senior xxx!

Please, don't do this.

It feels lazy and like you're a "freeloader". The better move is to absorb the answer, then describe it back in your own words. I know thanking is important, but I'd rather see what you learned. That way we can confirm whether you actually got the concept. Right?

Finally — remember to mark the thread "answered". Otherwise others reading later might think your question is still unsolved.

Reflect on your questions

If you've made it this far, I'd hope you'd reflect on whether your past asking style had issues. Whether you found this article yourself or someone sent it to you, you presumably want to ask better and get faster help.

So after getting an answer, write up the question + resolution into a note or blog post, and share it publicly. One day someone with the same or similar question will Google your post and benefit — that's the right cycle.

A good question is a two-way exchange, not one-sided. If you see someone in the future asking a question you once asked, try answering them. When you can answer, it means you actually learned it.
