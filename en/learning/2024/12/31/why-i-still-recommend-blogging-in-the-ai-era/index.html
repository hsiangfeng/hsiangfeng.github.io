AI Is Everywhere — Why I Still Recommend Blogging

Intro

At WebConf the other day, a few students came up to ask me about AI. Their thinking was that AI is everywhere now and can generate just about anything — so why do I still recommend they blog? This post is a quick take on my view of AI and why I still think blogging matters.

AI is everywhere

There's no denying AI is incredibly widespread. There's basically nothing it can't help you generate, explain, or analyze. Examples:

  • ChatGPT — Generate me an article about...
  • Gemini — Summarize this article for me
  • Grok — What's a black hole particle?
  • Midjourney — Make me an Iron Man cover image
  • GitHub Copilot — Explain this snippet of code (article in Mandarin)
  • Claude.ai — Write me a Todo List
  • Gamma — Build me the slide deck for tonight's talk
  • Suno — Generate a heavy-metal style song

These are just my own examples. From them you can see how thoroughly AI has woven into our lives. If you're not using AI to help with your work yet, there's a real chance you'll get left behind.

That said — I'm not trying to manufacture AI panic or AI anxiety. I just hope people who haven't picked it up start learning, so you can multiply your productivity.

Note Try clicking those links — I included them all. Except GitHub Copilot (which goes to my own article), they all link to the relevant official sites.

Why I still recommend blogging

I just listed a bunch of AI use cases, and it's hard to argue that AI hasn't sped up our work and learning. Whether it's knowledge or hard problems, you can use AI to solve them or get unstuck.

Blog positioning

So why do I still recommend blogging? Let's first talk about the common "positionings" a blog can take:

  • Learning notes: use the blog to record your own learning journey. Input + output deepens your retention and threads ideas together.
  • Technical sharing: some people go deep on a specific tech and share what they found, so others can benefit.
  • Life logs: ticket-buying processes, travel notes, etc. — record stuff for yourself to look back on, and share with others.
  • Creative sharing: art, photography, music — share your creative work so others can enjoy it.

These four cover most common blog types, but there's no strict categorization. Define your own.

My blog "isRayNotArray" started as learning notes. Later it started carrying some technical sharing and a bit of life logging. Ratio-wise, it's roughly 70% learning notes, 20% technical sharing, 10% life logs. So there's no one true positioning.

What's compelling about blogging

So why mention positioning first? When people start writing, they worry a lot about whether they're writing it well, or whether it's "correct". Those worries make sense — a blog is public, others will read it. But that's not the point of writing one.

The reason I want you to clarify your positioning first: if you're starting with learning notes like I did, you're writing primarily for yourself. Whether others understand it isn't that important. The real question is: will you, a month from now or a year from now, still understand what you wrote.

Early on, your writing will be messy and scattered. I can tell you that's totally normal — it's hard to start anything, and you haven't found your own writing voice yet. If you don't believe me, dig up some of my earliest posts. Same deal.

If you keep writing, you'll slowly find your voice and your writing speed will pick up. Then, because you want your future self to follow your own writing more easily, you'll start hunting for better phrasings and clearer ways to describe things. That's the charm of blogging — but it takes time and iteration.

For a learning-notes blog, helping others is a secondary goal. The main point is the growth from your own input/output, not chasing readers. Helping someone else is just a happy accident — and that accident might become the motivation that keeps you going.

Also, being wrong isn't shameful. Sometimes being wrong sparks discussion, which leads to growth. If you take feedback in good faith, the blog gets better.

What's wrong with keeping notes locally?

Some people might ask...

"Can't I just keep notes locally or in a private notes app?"

Sure — having any kind of starting point is the best. But here's why I think a blog beats local notes:

  • Public-ness: your notes are public and crawled by search engines, which subconsciously pushes you to think about whether your notes make sense to anyone else, which makes them more complete.
  • Shareable: when you hit a problem, you can drop a link to your blog post for help. When my coworkers hit something I've already written up, I just send them the link — they solve it fast, and I only step in if they actually need it.
  • Visible: you can watch your notes grow over time. Seeing them accumulate slowly is genuinely satisfying.

These are the blog-specific upsides. You can absolutely keep notes locally, but for me the points above are what make blogs worth it.

AI is everywhere — why still blog?

OK, back to "AI is everywhere — why still blog?" AI does speed things up and solves a lot of problems. But AI can't develop these skills for you:

  • Thinking
  • Articulating
  • Writing

Before you use AI to solve a problem, you still have to think about what the problem is. Then you have to articulate it, or AI can't even tell what kind of help you want. Then, once AI gives you an answer, you still have to write it out to crystallize your thinking.

So a blog isn't just a notebook for you — it's a platform for thinking, articulating, and writing. Those are skills AI can't replace, and that's why I still recommend blogging.

Closing thoughts

I just made a lot of cases for blogging, but it all comes back to one thing: sticking with it. A lot of people quit after a few posts, which is honestly a shame. The reasons for quitting come in many flavors:

  • No time
  • No inspiration
  • No motivation ...etc.

On time — you don't have to produce a post a day. One post a week, or even 20 minutes of writing a day, is fine. The point is consistency. Over time it stacks into a huge notes library, and that's part of why I still recommend it.

On inspiration — I've asked a lot of people, and most are worried someone else already wrote it, so doing their own version feels like plagiarism. They feel like they have to be unique. Honestly, I think everything starts with imitation. If you want to learn fried rice, you watch how others fry it. That's just the natural process. Don't be afraid your content overlaps with someone else's — that's normal. The point is whether you can improve on it.

On motivation — clarify your positioning. Know why you're writing. My original goal was learning frontend, and that's what kept me going. That's why my blog has kept updating.

In closing, I hope this post answered at least part of your question, and that you'll actually start writing. I think it'll turn out to be one of the best decisions you'll make.
