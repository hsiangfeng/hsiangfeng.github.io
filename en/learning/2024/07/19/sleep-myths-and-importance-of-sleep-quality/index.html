Some Sleep Myths and the Importance of Sleep Quality

Intro

Many of my students and coworkers have had sleep issues, including people close to me. So I've done a fair amount of reading and trial. Since a lot of people keep asking lately, here's a quick share on what I learned.

Important disclaimer

Wait!! A heads-up first: I'm not from a medical background. I've personally hit related sleep issues, so I've researched and experimented around sleep. This post is for reference only. If you really have a sleep disorder, please go seek professional help. See a doctor. One more reminder — info online and what netizens say is reference only. If you have a serious sleep disorder, see a professional. Don't experiment on yourself or follow random advice — those people can't take responsibility for you, but a professional can give you the right advice based on your situation. That's the right way to actually solve the problem.

Sleep myths

I'll share my own takes on common sleep myths and why sleep quality matters. Hope it helps you understand yourself and improve your sleep.

Myth 1: You must sleep 7–8 hours

You've probably heard parents or teachers say:

"You have to sleep 7–8 hours or you'll get dumber!"

Let's break this one. "Sleep 7–8 hours" is not an absolute answer or range. This number varies by person — some need 9–10, some need 6–7, some more, some less. The 7–8 hour figure is a reference value.

For me, average sleep duration around 5–7 hours is plenty. Here's a sleep tracker readout I had (for reference only):

Sleep tracker

My typical pattern: in bed at 00:00 (sometimes earlier), scroll on the phone for 30 min – 1 hour, lights out for actual sleep around 01:00, alarm at 07:00. Average sleep is 5–7 hours.

I once experimented with sleeping a full 8 or even 8+ hours. Some side effects for me:

  • Severe headache: needed extra-strength Panadol to suppress
  • Fatigue: heavy lethargy as if I hadn't slept enough
  • Hard to tell reality from dream: vague dizziness all day, like I'm still in the dream

For me, my personal optimal sleep is 5–7 hours. Too much or too little doesn't work.

So the real takeaway: find your own sleep cycle. Everyone's needed cycle differs.

So everyone's sleep duration isn't the widely-heard "must sleep 7–8 hours" — that's just a stat / average across the world's population. Doesn't mean you have to. That's myth #1 to break.

Myth 2: Sleeping poorly = not enough sleep

Sleeping poorly isn't necessarily because of insufficient duration. It's bad sleep quality. Long-term bad sleep quality = sleep deprivation. Important concept.

What does long-term bad sleep quality lead to?

  • Memory decline: Sleep is when the brain organizes and remembers info from the day — what's important, what's not. Memory is finite. Without enough sleep, the brain can't sort properly, leading to "common memory decline" or even confusion.
  • Reduced focus: Without proper rest, you're in constant want-to-sleep mode. Focus drops, productivity drops, work mistakes go up.
  • Mood instability: Constant sleepiness makes mood worse — short temper, even depression.
  • Lowered immunity: Sleep deprivation lowers immunity. Body becomes more prone to colds, weaker.

We often hear:

"When you're sick, rest more, sleep more."

This is actually scientifically grounded — your body is fighting invading viruses, needs lots of energy. The brain is like a command center telling your body:

"White blood cells! Get to position X to fight the enemy!"

So adequate rest is needed to recover, boost immunity, restore health. During illness you feel abnormally tired because the brain is busy commanding the body to fight invaders.

So we can see: long-term bad sleep quality raises the risk of:

  • Obesity
  • Diabetes
  • Cardiovascular disease
  • Anxiety
  • Depression
  • Memory decline
  • Reduced focus

Etc.

So "sleeping poorly = not enough sleep" isn't accurate — it's bad sleep quality. That's myth #2.

Myth 3: Earlier sleep is always better

Also a major myth. Everyone's circadian rhythm differs — some are "night owls", some are "early birds". Person-by-person.

Think about it — while you're sleeping, people on the other side of the world are awake working. The real point is everyone's "circadian rhythm" differs, but we can adjust our lifestyle to fit our rhythm.

Example: many friends of mine are night owls. They're sluggish during the day, but around 10 PM their energy kicks in. The brain becomes super active and productivity peaks. That's their circadian rhythm.

But adjusting your circadian rhythm isn't easy. Anyone who's traveled abroad has had "jet lag" — your rhythm is misaligned with local time, your body protests. The body adjusts only slowly. Ways to help:

  • Try sleeping earlier to align with local time
  • Open the curtains so daylight helps adjust the rhythm
  • Avoid heavy activity during local nighttime

These are ways. Lifestyle hugely impacts circadian rhythm. That's myth #3.

Myth 4: Older people always wake up early

The most common myth. It's not necessarily "older = wakes up early". Sleep cycles shift with age / life stage, especially relative to brain activity.

Teenagers tend to sleep late and wake late — that's normal. Sleep needs change with age. Teen years are an active brain-development phase — the brain wants to absorb lots of info, then needs lots of energy and time at night to process. So teens need more sleep.

In middle age, sleep duration tends to decrease, because the brain has less info to organize (you've been through more life experience), needs less to absorb, less energy needed. Sleep decreases, waking doesn't feel tired. Normal.

So "older people always wake up early"? Not necessarily. Stats show this for most, but not absolute. That's why some countries let students sleep more — students need more energy to process and revise info, so the brain develops better.

Some older folks actually maintain late-sleep, late-wake schedules — partly tied to lifestyle. So "older = wakes early" isn't accurate. Myth #4.

Myth 5: Napping at noon just makes you more tired

Time to talk about "catch-up sleep" — it's necessary, but clarify why you need it. Catch-up sleep is meant to let your body recover, not to throw your daily schedule into chaos.

Needing catch-up sleep means your body has been in sleep deficit long-term. This is a "sleep debt" — same idea as us programmers / engineers. Long-term, if you don't maintain or refactor code, one day it blows up. That's "tech debt". Same with sleep.

Sleep debt means your body and brain haven't been getting enough, accumulating long-term. You start feeling work fatigue, getting irritable, skin worsening. That's your sleep debt hitting a threshold. You need to catch up.

But catch-up sleep isn't done casually. When you nap to repay sleep debt is the key, not just sleeping more at noon, in the morning, or on weekends.

Generally, the best window for catch-up sleep is 1–4 PM. But:

"Each nap shouldn't exceed 20–30 minutes"

Past that, you can enter "deep sleep" mode. That's why some people feel worse after a nap — the brain "thought it was entering deep sleep", so being woken up mid-cycle feels exhausting.

For me, naps are typically 15–20 minutes with an alarm to wake me — that way I don't enter deep sleep, and I wake refreshed. That's the right way to catch up, and a quick way to repay sleep debt.

I've experimented with napping 30+ minutes and having someone wake me mid-cycle. I'd feel like I hadn't slept at all, body felt hit by a truck, severe headache — because the brain was in deep sleep, actively organizing info, interrupted mid-process. That's why you feel terrible.

So catch-up sleep matters, but technique matters too. Not just "sleep whenever".

How do I fix sleeping poorly / insomnia?

A lot of people share this issue:

"Tossing and turning, can't fall asleep"

Many fixes to try, like:

Exercise

Exercise is great. When you exercise, the brain produces "dopamine" — a happiness chemical. Moderate exercise helps your daily productivity and sleep. But watch timing — especially "30 minutes – 1 hour before bed" — I really don't recommend exercising then, because it activates your brain and makes falling asleep harder.

Distance from screens

Not because of phone blue light per se — it's the light itself that the brain misreads as daylight, activating you and making sleep harder. So distance yourself from screens 30 minutes – 1 hour before bed, letting your brain relax.

Blue light is a factor, but the real problem is light.

Avoid caffeine

Caffeine is a great stimulant, but at night it activates your brain and makes sleep harder. Avoid caffeine in the afternoon and evening.

Also avoid eating in the "1 hour before bed". The brain has to direct digestion, which activates it and makes sleep harder.

Find a mattress and pillow that fit

Mattress and pillow are big factors. You've probably tried a beanbag — body feels super relaxed, then you slowly enter "lose-all-will-to-move" mode. That's because the body feels "comfortable", letting the brain relax.

If your mattress / pillow is too firm or too soft, your body feels off and the brain doesn't relax. Find one that fits — some offer trial periods. Remember: "your mattress / pillow determines the pressure your body feels", which determines how fast you fall asleep.

Right sleep temperature

Room temperature too high or too low affects sleep onset. AC during sleep helps cool the brain into a sleep state — but don't overcool. For humans, generally 24–26°C is comfortable, person-by-person.

A hot bath before bed can also help — gently relax and cool the day's fatigue. Another way to fall asleep faster.

Supplements

Supplements can help relaxation / falling asleep. The most common one: melatonin — actually a "sleep hormone" that helps your brain's pineal gland secrete melatonin, making sleep easier.

But not everyone responds to these supplements — person-by-person. Time and dosage should match your body. Supplements aren't automatically "good" — they're supplements, supportive. Overdose hurts you.

So for melatonin timing, ask a specialist sleep doctor — that's how to get optimal benefit.

Address your emotions

Sleep disorders (insomnia) are strongly tied to emotion. I've personally had insomnia — it's not just "thinking too much before bed". I had nearly two weeks of insomnia: lying in bed, fully unable to sleep, hard and painful. The cause was emotional pressure from something, even though I "wasn't actively thinking" about it, I still couldn't sleep.

When sleep-deprived, emotional regulation is harder, which feeds back into insomnia. So emotional state is one of the causes of insomnia — that's why some people recommend counseling. That's a real way to address insomnia.

So address and face your stress / anxiety sources. Once emotions release, sleep quality can truly relax.

Build a sleep ritual

Rituals matter. Like we have "work rituals", you can build a sleep ritual. For me, I read manga before bed, then 30 minutes before sleep, distance from the phone, then lights out. That's my ritual — it feels super relaxing and helps me fall asleep.

Everyone's ritual differs. Try cultivating one.

Seek professional help

If you've tried everything above and it doesn't fix things — and you wake up in the middle of the night easily — I'd recommend going to a professional. It means you have a "sleep disorder" that isn't going to solve itself. What you need is professional help — a doctor.

Having illness awareness is important. Many people are very averse to "seeing a doctor", but accumulated sleep debt is scary. It will cause systemic issues — for men: abnormal liver function, cardiovascular disease, even sexual dysfunction. For women: menstrual irregularities, uterine issues. All possibly caused by long-term bad sleep quality.

How do I know if I'm sleeping well or not?

Simple answer: how you feel the morning after. If you feel pain, fatigue, etc., it's a signal — pay attention to your sleep quality. Your "sleep debt" has accumulated. The methods above can help and break some myths.

Sleep is actually a deep topic — biorhythms, sleep quality, brain secretions, bedding choice, etc. — all matter. Know how to maintain your sleep quality.

Even tossing and turning before bed could indicate a "sleep disorder". So I recommend, if you've tried the above and still can't solve it, seek professional help.

Typically the professional doctor will ask:

  1. How long is your sleep?
  2. What's your sleep quality?
  3. What's your daily schedule?
  4. What's your emotional state?
  5. What's your diet?
  6. What kind of work do you do? ...

All of these affect sleep quality, same as what I covered in this article. Hope my long-time personal experience helps. That's why I wrote it.

Conclusion

Last reminder: I wrote this because coworkers and students have related issues, and I and people close to me have had sleep disorders too. That's why I researched and tried to understand sleep importance. I hope this article helps you understand the importance of sleep quality and breaks some myths, plus shares some techniques. But again — this is purely my personal experience-sharing, not a medical article.

What you really need is professional help. That's how you actually solve your sleep issues. That's my final advice. Hope this article helps everyone get good dreams.
