2024 Node.js Enterprise Capstone Program

Intro

I was already a capstone coach last year, but I'm honestly a bit surprised to have the "honor" of being part of the whole program again this year. So to debrief myself, I'm writing this post to record the process and my takeaways. Hopefully it helps future students understand what this program is about. Also, this is for my students.

The "kickoff" story

Warning: Before you read further, I want you to know — this post doesn't represent "HexSchool"'s position. This is my personal position and personal sharing.

One day around November–December 2023, a colleague named Jay said:

Jay: "Hey, Ray, I'm going to need a lot of time on course planning and external partnerships from now through next year, so I'll be pretty busy. There's something I need to ask you for help with — are you super busy right now?" Me: "Lately... not really. If you need help, I can pitch in." Jay: "Great, then planning and running the 2024 Node.js Enterprise Capstone Program is on you! From today, you're the head capstone coach!" Me: "???"

Yes, you read that right. I once again got dunked into a hole by my coworker and boss. Lesson learned: don't open your mouth and say "sure" right off the bat (lol).

OK, honestly, after 2023 Node.js Software Engineer Enterprise Capstone Program (article in Mandarin) wrapped, I'd already been told I'd be the head capstone coach this year. I just didn't expect to also get planning and operations dumped on me too ;!!!∑(ﾟДﾟノ)ノ

But it also means the boss trusts me, so I had to accept the assignment, QQ...

So let's talk about how this year's program differs from last year's.

About this program

I mentioned in "About How I Became the 2023 Node.js Software Engineer Enterprise Capstone Program Coach" (article in Mandarin) that this program is an advanced track. So it's significantly harder than a regular live-streamed class or capstone class.

You'll still experience:

  • Building one project over half a year (actually only about four months this year!)
  • Planning and designing a project from scratch, running through a real product dev flow
  • Picking up new tech in a short time
  • Fully remote collaboration

But every year's program takes feedback from previous students to improve. Some things that are different this year:

  • Milestones broken into finer steps
  • More reference templates provided
  • More opportunities for partner reviews and feedback
  • Stronger emphasis on team collaboration and communication
  • Program split into two classes (Intro Class, Enterprise Capstone Class)

Considering this is an enterprise capstone program, its difficulty is not comparable to a regular capstone class. If you're a junior beginner, you'd get transferred to a different class (e.g., the Intro Class), because this program is oriented toward real industry development.

Who am I

Before going further, quick intro about me so people aren't lost, QQ...

Hi, I'm Ray

This is my second time running the enterprise capstone program. In 2023 Node.js Enterprise Capstone I was a capstone coach; this year I'm the head capstone coach. The role covers planning, operations, coordination, communication, etc. Basically the commander-in-chief. The capstone coach role is filled by alumni HexSchool personally invited and vetted. Capstone coaches work in pairs, each pair handling 2–3 groups (each group is 5–6 people). This year we have 14 capstone coaches taking commands from me (huh?), and I focus on observing student situations and stepping in when needed. So I'm managing both students and coaches.

Last year teacher Liao, teacher Casper, and I co-led 25 groups of students — each of us covered 7–9 groups (each group 5–6 people, total ~130 students). This year we split into two classes: "Intro Class" and "Enterprise Capstone Class". You might ask:

"What's the difference between the two classes, and why split?"

The short answer:

Intro class is learning-focused. Capstone class is project output-focused.

Last year we noticed many students were still in learning-heavy mode, but capstone class is output-focused. The two directions were fighting each other. To give learning-focused students a more focused environment, we split into two classes — so learning-focused students can stay focused on learning instead of doubling up on capstone work + learning at the same time.

Who should join this program?

What kind of student fits this program?

I think you need to hit at least these four:

  • Some real work experience (at least 6 months)
  • Self-learning ability (can solve problems yourself)
  • Can commit 20+ hours per week to learning new tech + dev (2–3 hours per weekday after work, 8 hours per weekend day)
  • Willing to take on the challenge and collaborate / communicate with the team (you'll communicate with team members a LOT during the program)

These four are honestly not easy. Think about it: weekday work is 8 hours, then 2–3 hours of new tech + dev after work, plus team comms and progress reports — that's 16+ hours per day focused on this. That's super demanding for a working adult, especially over four months.

So you really need all four. If you join without thinking it through, you'll regret it deeply.

What changed this year

Now let's talk about the changes!

But first I want to thank my company colleagues for their help — without them, the docs, templates, and processes wouldn't have rolled out as smoothly. Thanks everyone!

Milestones broken into finer steps

Last year's milestones were relatively coarse — seven big ones:

  • Milestone 1: Topic confirmation
  • Milestone 2: Project finalization
  • Milestone 3: Tech upskill
  • Milestone 4: API spec
  • Milestone 5: Environment setup
  • Milestone 6: Role-side implementation
  • Milestone 7: Presentation phase

This year I broke them down into nine:

  • Milestone 0: Team formation
  • Milestone 1: Capstone coach scope, doc signing, related notices
  • Milestone 2: Topic confirmation, user stories, sitemap, tech discussion
  • Milestone 3: Flowcharts, swimlane diagrams, wireframes
  • Milestone 4: Design mockups (request designer service)
  • Milestone 5: API spec
  • Milestone 6: Environment setup
  • Milestone 7: Role-side implementation (actual dev)
  • Milestone 8: Capstone slide deck creation
  • Milestone 9: Capstone presentation (every group must attend)

You can see the split is much finer, and we pushed a lot of stuff to be settled earlier. In the 2023 Node.js capstone program, just milestones 1, 2, 3 took students nearly two months. This year we pushed everything we could earlier and provided lots of templates, e.g.:

  • User story template
  • Wireframe template
  • Flowchart, swimlane, sitemap templates
  • API spec template
  • Key reminders for each milestone ...etc.

Goal: reduce student time spent and mistakes made. Plus, each milestone has to be approved by the capstone coach before moving forward.

One thing I didn't mention: the enterprise capstone has 2 days of in-person offline workshops, one in the north (3/16), one in central (3/17). After students enroll, we send a survey covering work experience, personality, what they want to learn, MBTI, region, etc. — for grouping. On 3/08 I announce the groups, and teams form. Milestone 1 kicks off — everyone must read "Milestone 1: Capstone Coach Service Scope, Doc Signing, Notices" and sign the doc.

Milestone 1: Capstone coach service scope, doc signing, notices

This helps students clarify the coach's service scope and avoids treating coaches as personal TAs or asking them to do things outside scope.

Icebreaker tasks

There's an 8-day gap between 3/08 and 3/16–3/17, so we set up "icebreaker tasks" for that window:

  • Set up the group's own Discord server
  • Initial brainstorm + discussion of project topic, write the v0 user story
  • Group discussion + use AI to make the group's representative image (e.g., group logo)

With those tasks done, the offline workshop can push milestone 2 + topic confirmation way faster, getting groups into the build phase sooner.

A big difference from last year: we provided the "user story template" upfront, so when writing user stories and brainstorming topics, students don't diverge wildly or get too stuck. Not every engineer has been through a full dev flow:

User story template

We also tagged each topic and user story with a level rating + expected tech, giving students a reference baseline:

User story template

These templates and docs significantly improved last year's pain point: "students spent way too much time on user stories, topic discussion, and tech discussion".

Note Why ban POS systems? Because of demo and reference-source considerations, we restricted POS-system topics.

Two-day offline workshop

The 2-day offline workshop is split between northern and central Taiwan venues. Students attend based on their group's region — less travel friction, and the assigned capstone coach attends too.

The workshop covers:

  • Make clear that the program is project-focused, not a real production system. Think + build with MVP in mind.
  • Discuss and confirm topic, user stories, and tech.
  • v0 wireframe assignments + drawing (we only recommend Miro. We tested 2–3 times — students who use Figma burn a LOT of time learning Figma!!!!)

Basically "Milestone 2: Topic confirmation, user stories, sitemap, tech discussion" wraps in those 2 days, so students go home and dive straight into Milestone 3 ("Flowcharts, swimlane diagrams, wireframes"). Compared to last year, this is 100%+ faster — last year topic confirmation + user stories + sitemap + tech discussion alone took two months. This year we did Milestone 2 in two days, and some groups started Milestone 3 prep on the same day.

Mandatory group meetings

Last year's lesson: groups must meet weekly internally. One group last year refused to ever meet — by the end, everything was chaos and they parted on bad terms.

So this year we mandate weekly internal group meetings to share progress, discuss problems, solve them. This both speeds up the project and strengthens team bonds — that's what lets the project finish.

You'd think that was enough? Nope. To ensure each group's output, students must also do a weekly online progress report with their capstone coach. And before the online report, they write a weekly report form for the coach to review:

Weekly report

These questions are carefully designed — coaches can observe student state and project progress through the answers. So they're rigorous, and we strictly ban running-log style reports. Otherwise, what's the point? If we catch running-log style, we tell you to rewrite and remind you not to.

Through the program I also wrote a bunch of docs based on observed student situations, e.g.:

How to become a meeting expert: Anyone can become a meeting expert

How to collaborate and communicate with the team: Team collaboration & communication handbook

"Milestone owner" responsibility handbook: What does a milestone owner actually do?

You'd think that was enough? Of course not — also "How to Communicate With Your Capstone Coach": Communicating with capstone coaches is super easy!

So during the program, I'm not just sitting around — I'm constantly observing groups and writing docs based on the situation, so each group's pain points get resolved fast. Before I wrote those docs, some groups had 3–4 hour internal meetings. After coaches relayed my docs, internal meetings dropped to under an hour — some even 5 minutes.

API doc redesign

This year's Milestone 5 was the one I spent the most time planning, because I kept asking:

"How do I let a frontend dev with no backend experience write a good API doc, AND let coaches review it AND track dev progress?"

I know in industry people use Swagger, Postman, etc. for internal team comms, but for the coaches, those tools don't quite work for review. So I spent two weeks before landing on this version:

API spec doc

During this stretch, some students would enter "idle" state, so coaches let them start Milestone 6 — get environments ready — so overall pace stayed up.

Milestones 6 and 7

Milestones 6 and 7 are simpler. Milestone 6 is environment setup, but still has basic review criteria. We need to verify frontend ↔ backend comms work, so at minimum login, signup, and edit-profile must work. That tells coaches the front/back comms are good and environments are ready.

Milestone 7 is role-side implementation — basically the build phase. Honestly, by milestones 5–6, prep work for the build is already happening — API spec, backend API definitions, etc. That sets up Milestone 7 for smooth sailing.

Milestone 7 will of course have a lot of friction — backend can't ship APIs in time but frontend has finished the design slice. What now? If backend wrote good docs in Milestone 5, frontend can build against a Mock API based on the docs, then swap in the real API once backend ships. So frontend doesn't stall on backend.

Overall, we tried hard to design the plan so nobody is waiting on anyone.

Partner reviews and exchange

Partner reviews and exchange are the core of this program — and what last year's alumni wished for. Due to time last year, we couldn't fit partner reviews. So this year I baked them in. I deliberated a lot on this, because partner reviews are brutal — every partner knows the students have work experience, which made me ask:

"Partners don't soften feedback like coaches — they give brutal feedback from a business and market-value angle. Is this too harsh for students?"

But as educators, isn't the goal student growth? So I scheduled 5 minutes of partner review after each group's pitch, so students learn what's still missing or growable in their work. That's truly helpful and aligns with the program's core philosophy. I know partners will pose hard questions to trip students up, but really they're testing real-time response, not actually trying to take you down — they want to see how you handle it.

Student presentations and partner reviews

Student presentations and partner reviews

I hope the partner review actually helps students grow — not be a "pat-on-the-head" ritual that says "you all did great" and then their project doesn't land them any interviews. That's neither my planning intent nor my intent as an educator.

New capstone coaches

I mentioned my role shifted from "capstone coach" to "head capstone coach". Coaches are now alumni HexSchool personally invited and vetted, so every one is trusted. Before the program started, we did dedicated training and a program kickoff meeting for the coaches, so they understood their role and responsibilities. That keeps the coaching team coherent.

Beyond weekly online meetings with their students, coaches also do weekly online meetings with me (every Friday) where they report each group's status and milestone progress. That lets me track every group's state and step in when appropriate.

Weekly progress report

Because I've been a capstone coach before, I know how hard the role is. So my coaching team runs on open / transparent / we're-all-one-team principles, not manager/subordinate vibes. Coaches already spend a lot of time understanding students, giving timely milestone-push advice, and sharing dev experience — plus 2–3 hours of weekly reporting with me. If I treated this like manager/subordinate, the team would burn out fast.

So in coach meetings, I also ask everyone how life's going lately, anything to share. I share my own life and experiences too, and we joke around together, keeping meetings closer to a chat than a meeting.

For me, the coaches are like family — I pay close attention to each of their life situations and give targeted feedback.

What does the head coach actually do?

You might wonder — as head capstone coach, what am I actually doing?

I'm watching every group all the time. In the Friday meetings, I log each group's status and progress. If a student situation gets unusual, I escalate to the two bosses upstairs, so they have heads-up. When something blows up, they can step in or advise (though by then it's usually already bad).

So after each Friday coaches meeting (around 11pm–12am), I start writing up the weekly progress report and send it to the two bosses. Total wrap-up time: usually around 1–2am.

The enterprise capstone team structure is roughly...

Two bosses (Liao, Casper) → Head capstone coach (me) → Capstone coaches (2 per pair, 7 pairs total) → Students (5–6 per group, 16 groups total)

Then I write a weekly report based on observed student + coach status, and sync it to all coaches.

Capstone weekly report

Benefit: every coach has synced info, plus what to focus on this week milestone-wise and some dark-tactic tips.

Honestly, my role is more of a middleman. On one side I'm optimizing the curriculum and resources based on student situations; on the other, I'm giving coaches the backing they need to face students confidently. So coaches and students are learning "managing up", I'm learning "managing down", and we're all in mutual-learning mode. Everyone in this program is in the middle of learning — and burning their liver to the point of breaking, QQ...

Closing thoughts

Got a bit long. Let me end with this program's core values.

The enterprise capstone's core values are "bridging into industry" and "raising self-value". That's why we put so much effort into pushing students to write docs, communicate, hold meetings, set up environments, write API specs — all of it. These simulate the flow and process of building a product at a company, which most engineers don't get to experience. We want students to see that flow up close, so they can transition into industry without getting phased out. Otherwise you're just a "code grinder" who's slightly better at writing code.

Honestly the past few months have been brutal, but every student and coach who made it through has grown, and they understand how hard it is to build a product and manage people. That's the program's goal and mission — every student becomes an irreplaceable strong engineer, shining in their company
